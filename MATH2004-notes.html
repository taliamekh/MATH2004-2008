Chapter 1

Vectors in the Plane

Vectors, scalar multiplication, dot product, cross product, projections, and their geometry in ℝ² and ℝ³.

Vectors are one of the most important tools in science and engineering. They originated as a way to describe physical quantities that have both a size and a direction — things like velocity, force, and acceleration. Unlike ordinary numbers (scalars), which only tell you "how much," a vector also tells you "which way." This chapter builds the algebra and geometry of vectors from scratch, starting in two dimensions and extending to three.

1.1 — Vectors in the Plane

We begin in the Cartesian plane \(\mathbb{R}^2\). A plane is a flat, two-dimensional surface extending infinitely in both length and width. We set up the usual rectangular coordinate system with perpendicular x- and y-axes meeting at the origin \(O\). Every point \(P\) in the plane is identified by an ordered pair \((a,b)\) of real numbers.

Vector (Geometric)

A vector is a directed line segment — an arrow with a definite length (also called magnitude) and a definite direction. Every vector has an initial point (tail) where the arrow begins and a terminal point (tip) where it ends. Vectors are denoted by boldface letters \(\mathbf{v}\) or with an arrow: \(\vec{v}\), \(\vec{u}\), \(\vec{w}\).

The critical idea is that a vector is determined entirely by its length and direction — not by where it sits in the plane. If you slide an arrow to a new position without rotating or stretching it, you still have the same vector. This leads to the formal notion of equality.

Vector Equality

Two vectors are equal if and only if they have the same length and the same direction. Their position in the plane is irrelevant — you can translate a vector anywhere without changing it.

All three arrows represent the SAME vector — same length and direction, different positions.

Because position doesn't matter, we adopt a standard convention: among all the arrows representing a given vector, we pick the one whose initial point is the origin \(O=(0,0)\). Its terminal point then lands at some point \(P(a,b)\), and we identify the vector with that point. This is why we can write \(\vec{v} = (a,b)\).

Opposite and Zero Vectors

The opposite (or negative) of \(\vec{v}\) is \(-\vec{v}\): a vector with the same length but the opposite direction. The zero vector \(\vec{0} = (0,0)\) has zero length and no defined direction. It is the only vector with magnitude zero.

1.2 — Scalar Multiplication

A scalar is simply a real number — the word emphasizes that it has magnitude only, no direction. We often need to scale a vector (stretch it, shrink it, or flip it), which is done by multiplying it by a scalar.

Scalar Multiplication

For a vector \(\vec{v}\) and a scalar \(a \in \mathbb{R}\), the product \(a\vec{v}\) is a new vector whose length is \(|a|\,|\vec{v}|\) (the absolute value of \(a\) times the original length). If \(a > 0\), it points in the same direction as \(\vec{v}\). If \(a < 0\), it points in the opposite direction. If \(a = 0\), the result is the zero vector. When \(a < 0\), we say \(a\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are antiparallel.

Magnitude of a Scalar Multiple
\[|a\vec{v}| = |a|\,|\vec{v}|\]
a — scalar multiplier|a| — absolute value of the scalar|v| — magnitude of the original vector
★ Tip

Positive scalar = stretch/shrink (same direction). Negative scalar = flip + scale (opposite direction). Zero scalar = zero vector. Scalar multiplication in components: \(a(v_1, v_2) = (av_1, av_2)\).

1.3 — Vectors and Points

The geometric picture of vectors as arrows is intuitive, but for computation we need an algebraic framework. The key idea is to identify each vector with a point in the coordinate system. For any vector, we choose the representative arrow starting at the origin. Its tip lands at a unique point, and we use that point's coordinates as the vector's components.

If \(P(x_0, y_0)\) and \(Q(x_1, y_1)\) are two points, the vector from \(P\) to \(Q\) is found by subtracting coordinates: the arrow \(\vec{PQ}\) has the same length and direction as the arrow from the origin to the point \((x_1 - x_0,\; y_1 - y_0)\).

Vector Between Two Points
\[\vec{PQ} = (x_1 - x_0,\; y_1 - y_0)\]
P = (x₀, y₀) — initial point (tail)Q = (x₁, y₁) — terminal point (tip)PQ — vector from P to Q (terminal minus initial)

The distance between two points is the length of the vector joining them. This follows directly from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the horizontal and vertical legs of the right triangle formed by the two points.

Distance Formula
\[d(P,Q) = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_0)^2 + (y_1 - y_0)^2}\]
d(P,Q) — distance between P and Q(x₁−x₀) — horizontal displacement(y₁−y₀) — vertical displacement
Magnitude (Length) of a Vector
\[|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}\]
|v| — magnitude (always ≥ 0)v₁ — x-componentv₂ — y-component|v| = 0 only when v = 0
★ Key Fact

From now on, points and vectors are interchangeable. The vector \(\vec{v} = (a,b)\) IS the point \(P(a,b)\), and vice versa. We use whichever language is more convenient.

1.4 — Sums and Differences

Now that vectors are identified with coordinate pairs, we can define arithmetic operations. The sum and difference of vectors are defined component-by-component, which turns out to match the geometric "parallelogram law" perfectly.

Vector Addition / Subtraction
\[(a,b) \pm (c,d) = (a \pm c,\; b \pm d)\]
(a,b) — first vector(c,d) — second vectoradd/subtract component-by-component

Geometrically, vector addition follows the Parallelogram Law: place both vectors at the same starting point, complete the parallelogram, and the diagonal from the starting point is the sum. Equivalently, you can use the "tip-to-tail" method — place the tail of the second vector at the tip of the first, and the sum goes from the start of the first to the end of the second.

Parallelogram Law: the sum v + w is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by v and w.

Scalar multiplication in component form works naturally: \(\alpha(a,b) = (\alpha a, \alpha b)\). This is consistent with the geometric definition — the resulting vector has length \(|\alpha|\) times the original and points in the same (or opposite) direction.

Unit Vectors

A unit vector is any vector with magnitude exactly 1. Unit vectors are important because they isolate direction — they tell you "which way" without any information about "how far." Any nonzero vector can be turned into a unit vector by dividing by its length.

Unit Vector (Normalization)
\[\hat{u} = \frac{\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\]
û — unit vector (length = 1)v — the original nonzero vector|v| — its magnitude (must ≠ 0)

Why does this work? Because \(|\hat{u}| = \left|\frac{\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\right| = \frac{|\vec{v}|}{|\vec{v}|} = 1\). The division scales the vector down (or up) until its length is exactly 1, while preserving its direction.

Standard Basis Vectors in ℝ²

The two most fundamental unit vectors in the plane are \(\mathbf{i} = (1,0)\) and \(\mathbf{j} = (0,1)\). They point along the positive x- and y-axes respectively. Every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) can be written uniquely as a combination of these two: \(\vec{v} = (a,b) = a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}\). This is called the decomposition of \(\vec{v}\) into its basis components.

PropertyStatement
Commutative\(\vec{v}+\vec{w}=\vec{w}+\vec{v}\)
Associative\((\vec{u}+\vec{v})+\vec{w}=\vec{u}+(\vec{v}+\vec{w})\)
Identity\(\vec{v}+\vec{0}=\vec{v}\)
Inverse\(\vec{v}+(-\vec{v})=\vec{0}\)
Distribution (scalar)\(a(\vec{v}+\vec{w})=a\vec{v}+a\vec{w}\)
Distribution (sum of scalars)\((a+b)\vec{v}=a\vec{v}+b\vec{v}\)

1.5 — The Inclination of a Vector

Every unit vector in the plane can be written using trigonometry. If \(\vec{u}\) is a unit vector making angle \(\theta\) with the positive x-axis, then \(\vec{u} = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\). More generally, any nonzero vector \(\vec{v} = (a,b)\) makes some angle \(\theta\) with the x-axis, and we can write \(a = |\vec{v}|\cos\theta\) and \(b = |\vec{v}|\sin\theta\). This angle \(\theta\) is called the inclination of the vector.

Inclination Angle
\[\tan\theta = \frac{b}{a}\quad(a\neq 0),\qquad \vec{v} = |\vec{v}|(\cos\theta,\;\sin\theta)\]
θ — angle with positive x-axis (0 ≤ θ < 2π)a — horizontal componentb — vertical component|v| — magnitude

Finding \(\theta\) from \(\tan\theta = b/a\) requires care because the arctangent function only returns values in \((-\pi/2,\;\pi/2)\), which covers Quadrants I and IV. For vectors in Quadrants II and III (where \(a < 0\)), you must add \(\pi\) to the arctangent result. If \(a = 0\), the vector points straight up (\(\theta = \pi/2\)) or straight down (\(\theta = 3\pi/2\)) depending on the sign of \(b\).

★ Exam Warning — Quadrant Adjustment

The formula \(\theta = \arctan(b/a)\) only works directly in QI and QIV. For QII/QIII (where \(a < 0\)): add \(\pi\). Specifically: if \(a \geq 0, b \geq 0\) (QI): \(\theta = \arctan(b/a)\). If \(a < 0\) (QII or QIII): \(\theta = \arctan(b/a) + \pi\). If \(a > 0, b < 0\) (QIV): \(\theta = \arctan(b/a) + 2\pi\). Always sketch the vector to verify your answer is in the right quadrant.

Show Solution

2

1.6 — Vectors in ℝ³

Everything we've done in two dimensions extends naturally to three. A point in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) has three coordinates \((x,y,z)\), and vectors now have three components. The three-dimensional space is divided into eight octants (analogous to quadrants in 2D) by the three coordinate planes (\(xy\)-plane where \(z=0\), \(xz\)-plane where \(y=0\), \(yz\)-plane where \(x=0\)).

Vectors in ℝ³

A vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is an ordered triple \(\vec{v} = (v_1, v_2, v_3)\). All operations — addition, scalar multiplication, magnitude — work exactly as in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) but with an extra component. The vector from \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) to \(Q(x_1,y_1,z_1)\) is \(\vec{PQ} = (x_1-x_0,\;y_1-y_0,\;z_1-z_0)\).

Magnitude in ℝ³
\[|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2}\]
v₁ — x-componentv₂ — y-componentv₃ — z-component|v| — 3D Pythagorean theorem
Standard Basis in ℝ³

\(\mathbf{i}=(1,0,0)\), \(\mathbf{j}=(0,1,0)\), \(\mathbf{k}=(0,0,1)\). These are mutually perpendicular unit vectors along the three axes. Any vector decomposes as \(\vec{v} = v_1\mathbf{i} + v_2\mathbf{j} + v_3\mathbf{k}\).

A 3D coordinate system with position vector OP = (x, y, z).

1.7 — The Dot Product

There are two fundamentally different ways to "multiply" vectors. The first — the dot product (also called scalar product or inner product) — takes two vectors and produces a scalar (a number). The second — the cross product (Section 1.9) — produces a vector. Remembering which is which is critical.

Dot Product — Algebraic Definition
\[\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w} = v_1w_1 + v_2w_2 + v_3w_3\]
v = (v₁, v₂, v₃) — first vectorw = (w₁, w₂, w₃) — second vectorresult is a scalar (a number, not a vector)

The algebraic definition is simple: multiply matching components and add. But the dot product has a deep geometric meaning: it measures how much two vectors "align" with each other.

Dot Product — Geometric Form
\[\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w} = |\vec{v}|\,|\vec{w}|\cos\theta\]
|v| — magnitude of v|w| — magnitude of wθ — angle between v and w (0 ≤ θ ≤ π)
Proof Sketch — Why Both Forms Are Equal

Apply the law of cosines to the triangle with sides \(\vec{v}\), \(\vec{w}\), and \(\vec{v}-\vec{w}\). The law gives \(|\vec{v}-\vec{w}|^2 = |\vec{v}|^2 + |\vec{w}|^2 - 2|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\cos\theta\). Expanding \(|\vec{v}-\vec{w}|^2\) in components and simplifying yields \(v_1w_1 + v_2w_2 + v_3w_3 = |\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\cos\theta\), connecting the algebraic and geometric forms.

Rearranging the geometric form gives us a formula for the angle between any two nonzero vectors:

Angle Between Two Vectors
\[\cos\theta = \frac{\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}}{|\vec{v}|\,|\vec{w}|}\]
v · w — compute the dot product|v|, |w| — compute both magnitudesθ = arccos(result) — always in [0, π]

The sign of the dot product encodes the geometric relationship between two vectors: positive means the angle is acute (vectors point roughly the same way), zero means perpendicular (90°), and negative means obtuse (vectors point roughly opposite).

Orthogonality (Perpendicularity)

Two vectors \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) are orthogonal (perpendicular), written \(\vec{v} \perp \vec{w}\), if and only if \(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w} = 0\). This is because \(\cos 90° = 0\).

PropertyStatement
Commutative\(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}=\vec{w}\cdot\vec{v}\)
Distributive\(\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}+\vec{w})=\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}+\vec{u}\cdot\vec{w}\)
Scalar factors out\(\alpha(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w})=(\alpha\vec{v})\cdot\vec{w}=\vec{v}\cdot(\alpha\vec{w})\)
Self-dot\(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{v}=|\vec{v}|^2\)
Zero dot\(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{0}=0\)
Orthogonality\(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}=0 \iff \vec{v}\perp\vec{w}\)
★ Key Insight — Dot Product = Alignment

Positive → angle < 90° (same general direction). Zero → angle = 90° (perpendicular). Negative → angle > 90° (opposing directions). Equal to |v||w| → parallel (same direction). Equal to −|v||w| → antiparallel (opposite direction).

1.8 — Direction Cosines and Projections

In two dimensions, a single angle \(\theta\) determines a vector's direction. In three dimensions, we need three angles — one with each coordinate axis. The cosines of these angles are called direction cosines.

Direction Cosines
\[\cos\alpha=\frac{a}{|\vec{v}|},\quad\cos\beta=\frac{b}{|\vec{v}|},\quad\cos\gamma=\frac{c}{|\vec{v}|}\]
\[\cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1\]
α — angle with x-axisβ — angle with y-axisγ — angle with z-axisa, b, c — components of v|v| — magnitude

Each direction cosine is just the dot product of the unit vector \(\hat{v}\) with the corresponding basis vector. The identity \(\cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1\) follows because the unit vector has magnitude 1. Any vector can be reconstructed from its direction cosines: \(\vec{v} = |\vec{v}|(\cos\alpha,\cos\beta,\cos\gamma)\).

Projections

One of the most important applications of the dot product is projection: decomposing one vector into a component parallel to another and a component perpendicular to it. Think of it as finding the "shadow" of one vector onto another.

Scalar Projection (Component)
\[\text{comp}_{\vec{v}}\,\vec{u} = \frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\]
comp_v u — signed length of shadow of u onto vu · v — dot product|v| — magnitude of v (the direction you project onto)positive if same direction, negative if opposite
Vector Projection
\[\text{proj}_{\vec{v}}\,\vec{u} = \frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|^2}\;\vec{v}\]
proj_v u — the vector component of u parallel to vu · v / |v|² — the scalar multiplierv — the direction being projected ontoNote: |v|² = v · v

The vector projection gives the part of \(\vec{u}\) that lies along \(\vec{v}\). The remaining part, \(\vec{u} - \text{proj}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}\), is perpendicular to \(\vec{v}\). A key physical application is work: when a force \(\vec{F}\) moves an object through displacement \(\vec{d}\), only the component of force along the displacement does work.

Work Done by a Force
\[W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = |\vec{F}|\,|\vec{d}|\cos\theta\]
W — work (scalar)F — force vectord — displacement vectorθ — angle between F and d
★ Exam Tip

Reconstruct any vector from direction cosines: \(\vec{v} = r(\cos\alpha,\cos\beta,\cos\gamma)\) where \(r = |\vec{v}|\). For projections, remember the denominator: scalar projection uses \(|\vec{v}|\), vector projection uses \(|\vec{v}|^2\) (equivalently \(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{v}\)).

Example — Vector Projection

Find the projection of \(\vec{u}=(3,4)\) onto \(\vec{v}=(1,0)\).

Show Solution

Thought process: Projecting onto \((1,0)\) means finding the x-component of \(\vec{u}\).

1
\(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v} = (3)(1)+(4)(0) = 3\)
2
\(|\vec{v}|^2 = 1\)
✓
Answer: \(\text{proj}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u} = \frac{3}{1}(1,0) = (3,0)\). The component of \(\vec{u}\) along the x-axis is 3, as expected.

1.9 — The Cross Product

The cross product answers a fundamental question: given two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), find a vector perpendicular to both of them. Unlike the dot product (which gives a scalar), the cross product gives a vector. It is defined only in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

The cross product is derived by requiring \(\vec{n}\cdot\vec{v}=0\) and \(\vec{n}\cdot\vec{w}=0\) simultaneously, then solving the resulting system of equations. The result can be computed using a 3×3 determinant.

Cross Product — Component Form
\[\vec{v}\times\vec{w} = (v_2w_3 - v_3w_2,\; v_3w_1 - v_1w_3,\; v_1w_2 - v_2w_1)\]
v = (v₁, v₂, v₃) — first vectorw = (w₁, w₂, w₃) — second vectorresult is a vector ⊥ to both v and w
Cross Product — Determinant Form
\[\vec{v}\times\vec{w}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\v_1&v_2&v_3\\w_1&w_2&w_3\end{vmatrix}\]
i, j, k — standard basis vectorsexpand along the first rowi-component: v₂w₃ − v₃w₂j-component: −(v₁w₃ − v₃w₁)k-component: v₁w₂ − v₂w₁

The direction of the cross product follows the right-hand rule: point your fingers along \(\vec{v}\), curl them toward \(\vec{w}\), and your thumb points in the direction of \(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}\). Quick reference: \(\mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{j}=\mathbf{k}\), \(\mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{k}=\mathbf{i}\), \(\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{i}=\mathbf{j}\).

Cross Product — Magnitude
\[|\vec{v}\times\vec{w}| = |\vec{v}|\,|\vec{w}|\sin\theta\]
|v|, |w| — magnitudesθ — angle between v and w|v × w| = area of parallelogram spanned by v and w
PropertyStatement
Anticommutative\(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}=-(\vec{w}\times\vec{v})\) — order matters!
Distributive\(\vec{u}\times(\vec{v}+\vec{w})=\vec{u}\times\vec{v}+\vec{u}\times\vec{w}\)
Scalar factors out\(c(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})=(c\vec{v})\times\vec{w}=\vec{v}\times(c\vec{w})\)
Self-cross\(\vec{v}\times\vec{v}=\vec{0}\)
Zero cross\(\vec{v}\times\vec{0}=\vec{0}\)
Parallel test\(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}=\vec{0}\iff\vec{v}\parallel\vec{w}\)
NOT associative\(\vec{u}\times(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}) \neq (\vec{u}\times\vec{v})\times\vec{w}\) in general
★ Right-Hand Rule Memory Aid

Fingers along \(\vec{v}\), curl toward \(\vec{w}\) → thumb = \(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}\). Cyclic: \(\mathbf{i}\to\mathbf{j}\to\mathbf{k}\to\mathbf{i}\) (forward = positive). Reversing any pair flips the sign.

1.10 — Geometry of Cross Products

The cross product has beautiful geometric applications. Since \(|\vec{v}\times\vec{w}| = |\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\sin\theta\), and the area of a parallelogram with sides of length \(|\vec{v}|\) and \(|\vec{w}|\) separated by angle \(\theta\) is exactly \(|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\sin\theta\), the magnitude of the cross product equals the parallelogram area. A triangle is half a parallelogram, so:

Parallelogram Area
\[\text{Area}_{\text{parallelogram}} = |\vec{u}\times\vec{v}|\]
u, v — the two edge vectors|u × v| = |u| |v| sin θ
Triangle Area
\[\text{Area}_{\triangle} = \tfrac{1}{2}|\vec{u}\times\vec{v}|\]
u, v — two edge vectors from the same vertex½ because a triangle is half a parallelogram

The Scalar Triple Product

The scalar triple product combines a dot product and a cross product to compute the volume of a parallelepiped (a "tilted box" whose faces are parallelograms). It also provides a quick test for coplanarity.

Scalar Triple Product (Volume)
\[V = |\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})| = \left|\det\begin{pmatrix}u_1&u_2&u_3\\v_1&v_2&v_3\\w_1&w_2&w_3\end{pmatrix}\right|\]
u, v, w — three edge vectors from the same vertexv × w — cross product (gives base area and normal)u · (v × w) — dot with third vector (gives signed volume)|...| — absolute value gives positive volume= 0 if and only if the three vectors are coplanar

The scalar triple product equals the determinant of the 3×3 matrix whose rows are the three vectors. A zero triple product means the vectors all lie in the same plane (coplanar), so the parallelepiped has zero volume — it's "flat."

Lines, Planes & Parametric Curves

From the vector toolkit of Chapter 1, we now describe geometric objects — lines, planes, and curves — in 2D and 3D space. This chapter also introduces parametric and polar representations, with applications to area and arc length.

§2.1–2.5   Lines and Planes

In single-variable calculus, a line is \(y=mx+b\). In multivariable calculus, we need a more flexible description that works in 3D. The key idea: a line is fully determined by any point on it plus a direction to move along.

Lines in Space

Line (Parametric Equation)

A line is determined by a point \(P\) and a direction vector \(\vec{v}\) parallel to the line. As the parameter \(t\) varies over all real numbers, the point \(\vec{r}(t)\) traces out every point on the line:

\[\vec{r}(t) = P + t\,\vec{v},\quad t\in\mathbb{R}\]
P — any known point on the line (the "anchor")v⃗ — direction vector (any vector parallel to the line)t — parameter (t=0 gives point P, t=1 gives P+v⃗)r⃗(t) — position vector of the point at parameter t

In 2D this gives \(\vec{r}(t)=(x,y)\) with two component equations. In 3D: \(\vec{r}(t)=(x,y,z)\), which means \(x=p_1+tv_1,\;y=p_2+tv_2,\;z=p_3+tv_3\).

If you're given two points on the line instead of a direction, compute the direction as the difference: \(\vec{v}=Q-P\). The direction vector is not unique — any scalar multiple works.

A line through point P in direction v⃗. Dashed extends mean t goes to ±∞.
★ 2D Line Shortcut

For a 2D line \(ax+by=c\), a direction vector is \((-b,\,a)\). This is perpendicular to the normal \((a,b)\).

Example 8 — Line Through Two Points

Find the equation of the line through \(P(1,2,3)\) and \(Q(3,1,1)\).

Solution

Thought process: We have two points but no direction vector. To use the parametric form \(\vec{r}=P+t\vec{v}\), we need a direction — compute it as \(Q-P\).

Setup: Point: \(P(1,2,3)\). Direction: \(\vec{v}=Q-P\).

1
Compute direction vector: \(\vec{v}=Q-P=(3-1,\;1-2,\;1-3)=(2,-1,-2)\)
2
Write parametric equation: \(\vec{r}(t)=(1,2,3)+t(2,-1,-2)\)
3
Component form: \(\boxed{x=1+2t,\quad y=2-t,\quad z=3-2t,\quad t\in\mathbb{R}}\)

★ Tips: Check your work: at \(t=0\) you should get \(P\), at \(t=1\) you should get \(Q\). Here: \(t=0\to(1,2,3)=P\) ✓, \(t=1\to(3,1,1)=Q\) ✓. Any scalar multiple of \(\vec{v}\) gives the same line — \((2,-1,-2)\) and \((-4,2,4)\) both work.

Line Segment

To trace only the portion of a line between \(P\) and \(Q\), restrict \(t\) to \([0,1]\):

\[\vec{r}(t) = (1-t)\,\vec{P}+t\,\vec{Q},\quad 0\le t\le 1\]
t=0 → at point P (start)t=1 → at point Q (end)t=½ → midpoint of PQ

This is just the parametric line formula rearranged: \(P+t(Q-P)=(1-t)P+tQ\).

Line segment from P to Q. Parameter t interpolates between endpoints.

Relations Between Two Lines in 3D

In 2D, two distinct lines either intersect or are parallel. In 3D there's a third option: skew lines, which are neither parallel nor intersecting — they pass each other in space without touching.

Classifying Two Lines

Given lines \(L_1\) with direction \(\vec{v}_1\) and \(L_2\) with direction \(\vec{v}_2\):

RelationHow to Test
Parallel\(\vec{v}_1\) and \(\vec{v}_2\) are scalar multiples (or equivalently, \(\vec{v}_1\times\vec{v}_2=\vec{0}\))
IntersectingNot parallel, AND solving the system for parameters gives consistent z-values
SkewNot parallel AND no intersection (z-values disagree after solving x,y)
Skew lines: neither parallel nor intersecting. Only possible in 3D.
Example 9 — Showing Two Lines Intersect

Show that \(L_1: x=2-t,\;y=-1-t,\;z=4-t\) and \(L_2: x=5-2s,\;y=-s,\;z=1+s\) intersect at \((1,-2,3)\).

Solution

Thought process: Different parameters (\(t\) for \(L_1\), \(s\) for \(L_2\)). Set the x-components equal and y-components equal to get two equations in two unknowns. Then CHECK if the z-components also agree — this is the critical step that distinguishes intersection from skew.

Setup: Set \(x_1=x_2\) and \(y_1=y_2\) to solve for \(t\) and \(s\). Then verify with z.

1
From \(x=x\): \(2-t=5-2s \;\Rightarrow\; 2s-t=3\) — equation (i)
2
From \(y=y\): \(-1-t=-s \;\Rightarrow\; s-t=1\) — equation (ii)
3
Subtract (ii) from (i): \(s=2\). Back-substitute: \(t=1\).
4
Verify z: \(L_1\): \(z=4-1=3\). \(L_2\): \(z=1+2=3\). Equal ✓
5
Point: \(L_1\) at \(t=1\): \((2-1,\;-1-1,\;4-1)=\boxed{(1,-2,3)}\) ✓

★ Tips: Always use different parameter names for different lines. The z-check is what makes this 3D — in 2D, two non-parallel lines always intersect.

Example 10 — Showing Lines Are Skew

Show that \(L_1: x=1+t,\;y=-2+3t,\;z=4-t\) and \(L_2: x=2s,\;y=3+s,\;z=-3+4s\) are skew.

Solution

Thought process: First check if they're parallel (compare direction vectors). If not, try to find an intersection. If that fails, they're skew.

1
Check parallel: \(\vec{v}_1=(1,3,-1)\), \(\vec{v}_2=(2,1,4)\). Not scalar multiples → not parallel.
2
From \(x=x\): \(1+t=2s\). From \(y=y\): \(-2+3t=3+s\). Solving: \(s=1.6,\;t=2.2\).
3
Verify z: \(L_1\): \(z=4-2.2=1.8\). \(L_2\): \(z=-3+4(1.6)=3.4\). Not equal → no intersection.
4
Not parallel + no intersection → \(\boxed{\text{skew lines}}\).

★ Tips: Skew lines only exist in 3D. If a problem says "in the plane" or you're working in 2D, the answer is never skew. The z-check failing is your proof of skewness.

Planes in 3D

While a line is defined by a direction along it, a plane is defined by a direction perpendicular to it. This perpendicular direction is called the normal vector.

Plane (General Equation)

A plane \(\Pi\) is determined by a point \(P(p_1,p_2,p_3)\) and a normal vector \(\vec{n}=(a,b,c)\) perpendicular to the plane:

\[ax+by+cz=d,\quad\text{where }d=ap_1+bp_2+cp_3\]
n⃗ = (a,b,c) — normal vector (perpendicular to the plane)d — constant, found by substituting any known point(a,b,c) are read directly from the normal vector

Any vector perpendicular to the plane works as a normal. If you have three points on the plane, find two in-plane vectors (e.g. \(\vec{PQ}\) and \(\vec{PR}\)), then take their cross product (recall from §1.9) to get the normal: \(\vec{n}=\vec{PQ}\times\vec{PR}\).

A plane defined by point P, with in-plane vectors PQ and PR. The normal n⃗ is perpendicular to the plane.
Example 12 — Plane Through Three Points

Find the equation of the plane through \(P(1,2,3)\), \(Q(-3,2,1)\), and \(R(2,4,5)\).

Solution

Thought process: Three points → need normal vector. Get two in-plane vectors from the three points, then cross product gives the normal. Finally, substitute any point to find \(d\).

Setup: Find \(\vec{PQ}\) and \(\vec{PR}\), cross them, then use the plane equation \(ax+by+cz=d\).

1
In-plane vectors: \(\vec{PQ}=Q-P=(-4,0,-2)\), \(\vec{PR}=R-P=(1,2,2)\)
2
Normal (cross product via determinant): \(\vec{n}=\vec{PQ}\times\vec{PR}=\begin{vmatrix}\vec{i}&\vec{j}&\vec{k}\\-4&0&-2\\1&2&2\end{vmatrix}=(0\cdot2-(-2)\cdot2,\;(-2)\cdot1-(-4)\cdot2,\;(-4)\cdot2-0\cdot1)=(4,6,-8)\)
3
Simplify: \(\vec{n}=2(2,3,-4)\), so use \((2,3,-4)\). Plane: \(2x+3y-4z=d\).
4
Sub \(P(1,2,3)\): \(d=2(1)+3(2)-4(3)=2+6-12=-4\)
5
\(\boxed{2x+3y-4z=-4}\)

★ Tips: Verify by substituting all three points. The normal is not unique — \((4,6,-8)\) and \((2,3,-4)\) give the same plane. Always simplify to avoid large coefficients.

Example 13 — Intersection of a Line and a Plane

Find the intersection between \(L: x=1+t,\;y=-2+3t,\;z=4-t\) and the plane \(3x+5y+8z=5\).

Solution

Thought process: Substitute the parametric line equations into the plane equation. This gives one equation in one unknown (\(t\)). Solve for \(t\), then plug back into the line to find the point.

1
Substitute into plane: \(3(1+t)+5(-2+3t)+8(4-t)=5\)
2
Expand: \(3+3t-10+15t+32-8t=5\;\Rightarrow\;10t+25=5\;\Rightarrow\;t=-2\)
3
Sub \(t=-2\) into line: \((1+(-2),\;-2+3(-2),\;4-(-2))=\boxed{(-1,-8,6)}\)

★ Tips: If \(t\) has no solution (contradiction like \(0=5\)), the line is parallel to the plane. If \(0=0\), the line lies entirely in the plane.

Angle and Intersection of Two Planes

Angle Between Two Planes

The angle between two planes equals the angle between their normal vectors:

\[\cos\theta=\frac{\vec{n}_1\cdot\vec{n}_2}{|\vec{n}_1|\,|\vec{n}_2|}\]
n⃗₁, n⃗₂ — normal vectors of the two planesθ — angle between the planes (same formula as angle between vectors, §1.7)

Two planes are parallel if their normal vectors are parallel (\(\vec{n}_1\times\vec{n}_2=\vec{0}\)).

Intersection of Two Planes

Two non-parallel planes intersect in a line. To find this line:

1. The direction of the line is \(\vec{n}_1\times\vec{n}_2\) (perpendicular to both normals).

2. To find one point on the line, set one variable to zero (e.g. \(z=0\)) and solve the two plane equations as a 2×2 system.

Two non-parallel planes intersect in a line. Direction from cross product of normals.
Example 16 — Intersection of Two Planes

Find the intersection of \(x-3y-2z=2\) and \(2x+y+3z=1\).

Solution

Thought process: Two non-parallel planes → intersection is a line. Get direction from cross product of normals, then find one point by setting a variable to zero.

1
Normals: \(\vec{n}_1=(1,-3,-2)\), \(\vec{n}_2=(2,1,3)\).
2
Direction: \(\vec{n}_1\times\vec{n}_2=(-7,-7,-7+0)=(-7,7,7)=7(-1,1,1)\). Use \((-1,1,1)\).
3
Find a point: set \(z=0\). System: \(x-3y=2,\;2x+y=1\). Multiply 2nd by 3: \(6x+3y=3\). Add to 1st: \(7x=5\Rightarrow x=5/7,\;y=-3/7\).
4
\(\boxed{x=\tfrac{5}{7}-t,\quad y=-\tfrac{3}{7}+t,\quad z=t}\)

★ Tips: Setting \(z=0\) is a convenience choice — any variable works. If it leads to no solution (rare), try a different variable. The direction vector can be simplified by removing common factors.

§2.6   Rotations and Translations in the Plane

Rotations and translations are coordinate transformations — they change how we describe points. An active rotation physically moves the point while keeping the axes fixed. A passive rotation keeps the point fixed but rotates the coordinate axes. Translations shift the origin to a new location.

Active rotation moves the point (uses A). Passive rotation rotates the axes (uses A⁻¹). Same θ, different matrices.
Active Rotation (Rotate the Point)

Rotate point \((x,y)\) counterclockwise by angle \(\theta\) about the origin:

\[\begin{pmatrix}x'\\y'\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\quad\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}\]
(x,y) — original point(x',y') — image after rotationθ — counterclockwise rotation angleA — rotation matrix (det A = 1, orthogonal)
Passive Rotation (Rotate the Axes)

Keep the point fixed, rotate the coordinate axes counterclockwise by \(\theta\). The point's coordinates in the new frame are:

\[\begin{pmatrix}x'\\y'\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&\sin\theta\\-\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\quad\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}\]
A⁻¹ — inverse rotation matrix (transpose of A)Signs on sin terms are flipped compared to active rotation
Translation of Origin

Move the origin to a new point \((h,k)\). The point's coordinates in the new frame:

\[\begin{pmatrix}x'\\y'\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}x-h\\y-k\end{pmatrix}\]
(h,k) — new origin location in the old frame(x',y') — coordinates in the new frame
★ Combined Transformations

Translation + Active rotation: First translate, then rotate: \(\binom{x'}{y'}=A\binom{x-h}{y-k}\).

Translation + Passive rotation: First translate, then apply \(A^{-1}\): \(\binom{x'}{y'}=A^{-1}\binom{x-h}{y-k}\).

Order matters! Translation is always applied first (subtract the new origin), then rotation.

Example 17 — Active Rotation

Find the image of \((-6,2)\) after a counterclockwise rotation by \(\theta=\pi/3\).

Solution

Thought process: Active rotation — use \(A\) (not \(A^{-1}\)). Plug \(\theta=\pi/3\) into the rotation matrix and multiply.

1
\(\cos(\pi/3)=1/2\), \(\sin(\pi/3)=\sqrt{3}/2\). Matrix: \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1/2&-\sqrt{3}/2\\\sqrt{3}/2&1/2\end{pmatrix}\)
2
\(\begin{pmatrix}x'\\y'\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/2&-\sqrt{3}/2\\\sqrt{3}/2&1/2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}-6\\2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-3-\sqrt{3}\\2\cdot\sqrt{3}/2+(-6)\cdot\sqrt{3}/2+1\end{pmatrix}\)
3
\(x'=(-6)(1/2)+(2)(-\sqrt{3}/2)=-3-\sqrt{3}\). \(y'=(-6)(\sqrt{3}/2)+(2)(1/2)=-3\sqrt{3}+1\).
4
\(\boxed{(x',y')=(-3-\sqrt{3},\;-3\sqrt{3}+1)}\)

★ Tips: Active vs passive: if the point moves, use \(A\). If the axes rotate, use \(A^{-1}\). The only difference is the sign on the \(\sin\theta\) terms.

§2.7–2.8   Parametric Curves

So far we've described curves as \(y=f(x)\). But many curves can't be written this way — a circle fails the vertical line test, and curves that cross themselves need multiple branches. Parametric equations solve this by describing both \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of a third variable (the parameter) \(t\).

A parametric curve (x,y) = (f(t), g(t)). Green arrows show the direction of increasing t.
Parametric Curve (Plane Curve)

The set of points \((x,y)=(f(t),\,g(t))\) for \(a\le t\le b\), where \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous functions. As \(t\) increases from \(a\) to \(b\), the point traces out the curve.

(f(a), g(a)) — initial point(f(b), g(b)) — terminal pointt — parameter (often represents time)

Conic Sections

The three classical conics — parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas — are important curves that appear throughout calculus. Each has a standard Cartesian equation and a natural parametrization.

Standard Conics — Equations and Parametrizations
ConicStandard FormParametrizationKey Relation
Parabola\((x-x_0)^2=4p(y-y_0)\)\(x=t,\; y=\tfrac{1}{4p}(t-x_0)^2+y_0\)Focus at distance \(p\) from vertex
Ellipse\(\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{a^2}+\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{b^2}=1\)\(x=x_0+a\cos t,\; y=y_0+b\sin t\)\(c^2=a^2-b^2\), \(a\ge b>0\)
Hyperbola\(\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{a^2}-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{b^2}=1\)\(x=x_0+a\sec t,\; y=y_0+b\tan t\)\(c^2=a^2+b^2\)

The ellipse parametrization uses \(\cos^2 t+\sin^2 t=1\). The hyperbola alternatively uses \(x=x_0+a\cosh t,\;y=y_0+b\sinh t\) via the identity \(\cosh^2 t-\sinh^2 t=1\).

The three conic sections with key features: focus, directrix, foci, semi-axes, asymptotes.
General Conic Classification

For the general second-degree equation \(Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0\), the discriminant \(\Delta=B^2-4AC\) determines the type:

\(B^2-4AC\)Type
\(=0\)Parabola (or degenerate: two parallel lines, one line, or empty)
\(>0\)Hyperbola (or two intersecting lines)
\(<0\)Ellipse (or circle, a point, or empty)

Calculus of Parametric Curves

To find slopes, tangent lines, and concavity of parametric curves, we use the chain rule. The key insight: \(dy/dx\) is the ratio of the rates \(dy/dt\) and \(dx/dt\).

First Derivative — Tangent Slope
\[\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt},\quad\text{provided }\frac{dx}{dt}\neq 0\]
dy/dt — rate of change of y with respect to the parameter tdx/dt — rate of change of x with respect to the parameter tdy/dx — slope of the tangent line to the curve at the point
Second Derivative — Concavity
\[\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)}{\frac{dx}{dt}}\]
Numerator — differentiate the first derivative dy/dx with respect to tDenominator — same dx/dt as befored²y/dx² > 0 → concave up; < 0 → concave down

Do NOT compute \(d^2y/dx^2\) as \((d^2y/dt^2)/(d^2x/dt^2)\) — this is a common error. The correct formula differentiates the first derivative, not the numerator/denominator separately.

★ Horizontal and Vertical Tangents

Horizontal tangent: \(dy/dt=0\) AND \(dx/dt\neq 0\) → slope is zero.

Vertical tangent: \(dx/dt=0\) AND \(dy/dt\neq 0\) → slope is undefined (vertical).

Both zero: If \(dy/dt=0\) and \(dx/dt=0\) simultaneously → the tangent is indeterminate. Need L'Hôpital's rule or further analysis (could be a cusp, corner, or smooth point).

Horizontal tangent when numerator dy/dt vanishes. Vertical when denominator dx/dt vanishes.
Example 27 — Tangent Lines to a Parametric Curve

Find the tangent line(s) to \(x=t^5-4t^3,\;y=t^2\) at the point \((0,4)\).

Solution

Thought process: We need the slope at a specific point. First find which value(s) of \(t\) give \((0,4)\) — there may be more than one (the curve might pass through this point twice). Then compute \(dy/dx\) at each \(t\).

Setup: From \(y=t^2=4\Rightarrow t=\pm 2\). Check: \(x=(\pm 2)^5-4(\pm 2)^3=\pm 32\mp 32=0\) ✓. Both work.

1
\(\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{2t}{5t^4-12t^2}=\frac{2}{5t^3-12t}\) (after cancelling \(t\neq 0\))
2
At \(t=-2\): slope \(=\frac{2}{5(-8)-12(-2)}=\frac{2}{-40+24}=\frac{2}{-16}=-\frac{1}{8}\)
3
At \(t=2\): slope \(=\frac{2}{5(8)-12(2)}=\frac{2}{40-24}=\frac{2}{16}=\frac{1}{8}\)
4
Tangent at \(t=-2\): \(y-4=-\frac{1}{8}(x-0)\Rightarrow\boxed{y=4-x/8}\)
5
Tangent at \(t=2\): \(\boxed{y=4+x/8}\)

★ Tips: A point on a parametric curve can correspond to multiple \(t\)-values — always check all of them. Each gives a different tangent line, creating a self-intersection.

Example 28 — Horizontal and Vertical Tangents

Find where \(x=t^3-3t,\;y=3t^2-9\) has horizontal or vertical tangents.

Solution

Thought process: Horizontal tangent: set \(dy/dt=0\) (numerator of slope). Vertical tangent: set \(dx/dt=0\) (denominator of slope). In each case verify the other derivative is nonzero.

1
\(dy/dt=6t\), \(dx/dt=3t^2-3=3(t^2-1)\)
2
Horizontal: \(6t=0\Rightarrow t=0\). Check: \(dx/dt|_{t=0}=-3\neq 0\) ✓. Point: \((0-0,\;0-9)=\boxed{(0,-9)}\).
3
Vertical: \(3(t^2-1)=0\Rightarrow t=\pm 1\). Check: \(dy/dt|_{t=1}=6\neq 0\) ✓, \(dy/dt|_{t=-1}=-6\neq 0\) ✓.
4
Points: \(t=1\to(1-3,\;3-9)=\boxed{(-2,-6)}\), \(t=-1\to(-1+3,\;3-9)=\boxed{(2,-6)}\).

★ Tips: At \(t=0\), \(dy/dt=0\) but \(dx/dt\neq 0\) → horizontal. At \(t=\pm 1\), \(dx/dt=0\) but \(dy/dt\neq 0\) → vertical. Always verify the "other" derivative isn't also zero.

§2.9   Applications to Area Problems

In Calculus I, the area under \(y=f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\). When the curve is parametric, we substitute \(x=f(t)\) and \(dx=f'(t)\,dt\) to convert everything to the parameter \(t\).

Area Under a Parametric Curve
\[A=\int_\alpha^\beta y(t)\,x'(t)\,dt\]
y(t) — height of the curve at parameter tx'(t) dt — infinitesimal width element (replaces dx)α, β — parameter values where x(α)=a and x(β)=b

This comes directly from \(\int_a^b y\,dx\) with the substitution \(dx=x'(t)\,dt\).

Area Enclosed by a Closed Parametric Curve

For a closed curve \(C: x=f(t),\;y=g(t),\;\alpha\le t\le\beta\) with \(f(\alpha)=f(\beta),\;g(\alpha)=g(\beta)\):

\[A(R)=\left|\int_\alpha^\beta g(t)\,f'(t)\,dt\right|\]
g(t) — y-component of the curvef'(t) — derivative of x-componentAbsolute value ensures positive area regardless of orientation
Cycloid: path traced by a point on a circle rolling along a line.
Example 30 — Area Under One Arch of a Cycloid

Find the area under one arch of the cycloid: \(x=r(t-\sin t),\;y=r(1-\cos t)\).

Solution

Thought process: The cycloid traces arches, each from \(y=0\) back to \(y=0\). One arch: \(t=0\) to \(t=2\pi\). Use the parametric area formula \(\int y\,x'\,dt\).

Setup: \(y=r(1-\cos t)\), \(x'=r(1-\cos t)\). Bounds: \(0\le t\le 2\pi\).

1
\(A=\int_0^{2\pi}r(1-\cos t)\cdot r(1-\cos t)\,dt=r^2\int_0^{2\pi}(1-\cos t)^2\,dt\)
2
Expand: \((1-\cos t)^2=1-2\cos t+\cos^2 t=1-2\cos t+\frac{1+\cos 2t}{2}\) (half-angle identity from Pre-knowledge)
3

§2.10   Arc Length

The arc length formula comes from summing infinitesimal line segments: each tiny piece has length \(\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}=\sqrt{(dx/dt)^2+(dy/dt)^2}\,dt\).

Each infinitesimal piece ds forms a right triangle: ds² = dx² + dy² (Pythagorean theorem).
Arc Length of a Parametric Curve
\[L=\int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\;dt\]
dx/dt, dy/dt — component velocitiesThe integrand is the speed: √(x'²+y'²)Curve must be traced exactly once over [α,β]

For curves given as \(y=f(x)\): set \(x=t\), \(dx/dt=1\), giving \(L=\int_a^b\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx\).

Cartesian coordinates locate a point by how far right and how far up it is. Polar coordinates instead specify how far from the origin and at what angle. Curves with rotational symmetry (circles, spirals, roses) have much simpler equations in polar form.

§3.1 — Limits and Continuity

In single-variable calculus, a function \(y=f(x)\) takes one input and produces one output. Now we work with functions of two (or more) variables: \(z = f(x,y)\). The graph of such a function is a surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Just as we studied limits of \(f(x)\) as \(x \to x_0\), we now study limits of \(f(x,y)\) as the point \((x,y)\) approaches a target point \((x_0,y_0)\) — but here the approach can come from infinitely many directions, not just left and right.

Limit of a Function of Two Variables

We write

\[\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} f(x,y) = L\]

if for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that whenever \(\sqrt{(x-x_0)^2+(y-y_0)^2} < \delta\), we have \(|f(x,y)-L| < \varepsilon\).

In words: no matter how tight a tolerance we demand on the output (\(\varepsilon\)), we can find a small enough disk around \((x_0,y_0)\) (\(\delta\)) so that every point inside that disk maps to within \(\varepsilon\) of \(L\).

★ Key Difference from Single-Variable

In one dimension, \(x \to x_0\) means approaching from the left or right (two directions). In two dimensions, \((x,y) \to (x_0,y_0)\) means approaching along any path whatsoever — straight lines, parabolas, spirals, or any curve. If even one path gives a different limit, the limit does not exist.

Proving a limit exists typically requires an \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) argument or algebraic bounding. Proving a limit does NOT exist is often easier: find two paths to the same point that give different limiting values.

Example 3.1 — Proving a Limit Exists (ε-δ)

Problem: Prove that \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{x^2 y^2}{x^2+y^2} = 0.\)

Show Solution ▾

Thought process: Plugging in gives 0/0 — indeterminate. We guess \(L = 0\) since the numerator has degree 4 while the denominator has degree 2, so the fraction should shrink to 0. We need an ε-δ proof.

Setup: We must show: for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(\delta > 0\) such that if \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2} < \delta\), then \(\left|\frac{x^2y^2}{x^2+y^2}\right| < \varepsilon\).

Solution:

Since \(|x| \le \sqrt{x^2+y^2} < \delta\) and \(|y| \le \sqrt{x^2+y^2} < \delta\), multiplying gives \(|x||y| \le \delta^2\).

Also, \((|x|-|y|)^2 \ge 0\) implies \(x^2+y^2 \ge 2|x||y|\), so \(\frac{1}{x^2+y^2} \le \frac{1}{2|x||y|}\).

Therefore: \[\frac{x^2y^2}{x^2+y^2} \le \frac{|x|^2|y|^2}{2|x||y|} = \frac{|x||y|}{2} < \frac{\delta^2}{2}.\]

Choose \(\delta = \sqrt{2\varepsilon}\). Then \(\delta^2/2 = \varepsilon\), so the expression is less than \(\varepsilon\). ∎

\[\boxed{\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{x^2 y^2}{x^2+y^2} = 0}\]
★ Tip

When bounding, use \(|x| \le \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) and \(|y| \le \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) freely. The key trick is expressing everything in terms of \(\delta = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\).

Example 3.2 — Proving a Limit Does Not Exist (Two-Path Test)

Problem: Prove that \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{xy}{x^2+y^2}\) does not exist.

Show Solution ▾

Thought process: Try two different paths to \((0,0)\). If they give different limits, the overall limit cannot exist.

Path 1 — along \(y = 0\): \(\frac{x \cdot 0}{x^2+0} = 0\). Limit = 0.

Path 2 — along \(y = x\): \(\frac{x \cdot x}{x^2+x^2} = \frac{x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{1}{2}\). Limit = 1/2.

Since \(0 \neq 1/2\), the two paths give different limits.

\[\boxed{\text{The limit does not exist (DNE).}}\]
★ Tip

Popular test paths: \(y=0\), \(x=0\), \(y=x\), \(y=mx\), \(y=x^2\). If \(y=mx\) gives a limit that depends on \(m\), the limit doesn't exist. You can also try polar: \(x=r\cos\theta, y=r\sin\theta\) — if the limit as \(r\to 0\) depends on \(\theta\), it DNE.

Continuity of f(x,y) at a Point

A function \(f\) is continuous at \((x_0,y_0)\) if:

1. \(f(x_0,y_0)\) is defined (a finite real number), and

2. \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)} f(x,y) = f(x_0,y_0)\).

Geometrically: the surface \(z=f(x,y)\) has no break, hole, or jump at that point.

★ Key Fact — Continuous Functions

All polynomials in \(x\) and \(y\) (like \(x^2y + y^2 - 3xy + 6\)) are continuous everywhere. Rational functions (ratio of polynomials) are continuous wherever the denominator is nonzero. Compositions with \(\sin, \cos, e^x, \ln\) remain continuous on their domains.

Example 3.3 — Proving Continuity

Problem: Show that \(f(x,y) = \sin(xy)\) is continuous at \((0,0)\).

Show Solution ▾

Step 1: \(f(0,0) = \sin(0) = 0\). ✓ Defined.

Step 2: Show \(\lim \sin(xy) = 0\). Use \(|\sin r| \le |r|\): \(|f(x,y)| = |\sin(xy)| \le |xy| \le |x||y| < \delta^2\).

Choose \(\delta = \sqrt{\varepsilon}\). Then \(|f(x,y)| < \varepsilon\). ∎

\[\boxed{f(x,y) = \sin(xy) \text{ is continuous at } (0,0).}\]
Example 3.4 — Function That Cannot Be Made Continuous

Problem: Let \(f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \frac{y^2}{x^2+y^2} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ c & (x,y)=(0,0) \end{cases}\). Show \(f\) is not continuous at \((0,0)\) for any \(c\).

Show Solution ▾

Path 1 — \(y=0\): \(f(x,0) = 0\). Limit = 0, forces \(c = 0\).

Path 2 — \(x=0\): \(f(0,y) = y^2/y^2 = 1\). Limit = 1, forces \(c = 1\).

Contradiction: \(c\) can't be both 0 and 1.

\[\boxed{f \text{ cannot be made continuous at } (0,0) \text{ for any } c.}\]

§3.2 — Partial Derivatives

When a function depends on several variables, we can ask: how does the output change when we wiggle just one input, holding all others fixed? This is a partial derivative.

Partial Derivative Definitions
\[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_0,y_0) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x_0+h,\,y_0) - f(x_0,y_0)}{h}, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0,y_0) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x_0,\,y_0+h) - f(x_0,y_0)}{h}\]
\(\partial f/\partial x\) — hold \(y\) constant, differentiate w.r.t. \(x\) \(\partial f/\partial y\) — hold \(x\) constant, differentiate w.r.t. \(y\)

Geometric meaning: \(f_x(x_0,y_0)\) is the slope of the curve formed by slicing the surface \(z = f(x,y)\) with the plane \(y = y_0\). Similarly, \(f_y\) is the slope of the slice by the plane \(x = x_0\). Each partial derivative gives the rate of change along one coordinate axis.

Notation: All equivalent: \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\ f_x,\ f_{(1)},\ D_xf\).

★ NOT Implicit Differentiation

Partial differentiation treats \(x\) and \(y\) as independent variables. Implicit differentiation (§3.6.2) assumes one variable depends on the others. Don't confuse them.

Example 3.5 — Computing Partial Derivatives

Problem: Let \(z = x^2 + 3xy + y^2\). Find \(\partial z/\partial x\) and \(\partial z/\partial y\).

Show Solution ▾

For \(\partial z/\partial x\) — treat \(y\) as a constant:

\[\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3xy) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(y^2) = 2x + 3y + 0 = 2x + 3y\]

(\(y^2\) is a constant → derivative is 0. In \(3xy\), the factor \(3y\) is a constant coefficient.)

For \(\partial z/\partial y\) — treat \(x\) as a constant:

\[\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = 0 + 3x + 2y = 3x + 2y\]

\[\boxed{\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 2x+3y, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = 3x+2y}\]
Example 3.6 — Three-Variable Partial Derivatives

Problem: Let \(f(x,y,z) = \ln(x+y^2+z^3)\). Find \(f_x, f_y, f_z\).

Show Solution ▾

Chain rule with outer \(\ln u\), inner \(u = x+y^2+z^3\):

\[f_x = \frac{1}{x+y^2+z^3}, \quad f_y = \frac{2y}{x+y^2+z^3}, \quad f_z = \frac{3z^2}{x+y^2+z^3}\]

\[\boxed{f_x = \frac{1}{x+y^2+z^3},\quad f_y = \frac{2y}{x+y^2+z^3},\quad f_z = \frac{3z^2}{x+y^2+z^3}}\]

§3.3 — Higher-Order Partial Derivatives

Just as in single-variable calculus, we can differentiate partial derivatives again. The four second-order partials of \(f(x,y)\) are: \(f_{xx}\), \(f_{xy}\), \(f_{yx}\), and \(f_{yy}\).

Clairaut's Theorem (Young's Theorem)
\[f_{xy} = f_{yx}\]
\(f_{xy}\) — differentiate w.r.t. \(x\) first, then \(y\) \(f_{yx}\) — differentiate w.r.t. \(y\) first, then \(x\)

If \(f\) and all its partial derivatives up to order 2 are continuous, then mixed partials are equal — the order of differentiation doesn't matter. This extends to higher orders.

Example 3.7 — Second-Order Partials and Clairaut's Theorem

Problem: Let \(z = 2x^2 - 5xy + y^2\). Find all four second-order partials. Verify \(z_{xy}=z_{yx}\).

Show Solution ▾

First partials: \(z_x = 4x - 5y\), \(\;z_y = -5x + 2y\).

Second partials:

\(z_{xx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(4x-5y) = 4\)

\(z_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(4x-5y) = -5\)

\(z_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-5x+2y) = -5\)

\(z_{yy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-5x+2y) = 2\)

✓ \(z_{xy} = z_{yx} = -5\) — Clairaut's theorem confirmed.

\[\boxed{z_{xx}=4,\quad z_{xy}=z_{yx}=-5,\quad z_{yy}=2}\]

§3.5 — Directional Derivatives and Gradients

Partial derivatives measure rates of change along the coordinate axes. But what about any other direction? The directional derivative answers this, and the gradient ties it all together.

The Gradient

The gradient of \(f\) at a point \(P\), written \(\nabla f(P)\), is the vector of all partial derivatives:

\[\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\;\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) \quad\text{(2D)}, \qquad \nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\;\frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\;\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right) \quad\text{(3D)}\]

Directional Derivative via the Gradient
\[D_{\mathbf{u}}f(P) = \nabla f(P) \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\nabla f(P)|\,|\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta\]
\(D_{\mathbf{u}}f\) — rate of change in direction \(\mathbf{u}\) \(\nabla f\) — gradient vector \(\theta\) — angle between \(\nabla f\) and \(\mathbf{u}\)

When \(\mathbf{u}\) is a unit vector, this is the directional derivative: the instantaneous rate of change per unit distance in that direction. Setting \(\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{i}\) recovers \(f_x\); setting \(\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{j}\) recovers \(f_y\).

Direction of Steepest Ascent & Descent
\[\max D_{\mathbf{u}}f = |\nabla f| \;\;\text{when } \mathbf{u} = \frac{\nabla f}{|\nabla f|}, \qquad \min D_{\mathbf{u}}f = -|\nabla f| \;\;\text{when } \mathbf{u} = -\frac{\nabla f}{|\nabla f|}\]
\(|\nabla f|\) — maximum rate of increase \(\nabla f/|\nabla f|\) — direction of steepest ascent

\(f\) increases fastest in the gradient direction. It decreases fastest opposite to the gradient. Perpendicular to the gradient (\(\cos\theta = 0\)), \(D_{\mathbf{u}}f = 0\) — you're on a level curve.

★ Gradient Perpendicular to Level Curves

The gradient \(\nabla f\) at any point is perpendicular to the level curve \(f(x,y) = c\) through that point, and points toward increasing \(f\). This is why hikers going straight uphill are walking in the gradient direction, and contour lines on a map are perpendicular to the steepest slope.

Example 3.8 — Maximum Directional Derivative

Problem: Let \(f(x,y) = e^x\cos y\), \(P(2, -\pi/4)\). Find the unit vector giving the maximum \(D_{\mathbf{u}}f\) and its value.

Show Solution ▾

Gradient: \(\nabla f = (e^x\cos y,\;-e^x\sin y)\). At \(P\): \(\nabla f = \left(\frac{e^2}{\sqrt{2}},\;\frac{e^2}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\).

\(|\nabla f| = \sqrt{\frac{e^4}{2}+\frac{e^4}{2}} = e^2\). Direction: \(\mathbf{u} = \frac{\nabla f}{|\nabla f|} = \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\).

\[\boxed{\mathbf{u} = \left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right), \quad \max D_{\mathbf{u}}f = e^2}\]
Example 3.9 — Directional Derivative in a Given Direction

Problem: Let \(f(x,y) = x^2-y^2\), \(P(2,-1)\), \(\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j}\). Find \(D_{\mathbf{u}}f(P)\) and the directional derivative at \(P\) in the same direction.

Show Solution ▾

Gradient: \(\nabla f = (2x, -2y)\). At \(P(2,-1)\): \(\nabla f = (4, 2)\).

Derivative w.r.t. \(\mathbf{u}\): \(D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = (4,2)\cdot(1,-1) = 4 - 2 = 2\).

Directional derivative (requires unit vector): \(\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u}}{|\mathbf{u}|} = \frac{(1,-1)}{\sqrt{2}}\).

\(D_{\mathbf{v}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{v} = (4,2)\cdot\frac{(1,-1)}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{2}\).

Check: \(D_{\mathbf{u}}f = |\mathbf{u}|\cdot D_{\mathbf{v}}f = \sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{2} = 2\). ✓

\[\boxed{D_{\mathbf{u}}f(P) = 2, \qquad D_{\mathbf{v}}f(P) = \sqrt{2}}\]

§3.6 — The Chain Rule in ℝ² and ℝ³

When the input variables of \(f\) are themselves functions of other parameters, we chain the derivatives. The multivariable chain rule generalizes \(\frac{d}{dt}f(r(t)) = f'(r(t))\,r'(t)\) to higher dimensions.

Chain Rule — One Parameter
\[\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}\]
\(z = f(x,y)\) where \(x = x(t),\;y = y(t)\) \(dz/dt\) — total derivative w.r.t. \(t\)

For three variables: add \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial w}\frac{dw}{dt}\). The pattern: sum over all intermediate variables.

Chain Rule — Two Parameters
\[\frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\]
\(x = x(s,t),\;y = y(s,t)\) — depend on two parameters
★ Tree Diagram Mnemonic

Draw a tree: \(z\) at the top branches to \(x\) and \(y\); each branches to \(s,t\). Each path from \(z\) to a bottom variable gives one term (multiply partials along the path). Add all paths to the same bottom variable.

Figure 3.3 — Chain rule tree: multiply partials along each path from z to t, then add. Each root-to-leaf path gives one term.
Example 3.10 — Related Rates via Chain Rule

Problem: A cylinder has radius 6″ (increasing at 0.2 in/s) and height 8″ (decreasing at 0.4 in/s). Find \(dV/dt\).

Show Solution ▾

\(V = \pi r^2 h\). \(\frac{dV}{dt} = 2\pi rh\frac{dr}{dt} + \pi r^2\frac{dh}{dt} = 2\pi(6)(8)(0.2) + \pi(36)(-0.4) = 19.2\pi - 14.4\pi\).

\[\boxed{\frac{dV}{dt} = 4.8\pi \approx 15.08\;\text{in}^3/\text{s}}\]

§3.6.2 — Implicit Differentiation

When a surface is defined implicitly by \(f(x,y,z) = 0\), we can find \(\partial z/\partial x\) and \(\partial z/\partial y\) without solving for \(z\) explicitly.

Implicit Partial Derivatives
\[\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{f_x}{f_z}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{f_y}{f_z}\]
\(f(x,y,z) = 0\) — implicit equation \(f_z \neq 0\) required
Example 3.11 — Implicit Differentiation

Problem: Let \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 + 2xyz - 10 = 0\). Find \(\partial z/\partial x\) at \(P(1,1,2)\).

Show Solution ▾

Set \(f = x^2+y^2+z^2+2xyz-10\). Then \(f_x = 2x+2yz = 6\), \(f_z = 2z+2xy = 6\) at \(P\).

\(\partial z/\partial x = -f_x/f_z = -6/6 = -1\).

\[\boxed{\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\bigg|_P = -1}\]

§3.6.3 — Tangent Planes and Normal Lines

In single-variable calculus, the tangent line approximates a curve. In multivariable calculus, the tangent plane approximates a surface. The gradient provides the normal direction.

Tangent Plane to f(x,y,z) = 0
\[f_x(P)(x-x_0) + f_y(P)(y-y_0) + f_z(P)(z-z_0) = 0\]
\(P = (x_0,y_0,z_0)\) — point on surface \(\nabla f(P)\) — normal to the plane
★ Special Case: z = g(x,y)

Rewrite as \(g(x,y) - z = 0\). Then \(f_z = -1\) and the tangent plane becomes:

\[z - z_0 = g_x(P)(x-x_0) + g_y(P)(y-y_0)\]

This is the multivariable analog of the tangent line formula \(y-y_0 = f'(x_0)(x-x_0)\).

Normal Line to a Surface
\[\mathbf{r}(t) = P + t\,\nabla f(P) = (x_0+f_x t,\;\;y_0+f_y t,\;\;z_0+f_z t)\]
Direction = \(\nabla f(P)\) Passes through \(P\), perpendicular to tangent plane
Figure 3.4 — The tangent plane touches the surface at P. The gradient ∇f(P) is the normal vector, perpendicular to the plane.
Example 3.12 — Tangent Plane and Normal Line

Problem: Find the tangent plane and normal line to \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = 9\) at \(P(1,2,2)\).

Show Solution ▾

\(f = x^2+y^2+z^2-9\). \(\nabla f = (2x,2y,2z)\). At \(P\): \(\nabla f = (2,4,4)\).

Tangent plane: \(2(x-1)+4(y-2)+4(z-2)=0 \implies x+2y+2z=9\).

Normal line: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1+2t,\;2+4t,\;2+4t)\).

\[\boxed{\text{Plane: } x+2y+2z=9 \qquad \text{Normal: } \mathbf{r}(t) = (1+2t,\,2+4t,\,2+4t)}\]
★ Tip

For a sphere centered at the origin, the normal line at any surface point always passes through \((0,0,0)\). Quick check: set \(t=-1/2\) to get \((0,0,0)\). ✓

Example 3.13 — Tangent Plane to z = g(x,y)

Problem: Find the tangent plane to \(z = x^2 + 2y^2\) at \((1,1,3)\).

Show Solution ▾

\(g_x = 2x = 2\), \(g_y = 4y = 4\) at \((1,1)\). Tangent plane: \(z - 3 = 2(x-1)+4(y-1)\).

\[\boxed{z = 2x + 4y - 3}\]

§3.7 — Conservative Fields, Curl, and Divergence

A vector field assigns a vector to every point in space: \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = P\,\mathbf{i} + Q\,\mathbf{j} + R\,\mathbf{k}\). Examples include gravitational fields, electric fields, and fluid velocity fields.

Conservative Vector Field

A vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative if there exists a scalar field \(f\) (called the potential function) such that \(\mathbf{F} = \nabla f\). Think of it as the multivariable analog of finding an antiderivative.

Curl of a Vector Field
\[\text{curl}\;\mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \\ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix}\]
\(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\) — curl (outputs a vector) Measures rotation at a point

Expanding: \(\text{curl}\;\mathbf{F} = (R_y-Q_z)\,\mathbf{i} - (R_x-P_z)\,\mathbf{j} + (Q_x-P_y)\,\mathbf{k}\).

In 2D (\(\mathbf{F} = P\,\mathbf{i}+Q\,\mathbf{j}\)): \(\text{curl}\;\mathbf{F} = (Q_x - P_y)\,\mathbf{k}\).

Conservative Field Test
\[\mathbf{F} \text{ is conservative} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{curl}\;\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\]
In 2D: \(Q_x = P_y\) In 3D: \(R_y = Q_z\), \(P_z = R_x\), \(Q_x = P_y\)
Divergence of a Vector Field
\[\text{div}\;\mathbf{F} = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}\]
\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\) — divergence (outputs a scalar) \(>0\): source, \(<0\): sink, \(=0\): incompressible
★ Key Identities

1. \(\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}\) — curl of a gradient is always zero (conservative ⟹ irrotational).

2. \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0\) — divergence of a curl is always zero.

Both follow from Clairaut's theorem: mixed partials are equal.

Figure 3.5 — Divergence measures net outward flow (source/sink). Curl measures rotational tendency. div = 0 means incompressible; curl = 0 means irrotational (conservative).
Example 3.14 — Conservative Field Test (2D)

Problem: Is \(\mathbf{F} = (x^2+y^2)\,\mathbf{i} + 2xy\,\mathbf{j}\) conservative?

Show Solution ▾

\(P = x^2+y^2\), \(Q = 2xy\). Check: \(Q_x = 2y\), \(P_y = 2y\). Equal ✓.

\[\boxed{\text{Yes — } Q_x = P_y = 2y, \text{ so } \mathbf{F} \text{ is conservative.}}\]
Example 3.15 — Finding a Potential Function

Problem: Find \(f\) such that \(\nabla f = (4x^3y^4+2x)\,\mathbf{i} + (4y^3x^4+2y)\,\mathbf{j}\).

Show Solution ▾

Step 1: Integrate \(f_x = 4x^3y^4+2x\) w.r.t. \(x\): \(f = x^4y^4 + x^2 + g(y)\).

Step 2: Differentiate w.r.t. \(y\): \(f_y = 4x^4y^3 + g'(y)\). Set equal to \(Q = 4y^3x^4+2y\): \(g'(y) = 2y\), so \(g(y) = y^2+C\).

\[\boxed{f(x,y) = x^4y^4 + x^2 + y^2 + C}\]
★ Tip

Always verify: \(\nabla f = (4x^3y^4+2x,\;4x^4y^3+2y) = \mathbf{F}\). ✓ If starting with \(f_x\) leads to messy integrals, try starting with \(f_y\) instead.

Example 3.16 — Conservative Field Test (3D)

Problem: Show \(\mathbf{F} = 4xy^2z\,\mathbf{i} + 4x^2yz\,\mathbf{j} + 2x^2y^2\,\mathbf{k}\) is conservative.

Show Solution ▾

\(P = 4xy^2z\), \(Q = 4x^2yz\), \(R = 2x^2y^2\).

\(Q_x = 8xyz = P_y\) ✓. \(R_x = 4xy^2 = P_z\) ✓. \(R_y = 4x^2y = Q_z\) ✓.

All three conditions met: curl \(\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\).

\[\boxed{\text{curl } \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{F} \text{ is conservative.}}\]
Figure 3.6 — The grad–curl–div hierarchy. Gradient maps scalars to vectors; curl maps vectors to vectors; divergence maps vectors to scalars. Two key identities: curl of any gradient is zero; divergence of any curl is zero.
Example 3.17 — Computing Divergence

Problem: Find \(\text{div}\;\mathbf{F}\) for \(\mathbf{F} = \sin x\,\mathbf{i} + x\cos y\,\mathbf{j} + \sin z\,\mathbf{k}\) at \(P(\pi/2, 0, \pi)\).

Show Solution ▾

\(\text{div}\;\mathbf{F} = \cos x - x\sin y + \cos z\). At \(P\): \(0 - 0 + (-1) = -1\).

\[\boxed{\text{div}\;\mathbf{F}(P) = -1 \;\text{(sink)}}\]
Example 3.18 — Proving div(curl F) = 0

Problem: Show \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0\) for any twice-differentiable \(\mathbf{F} = (P,Q,R)\).

Show Solution ▾

\(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = (R_y-Q_z,\;P_z-R_x,\;Q_x-P_y)\).

\(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = R_{yx}-Q_{zx}+P_{zy}-R_{xy}+Q_{xz}-P_{yz}\).

By Clairaut's theorem: \(R_{yx}=R_{xy}\), \(Q_{zx}=Q_{xz}\), \(P_{zy}=P_{yz}\). Each pair cancels.

\[\boxed{\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0}\]

§4.1 — Line Integrals with Respect to Arc Length

In single-variable calculus you integrate a function over an interval \([a,b]\). A line integral extends this idea: instead of integrating over a straight interval, you integrate a function along a curve \(\mathscr{C}\) in the plane or in space. The function \(f\) is evaluated at points on the curve, and you sum up \(f\) values weighted by tiny arc-length increments \(ds\).

Definition — Line Integral w.r.t. Arc Length

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be a regular curve parametrized by \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) for \(t\in[a,b]\), and let \(f\) be a continuous scalar field defined on \(\mathscr{C}\). Partition \(\mathscr{C}\) into subarcs of lengths \(\Delta s_i\), pick sample points \(P_i^*\) on each subarc, and form the Riemann sum \(\sum f(P_i^*)\Delta s_i\). If this limit exists as \(\max\{\Delta s_i\}\to 0\), we call it the line integral of \(f\) over \(\mathscr{C}\) with respect to arc length:

Line Integral (arc-length form)
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}} f(x,y,z)\,ds = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^{n}f(P_i^*)\,\Delta s_i\]
f — scalar field evaluated on the curveds — infinitesimal arc length𝒞 — the curve of integration

Key Properties

✓ f = 1 → Arc Length Setting \(f=1\) gives the total arc length of the curve: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}1\,ds = L\). The simplest line integral — just measuring how long the curve is.
✓ Piecewise Curves If \(\mathscr{C}\) is made of pieces \(\mathscr{C}_1,\mathscr{C}_2,\ldots\), add up each piece: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds=\int_{\mathscr{C}_1}f\,ds+\int_{\mathscr{C}_2}f\,ds+\cdots\)
✓ Mass of a Wire If \(f\) is mass density (g/cm), the integral gives total mass: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds=M\). Think: density × length, summed along the curve.
✓ Curtain Area If \(f\geq 0\), the integral equals the area of the "curtain" hanging from \(z=f(x,y)\) down to the curve in the \(xy\)-plane (see Fig 4.1 below).
★ Orientation-Independent Reversing the direction of \(\mathscr{C}\) gives the same answer. This is NOT true for vector field integrals (§4.2).
Fig 4.1 — The line integral ∫𝒞 f ds equals the area of the "curtain" hanging from the curve z = f(x,y) down to the base curve 𝒞 in the xy-plane.

Computation Formulas

General Parametric (3D)
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}} f(x,y,z)\,ds = \int_a^b f\!\big(x(t),y(t),z(t)\big)\sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2+z'(t)^2}\;dt\]
r(t) = (x(t), y(t), z(t)) — parametrization|r′(t)| = √(x′²+y′²+z′²) — speed[a,b] — parameter interval

In compact notation: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds=\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\).

2D Special Case
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}} f(x,y)\,ds = \int_a^b f\!\big(x(t),y(t)\big)\sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2}\;dt\]

If \(\mathscr{C}\) is parametrized by arc length (so \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=1\)), the formula simplifies to \(\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,dt\).

★ Strategy for Computing Line Integrals (Scalar)

Step 1: Parametrize the curve: find \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) and the interval \([a,b]\).

Step 2: Compute \(\mathbf{r}'(t)\) and then \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\).

Step 3: Substitute \(x=x(t),\,y=y(t),\,z=z(t)\) into \(f\) to get \(f(\mathbf{r}(t))\).

Step 4: Evaluate the single-variable integral \(\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\).

Example 4.1 — Line Integral of x Along a Parabola (Ex. 200)

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be the arc of the parabola \(y=x^2\) from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\). Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}x\,ds\) and show the value is independent of orientation.

Show Solution

Thought process: We need a parametrization of \(y=x^2\) from \((0,0)\) to \((1,1)\). The simplest choice: let \(t=x\).

Setup: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t,\,t^2)\), \(t\in[0,1]\). Then \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,2t)\), \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=\sqrt{1+4t^2}\).

1
Substitute into the integral: \(\int_0^1 t\sqrt{1+4t^2}\,dt\).
2
Let \(u=1+4t^2\), so \(du=8t\,dt\), i.e., \(t\,dt=du/8\). When \(t=0\), \(u=1\); when \(t=1\), \(u=5\).
3
\(=\frac{1}{8}\int_1^5\sqrt{u}\,du=\frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\Big|_1^5=\frac{1}{12}\left(5\sqrt{5}-1\right)\).

Answer: \(\displaystyle\frac{5\sqrt{5}-1}{12}\)

Orientation check: Reversing the curve (from \((1,1)\) to \((0,0)\)) uses \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t,(1-t)^2)\). A similar u-substitution yields the same value — confirming that line integrals w.r.t. arc length are orientation-independent.

Example 4.2 — 3D Helix Integral (Ex. 201)

Define \(\mathscr{C}\) by \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t\cos t,\,t\sin t,\,t)\) for \(t\in[-1,1]\) and \(f(x,y,z)=z\). Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}z\,ds\).

Show Solution

Setup: \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(-t\sin t+\cos t,\;t\cos t+\sin t,\;1)\).

1
Compute \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|^2=(-t\sin t+\cos t)^2+(t\cos t+\sin t)^2+1\). Expand: \(=t^2\sin^2t-2t\sin t\cos t+\cos^2t+t^2\cos^2t+2t\sin t\cos t+\sin^2t+1=t^2+1+1=t^2+2\).
2
Since \(z=t\) on \(\mathscr{C}\): \(\int_{-1}^{1}t\sqrt{2+t^2}\,dt\).
3
The integrand \(t\sqrt{2+t^2}\) is an odd function on the symmetric interval \([-1,1]\).

Answer: \(0\) (odd function on symmetric interval)

Example 4.3 — Piecewise Curve (Triangle, Ex. 203)

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be the perimeter of the triangle in the \(xz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0)\), \((1,0)\), \((1,1)\), oriented counterclockwise. Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}(x^2+z^2)\,ds\).

Show Solution

Thought process: The curve consists of three line segments. Use different parameter symbols \(t_1,t_2,t_3\) for each to avoid confusion (the arc-length element \(ds\) depends on the segment).

\(\mathscr{C}_1\): \((0,0)\to(1,0)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t_1)=(t_1,0)\), \(t_1\in[0,1]\), \(|\mathbf{r}'|=1\).

\(\mathscr{C}_2\): \((1,0)\to(1,1)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t_2)=(1,t_2)\), \(t_2\in[0,1]\), \(|\mathbf{r}'|=1\).

\(\mathscr{C}_3\): \((1,1)\to(0,0)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t_3)=(1-t_3,1-t_3)\), \(t_3\in[0,1]\), \(|\mathbf{r}'|=\sqrt{2}\).

1
\(\int_{\mathscr{C}_1}(x^2+z^2)\,ds=\int_0^1 t_1^2\cdot 1\,dt_1=\frac{1}{3}\).
2
\(\int_{\mathscr{C}_2}(x^2+z^2)\,ds=\int_0^1(1+t_2^2)\cdot 1\,dt_2=1+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{4}{3}\).
3
\(\int_{\mathscr{C}_3}(x^2+z^2)\,ds=\int_0^1 2(1-t_3)^2\cdot\sqrt{2}\,dt_3=2\sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3}=\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}\).

Answer: \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{3}+\frac{4}{3}+\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}=\frac{5+2\sqrt{2}}{3}\)

★ Tip: Always use different parameter variables for each piece of a piecewise curve. The arc-length element \(ds\) is determined by the curve, not the parameter name.

Example 4.4 — Mass of a Wire (Ex. 204)

A thin wire has the shape \(\mathscr{C}:\; y=2x^2\), \(1\leq x\leq 2\), with mass density \(f(x,y)=y/x\) in g/cm. Find the total mass.

Show Solution

Setup: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t,2t^2)\), \(t\in[1,2]\). Then \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,4t)\), \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=\sqrt{1+16t^2}\).

\(f(\mathbf{r}(t))=y(t)/x(t)=2t^2/t=2t\).

1
\(M=\int_1^2 2t\sqrt{1+16t^2}\,dt\). Let \(u=1+16t^2\), \(du=32t\,dt\), so \(2t\,dt=du/16\).
2
Bounds: \(u(1)=17\), \(u(2)=65\). So \(M=\frac{1}{16}\int_{17}^{65}\sqrt{u}\,du=\frac{1}{16}\cdot\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\Big|_{17}^{65}=\frac{65\sqrt{65}-17\sqrt{17}}{24}\approx 18.9\) g.

Average Value and Center of Mass

Why these matter: Imagine a wire (not necessarily straight) with varying thickness. The average value tells you what the "typical" density is along the wire. The center of mass tells you where you'd need to place your finger to balance the wire.

Average Value on a Curve
\[\bar{f}=\frac{1}{L}\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds\]
L — total arc length of 𝒞f̄ (f-bar) — average value of f along 𝒞

This is the curve version of the single-variable average \(\bar{f}=\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\). Instead of dividing by the interval length \(b-a\), you divide by the curve length \(L\). The bar notation \(\bar{f}\) means "average of \(f\)."

Center of Mass of a Wire
\[\bar{x}=\frac{1}{M}\int_{\mathscr{C}}x\,f\,ds,\quad\bar{y}=\frac{1}{M}\int_{\mathscr{C}}y\,f\,ds,\quad\bar{z}=\frac{1}{M}\int_{\mathscr{C}}z\,f\,ds\]
M — total mass (M = ∫𝒞 f ds)f — mass density at each point(x̄, ȳ, z̄) — the balance point

Each coordinate of the center of mass is a weighted average: \(\bar{x}\) is the average \(x\)-position, weighted by how much mass is at each point. The bar notation \(\bar{x}\), \(\bar{y}\), \(\bar{z}\) means "average coordinate." If the density is constant (\(f=c\) everywhere), the center of mass is the geometric center of the curve.

§4.3 — Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

This section ties together line integrals and conservative fields (recall from §3.7 that \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative if \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\) for some scalar field \(f\), equivalently \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\)).

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for Line Integrals

The problem: Computing \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) normally requires parametrizing the curve, dotting, and integrating — a lot of work. Is there a shortcut?

The shortcut: If \(\mathbf{F}\) happens to be a gradient field (\(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\) for some scalar \(f\)), then the entire line integral collapses to just plugging in the endpoints:

FTCLI
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\int_{\mathscr{C}}\nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r}=f(\mathbf{r}(b))-f(\mathbf{r}(a))\]
f — potential function (∇f gives you F)r(a) — start pointr(b) — end point

When to use it: Whenever the field is conservative. First verify \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\) (from §3.7), then find the potential \(f\), then just subtract: \(f(\text{end})-f(\text{start})\). No parametrization needed.

Analogy: This is exactly like the single-variable FTC: \(\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx=f(b)-f(a)\). There, you skip the Riemann sum by finding an antiderivative. Here, you skip the parametrization by finding a potential function.

Consequences

★ Path Independence

If \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\), the integral depends only on the endpoints, not on the path taken between them. All paths from \(P\) to \(Q\) give the same answer: \(f(Q)-f(P)\).

Fig 4.3 — Path independence: when F = ∇f is conservative, all paths from P to Q yield the same integral f(Q) − f(P).
Closed-Loop Test
\[\mathbf{F}\text{ conservative and }\mathscr{C}\text{ closed}\;\Longrightarrow\;\oint_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=0\]

What this says: If a field is conservative and you walk in a closed loop (ending where you started), the total work is zero. Makes sense from the FTCLI: \(f(\text{end})-f(\text{start})=0\) because end = start.

Why it's useful: It works as a test. If you find ANY closed path where \(\oint\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\neq 0\), the field is definitely NOT conservative. Conversely, if the integral is zero around every closed loop (in a simply connected region), the field IS conservative.

Testing for Conservativeness (Summary)

Equivalences in a Simply Connected Region

For a continuously differentiable \(\mathbf{F}\) defined on a simply connected region \(\mathscr{R}\) (no holes), the following are equivalent:

1. \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\) (curl-free / irrotational)

2. There exists a scalar field \(f\) such that \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\) (gradient field)

3. \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) is path-independent (depends only on endpoints)

A simply connected region is one where every closed curve can be continuously shrunk to a point without leaving \(\mathscr{R}\). Disks, rectangles, and all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) are simply connected. A disk with its center removed is NOT simply connected.

Finding the Potential Function via Line Integrals

Once you verify \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative, the potential function can be found by evaluating:

Potential via Line Integral
\[f(x,y,z)=\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\]
𝒞 — any curve from a fixed P₀ to (x,y,z)The easiest choice: a line segment from the origin

The simplest path is the line segment from a convenient fixed point \(P_0\) to a general point \(P(x,y,z)\), parametrized as \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t)P_0+tP\), \(t\in[0,1]\). During integration, treat \(x,y,z\) as constants.

★ Two Methods to Find Potential Functions

Method 1 (Component integration, from §3.7): Integrate \(f_x=P\) w.r.t. \(x\), then match \(f_y=Q\) and \(f_z=R\) to determine the "constants" of integration. Best for polynomial \(\mathbf{F}\).

Method 2 (Line integral method, eq. 4.12): Choose \(P_0\), parametrize the segment from \(P_0\) to \((x,y,z)\), integrate. The answer gives \(f\) directly. Best for complicated non-polynomial fields.

Work-Energy Theorem

Why this matters: This connects the math (line integrals) to physics (kinetic energy). It's the reason line integrals are so important in science and engineering.

Kinetic Energy and Line Integrals

The idea: When a force \(\mathbf{F}\) pushes a mass \(m\) along a curve \(\mathscr{C}\), the work done by \(\mathbf{F}\) equals the change in the object's kinetic energy:

\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}(b)|^2}_{\text{KE at end}}-\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}(a)|^2}_{\text{KE at start}}\]

In plain English: Positive work speeds the object up; negative work slows it down. The line integral tells you exactly how much.

Conservation of energy: If \(\mathbf{F}\) is also conservative with potential \(f\), then the total energy \(\frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}|^2 + f\) stays constant along the path. Energy can convert between kinetic (motion) and potential (position) forms, but the total never changes.

§5.1 — Describing Planar Regions

Before integrating over a region, you need to describe it in a way that gives you integration limits. The key idea: describe the region using slices — either vertical (fixing \(x\)) or horizontal (fixing \(y\)).

x-simple Region (Type I)

A region \(\mathscr{R}\subseteq\mathbb{R}^2\) is x-simple if every vertical line through \(\mathscr{R}\) intersects it in at most two points. Equivalently:

\[\mathscr{R}_{yx}=\{(x,y): f_1(x)\leq y\leq f_2(x),\; a\leq x\leq b\}\]

The outer variable is \(x\) (constant limits), the inner is \(y\) (limits depend on \(x\)). Integrate dy dx.

y-simple Region (Type II)

A region \(\mathscr{R}\) is y-simple if every horizontal line through \(\mathscr{R}\) intersects it in at most two points:

\[\mathscr{R}_{xy}=\{(x,y): g_1(y)\leq x\leq g_2(y),\; c\leq y\leq d\}\]

The outer variable is \(y\), the inner is \(x\). Integrate dx dy.

★ Memory Trick: Subscript = Integration Order

\(\mathscr{R}_{yx}\) → integrate \(y\) first, then \(x\) → dy dx (vertical slices).

\(\mathscr{R}_{xy}\) → integrate \(x\) first, then \(y\) → dx dy (horizontal slices).

The innermost integral has limits that depend on the outer variable. The outer integral has constant limits.

Fig 5.1 — Left: x-simple region with vertical slices (integrate dy then dx). Right: y-simple region with horizontal slices (integrate dx then dy).

§5.2 — Double Integrals and Iterated Integrals

The double integral generalizes the definite integral to two dimensions. Just as a single integral sums \(f(x)\Delta x\) over an interval, a double integral sums \(f(x,y)\Delta A\) over a region in the plane.

Definition — Double Integral

Partition a region \(\mathscr{R}\) into small rectangles \(\mathscr{R}_{ij}\) of area \(\Delta A_{ij}\). Pick \((x_i,y_j)\in\mathscr{R}_{ij}\). If the limit exists:

Double Integral
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\lim_{\|\mathscr{P}\|\to 0}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\sum_{i=1}^{m}f(x_i,y_j)\,\Delta A_{ij}\]
dA — area element (dx dy or dy dx)‖𝒫‖ — mesh of partition → 0𝒬 — region of integration

Geometric Meaning

The double integral has two key geometric interpretations:

Volume: If \(f(x,y)\geq 0\), then \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f\,dA\) equals the volume of the solid that sits above the region \(\mathscr{R}\) in the \(xy\)-plane and below the surface \(z=f(x,y)\). Think of it as stacking infinitely many thin vertical columns of height \(f(x,y)\) and cross-sectional area \(dA\) — the integral adds up all their volumes.

Area: If \(f(x,y)=1\), then every column has height 1, so the integral just measures how much "floor space" there is: \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}1\,dA=\) area of \(\mathscr{R}\).

If \(f\) takes negative values, the integral counts volume below the \(xy\)-plane as negative — just like single-variable integrals count area below the \(x\)-axis as negative.

Fubini's Theorem

The definition of the double integral as a limit of Riemann sums is important for theory but useless for computation. Fubini's Theorem is the tool that makes double integrals computable: it says you can evaluate a double integral by doing two single-variable integrals, one inside the other — called an iterated integral.

Fubini's Theorem (Rectangles)

If \(f\) is continuous on a rectangle \(\mathscr{R}=\{(x,y):a\leq x\leq b,\;c\leq y\leq d\}\), then:

Fubini — Rectangular Region
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx=\int_c^d\!\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\]

For rectangles, the order of integration doesn't matter — you can integrate in either order and get the same answer. Pick whichever is easier to compute.

★ Why Fubini Works — The Intuition

Imagine computing the volume under \(z=f(x,y)\) by slicing the solid into thin vertical slabs perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis. Each slab at position \(x\) has a cross-sectional area of \(A(x)=\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\) (a single integral in \(y\), treating \(x\) as a constant). Then the total volume is \(\int_a^b A(x)\,dx\) — stack up all the slabs. That's exactly the iterated integral \(\int_a^b\int_c^d f\,dy\,dx\).

Fubini's Theorem (General Regions)

For non-rectangular regions, the limits on the inner integral become functions of the outer variable (from the §5.1 region descriptions):

Fubini — x-simple (vertical slices)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_{f_1(x)}^{f_2(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\]
Fubini — y-simple (horizontal slices)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f\,dA=\int_c^d\!\int_{g_1(y)}^{g_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\]

Key rule: the inner limits can depend on the outer variable, but the outer limits must be constants. If your outer limits contain a variable, your integral is set up incorrectly.

★ Anatomy of an Iterated Integral

In \(\int_a^b\int_{f_1(x)}^{f_2(x)}f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\):

• Inner integral: integrate w.r.t. \(y\) while holding \(x\) constant. Limits depend on \(x\).

• Outer integral: integrate the result w.r.t. \(x\). Limits are constants.

• Rule: the innermost differential (\(dy\)) matches the subscript order in \(\mathscr{R}_{yx}\).

Note: the \(3y\) term vanishes because \(y\) is odd on \([-1,1]\).

Example 5.3 — Double Integral with Variable Limits (Ex. 232)

Evaluate \(\int_0^{1/2}\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x}}2xy\,dy\,dx\).

Show Solution
1
Inner: \(\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x}}2xy\,dy=2x\cdot\frac{y^2}{2}\Big|_0^{\sqrt{1-x}}=x(1-x)\).
2
Outer: \(\int_0^{1/2}(x-x^2)\,dx=[\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^3}{3}]_0^{1/2}=\frac{1}{8}-\frac{1}{24}=\frac{1}{12}\).

Answer: \(\frac{1}{12}\)

Reversing the Order of Integration

Sometimes one order produces an integral you can't evaluate, but reversing the order makes it tractable. To reverse: sketch the region, then re-describe it using the other slice direction.

Example 5.4 — Reversing Integration Order (Ex. 247)

Evaluate \(\int_0^1\int_0^{\text{Arccos}\,y}\sin(\pi\sin x)\,dx\,dy\) by interchanging the order.

Show Solution

Original: horizontal slices, \(\mathscr{R}_{xy}=\{(x,y): 0\leq x\leq\text{Arccos}\,y,\;0\leq y\leq 1\}\).

Reverse: vertical slices. Since \(x\leq\text{Arccos}\,y\iff y\leq\cos x\), and \(x\in[0,\pi/2]\):

\(\mathscr{R}_{yx}=\{(x,y): 0\leq y\leq\cos x,\; 0\leq x\leq\pi/2\}\).

1
\(\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\cos x}\sin(\pi\sin x)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin(\pi\sin x)\cos x\,dx\).
2
Let \(u=\sin x\), \(du=\cos x\,dx\). Limits: \(0\to 1\). So \(=\int_0^1\sin(\pi u)\,du=-\frac{1}{\pi}\cos(\pi u)\Big|_0^1=\frac{2}{\pi}\).

Answer: \(\frac{2}{\pi}\)

§5.3 — Volume Under a Surface

Volume computation is a direct application of double integrals:

Volume Under a Surface
\[V=\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA\]
f(x,y) ≥ 0 — height of the surface above the xy-plane𝒬 — projection of the surface onto the xy-plane

For volume between two surfaces \(z=f(x,y)\) (top) and \(z=g(x,y)\) (bottom) over \(\mathscr{R}\):

\[V=\iint_{\mathscr{R}}[f(x,y)-g(x,y)]\,dA\]

Example 5.5 — Volume Under a Paraboloid (Ex. 248)

Find the volume of the solid under \(z=4-x^2-y^2\) and above the \(xy\)-plane.

Show Solution

\(z=0\) when \(x^2+y^2=4\), so \(\mathscr{R}\) is the disk of radius 2. Using vertical slices:

\(V=\int_{-2}^{2}\int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}(4-x^2-y^2)\,dy\,dx\)

This is much easier in polar coordinates (§5.4): \(V=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(4-r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta=2\pi\int_0^2(4r-r^3)\,dr=2\pi[2r^2-\frac{r^4}{4}]_0^2=2\pi(8-4)=8\pi\).

Answer: \(V=8\pi\)

§5.4 — Change of Variables / Polar Coordinates

Just as single-variable integrals use \(u\)-substitution, double integrals use a change of variables. The key extra ingredient: the Jacobian determinant, which accounts for how the transformation stretches or compresses area.

Jacobian Matrix and Determinant

For a transformation \(x=g(u,v),\;y=h(u,v)\), the Jacobian matrix is:

\[\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}&\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}\\\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}&\frac{\partial y}{\partial v}\end{pmatrix}\]

The Jacobian determinant \(J(u,v)=\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\) is the absolute value of the determinant of this matrix.

Change of Variables Formula
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\iint_{\mathscr{S}}f\!\big(g(u,v),h(u,v)\big)\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,dA\]
𝒬 — original region (xy-plane)𝒮 — transformed region (uv-plane)|∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)| — Jacobian (area scaling factor)

Polar Coordinates — The Most Important Special Case

For regions with circular symmetry, use \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\). The Jacobian is:

\[\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&\sin\theta\\-r\sin\theta&r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}=r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta=r\]

Double Integral in Polar Coordinates
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\iint_{\mathscr{S}}f(r\cos\theta,\,r\sin\theta)\;\boldsymbol{r}\,dr\,d\theta\]
r — the Jacobian (DON'T forget!)dA = r dr dθ — polar area elementx²+y² = r² — simplifies circular regions
★ When to Use Polar Coordinates

Switch to polar when:

• The region involves circles or arcs (\(x^2+y^2=R^2\))

• The integrand contains \(x^2+y^2\)

• The region is a sector, annulus, or cardioid

Common trap: forgetting the extra factor of \(\boldsymbol{r}\) in \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\)!

Fig 5.2 — The polar area element dA = r dr dθ. The extra factor of r comes from the arc length of the angular side: width = dr, height = r dθ.
Area in Polar Coordinates
\[\text{Area}(\mathscr{R})=\iint_{\mathscr{R}}r\,dr\,d\theta\]
Example 5.6 — Polar Double Integral (Ex. 253)

Evaluate \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}(x^2+y^2)\,dA\) where \(\mathscr{R}\) is the disk \(x^2+y^2\leq 4\).

Show Solution

In polar: \(x^2+y^2=r^2\), \(\mathscr{R}:\;0\leq r\leq 2,\;0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi\).

1
\(\iint r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\,dr\,d\theta\).
2
Inner: \(\int_0^2 r^3\,dr=\frac{r^4}{4}\Big|_0^2=4\). Outer: \(\int_0^{2\pi}4\,d\theta=8\pi\).

Answer: \(8\pi\)

Example 5.7 — Area of a Cardioid (Ex. 266)

Find the area enclosed by the cardioid \(r=2(1-\cos\theta)\).

Show Solution

\(\mathscr{R}=\{(r,\theta): 0\leq r\leq 2(1-\cos\theta),\; 0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi\}\).

1
\(A=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{2(1-\cos\theta)}r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{r^2}{2}\Big|_0^{2(1-\cos\theta)}d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}2(1-\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta\).
2
Expand: \(2(1-2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)=2-4\cos\theta+2\cos^2\theta=2-4\cos\theta+1+\cos 2\theta=3-4\cos\theta+\cos 2\theta\).
3
\(\int_0^{2\pi}(3-4\cos\theta+\cos 2\theta)\,d\theta=6\pi-0+0=6\pi\).

Surfaces in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) are sketched by computing their traces: the curves of intersection with coordinate planes (or planes parallel to them). Set one variable to a constant and identify the resulting 2D curve.

Trace (Cross-Section)

A trace of a surface \(f(x,y,z)=0\) on the plane \(z=c\) is the curve \(\{(x,y,c): f(x,y,c)=0\}\). Similarly for \(x=c\) or \(y=c\) traces. Sketch traces at several values of \(c\) and "stack" them to visualize the surface.

§5.6 — Parametric Equations of Surfaces

A curve is a 1D object, so one parameter \(t\) is enough to trace it out. A surface is a 2D object living in 3D space — you need to move in two independent directions to cover it. That means two parameters.

Think of it like an address system: on a curve, a single number \(t\) tells you where you are. On a surface, you need a pair \((u,v)\) — like latitude and longitude on Earth, or row and column on a grid.

Definition — Parametric Surface

A parametric surface is a vector-valued function of two variables:

Parametric Surface
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=\big(x(u,v),\;y(u,v),\;z(u,v)\big),\quad (u,v)\in D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^2\]
u, v — two independent parametersD — parameter domain (rectangle, disk, etc.)r(u,v) — position vector of a point on the surface

As \((u,v)\) ranges over the domain \(D\), the tip of \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\) traces out the surface. Fixing \(u=c\) and varying \(v\) gives a v-curve on the surface; fixing \(v=c\) and varying \(u\) gives a u-curve. Together these form a coordinate grid on the surface.

Common Parametrizations

1. Sphere of Radius R

To parametrize a sphere, use the same idea as geographic coordinates. Every point on the sphere is uniquely determined by two angles:

• \(u\) = longitude — the angle measured around the equator (like going east/west). Ranges over \([0,2\pi]\).

• \(v\) = latitude — the angle measured above or below the equator (like going north/south). Ranges over \([-\pi/2,\pi/2]\).

Sphere Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(R\cos v\cos u,\;R\cos v\sin u,\;R\sin v)\]
u ∈ [0, 2π] — longitude (around equator)v ∈ [−π/2, π/2] — latitude (pole to pole)R — radius

How to derive it: Start with a point \(P\) on the sphere. Drop it to the \(xy\)-plane: its projection \(Q\) is at distance \(R\cos v\) from the origin (the equatorial radius shrinks as you move toward the poles). In the \(xy\)-plane, \(Q\) makes angle \(u\) with the \(x\)-axis, so \(x=R\cos v\cos u\) and \(y=R\cos v\sin u\). The height is simply \(z=R\sin v\).

Fig 5.3 — Sphere parametrization. Point P is located by longitude u (green) and latitude v (pink). Its projection Q onto the equator is at distance R cos v from the center.
★ Why Not Spherical Coordinates?

This parametrization uses latitude \(v\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]\), not the physics convention of polar angle \(\phi\in[0,\pi]\) measured from the north pole. The textbook's convention matches geography: \(v=0\) is the equator, \(v=\pi/2\) is the north pole, \(v=-\pi/2\) is the south pole.

2. Graph Surface \(z=f(x,y)\)

If the surface is already given as \(z=f(x,y)\) — i.e., you can solve for \(z\) explicitly — then the parametrization is trivial: just let \(u=x\) and \(v=y\).

Graph Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(u,\,v,\,f(u,v)),\quad (u,v)\in\mathscr{R}\]

The parameter domain \(\mathscr{R}\) is just the region in the \(xy\)-plane over which the surface is defined. This is the simplest possible parametrization — you're using \(x\) and \(y\) themselves as the two parameters.

Example: The paraboloid \(z=x^2+y^2\) over the unit disk becomes \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(u,v,u^2+v^2)\) for \(u^2+v^2\leq 1\).

3. Surface of Revolution

Take a profile curve \(y=g(x)\geq 0\) in the \(xy\)-plane and spin it around the \(x\)-axis through a full \(2\pi\) rotation. Each point \((x,g(x),0)\) traces out a circle of radius \(g(x)\) centered on the \(x\)-axis.

Revolution Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=\big(u,\;g(u)\cos v,\;g(u)\sin v\big)\]
u ∈ [a, b] — position along the axisv ∈ [0, 2π] — rotation angle around the axisg(u) — radius of the cross-sectional circle

How it works: Parameter \(u\) slides along the \(x\)-axis (controlling which cross-section you're at). Parameter \(v\) rotates around the axis (controlling where on that circle you are). The radius of each circle is \(g(u)\) — determined by the profile curve.

Fig 5.4 — Surface of revolution. The profile curve y = g(x) is spun around the x-axis. Each blue ellipse is a cross-section whose radius matches the profile height g(u) at that position.

Example: Revolving \(y=\sqrt{x}\) for \(x\in[0,4]\) gives a trumpet-shaped surface: \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(u,\sqrt{u}\cos v,\sqrt{u}\sin v)\), and the cross-sections are circles of radius \(\sqrt{u}\) that grow as \(u\) increases.

★ How to Choose a Parametrization

Given as z = f(x,y)? → Use the graph parametrization. Simplest option.

Sphere or piece of a sphere? → Use latitude/longitude (the sphere parametrization above).

Cylinder \(x^2+y^2=R^2\)? → Let \(x=R\cos\theta,\;y=R\sin\theta\), use \(z\) as the second parameter.

Surface of revolution? → Identify the profile curve and axis of rotation, then apply the formula.

General rule: look for the natural "grid" on the surface — the two families of curves that cover it.

Example 5.9 — Parametrizing a Cylinder (Ex. 282)

Find a parametric representation of the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=4\) for \(0\leq z\leq 3\).

Show Solution

Thought process: The cylinder has circular cross-sections of radius 2. At any fixed height \(z\), the circle \(x^2+y^2=4\) is parametrized by the angle \(\theta\). That gives us one parameter. The second parameter is just \(z\) itself — it slides up and down the cylinder.

1
Circle at height \(z\): \(x=2\cos\theta,\;y=2\sin\theta\).
2
Let \(z\) be the second parameter (it ranges independently of \(\theta\)).

Answer: \(\mathbf{r}(\theta,z)=(2\cos\theta,\;2\sin\theta,\;z),\quad \theta\in[0,2\pi],\;z\in[0,3]\)

★ Tip: The \(\theta\)-curves (fix \(z\)) are horizontal circles. The \(z\)-curves (fix \(\theta\)) are vertical lines — the "rulings" of the cylinder.

Magnitude
\[|\vec{v}|=\sqrt{\htmlData{t=v₁ — x-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_1}}^2+\htmlData{t=v₂ — y-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_2}}^2+\htmlData{t=v₃ — z-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_3}}^2}\]

The magnitude (length) of a vector tells you how long the arrow is. For v = ⟨3, 4⟩, form a right triangle with legs 3 and 4: the hypotenuse is √(9+16) = 5. In 3D, add the third component squared. Magnitude is always non-negative; only the zero vector has magnitude zero.

Where it comes from: Pythagorean theorem extended to 3D. Also, |v|² = v·v (dot product with itself).

✓ Use when asked to find length/magnitude, normalize a vector (step 1), or find distance between two points.
✗ Not for finding direction — magnitude gives only size.
Length of a vector — always ≥ 0. Use for normalizing, distances.
Unit Vector
\[\hat{u}=\frac{\htmlData{t=v — the vector being normalized}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}}{\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of v (must be nonzero)}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}}\]

A unit vector has length exactly 1 — it keeps the direction but discards the size. Divide any nonzero vector by its magnitude: û = v/|v|. The standard basis î, ĵ, k̂ are unit vectors along the axes. Any vector can be written as magnitude × direction: v = |v| · û.

Where it comes from: Scalar multiplication by 1/|v| scales the arrow to length 1 without changing direction. Undefined for the zero vector (division by zero).

✓ Use when asked to "normalize" or "find the direction of." Essential for projections and directional derivatives.
✗ Not for the zero vector. Don't confuse normalizing with making components positive.
Direction only, length 1. Undefined for the zero vector.
Dot Product
\[\htmlData{t=v — first vector}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\cdot\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}}=\htmlData{t=v₁ — x-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_1}}\htmlData{t=w₁ — x-component of w}{\color{#0B7D7D}{w_1}}+\htmlData{t=v₂ — y-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_2}}\htmlData{t=w₂ — y-component of w}{\color{#0B7D7D}{w_2}}+\htmlData{t=v₃ — z-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_3}}\htmlData{t=w₃ — z-component of w}{\color{#0B7D7D}{w_3}}=\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of v}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}\,\htmlData{t=|w| — magnitude of w}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{w}|}}\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle between v and w}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}\]

The dot product takes two vectors and produces a scalar (NOT a vector). Two equivalent forms: algebraic (v₁w₁ + v₂w₂ + v₃w₃) and geometric (|v||w|cosθ). The sign reveals alignment: positive = same direction (θ < 90°), zero = perpendicular, negative = opposing (θ > 90°).

Where it comes from: Law of cosines: |v−w|² = |v|²+|w|²−2|v||w|cosθ. Expand the left side with components and the algebraic form emerges.

✓ Use when finding angles between vectors, testing perpendicularity (dot = 0), computing projections, or calculating work (W = F·d).
✗ Not for finding a vector ⊥ to two others — that's the cross product. Dot = scalar, cross = vector.
Scalar measuring alignment. Positive = same dir, zero = ⊥, negative = opposing.
Angle Between Vectors
\[\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle between v and w (0 to π)}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}=\frac{\htmlData{t=v — first vector}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\cdot\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}}}{\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of first vector}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}\,\htmlData{t=|w| — magnitude of second vector}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{w}|}}}\]

Rearrange the geometric dot product to isolate the angle: cosθ = (v·w)/(|v||w|). Steps: (1) compute v·w, (2) compute |v| and |w|, (3) divide, (4) take arccos. Result is always in [0, π]. cosθ = 0 → perpendicular, cosθ = 1 → parallel same direction, cosθ = −1 → parallel opposite.

Where it comes from: From v·w = |v||w|cosθ, divide both sides by |v||w|.

✓ Use when you have two vectors as components and need the angle. Standard 4-step method tested on most exams.
✗ Not for inclination angles of a single vector with an axis — use direction cosines for that.
Isolate θ from the dot product. Three-step: dot → magnitudes → arccos.
Direction Cosines
\[\cos\htmlData{t=α — angle v makes with x-axis}{\color{#A82870}{\alpha}}=\frac{\htmlData{t=a — x-component of v}{\color{#5D7A20}{a}}}{\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of v}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}},\;\cos\htmlData{t=β — angle v makes with y-axis}{\color{#A82870}{\beta}}=\frac{\htmlData{t=b — y-component of v}{\color{#5D7A20}{b}}}{\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of v}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}},\;\cos\htmlData{t=γ — angle v makes with z-axis}{\color{#A82870}{\gamma}}=\frac{\htmlData{t=c — z-component of v}{\color{#5D7A20}{c}}}{\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of v}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}}\]

Direction cosines are the cosines of the angles a vector makes with the x, y, z axes: cosα = a/|v|, cosβ = b/|v|, cosγ = c/|v|. These are simply the components of the unit vector. The identity cos²α + cos²β + cos²γ = 1 always holds. Reconstruct any vector: v = |v|·(cosα, cosβ, cosγ).

Where it comes from: Each direction cosine is the dot product of û with the corresponding basis vector: cosα = û·î = a/|v|.

✓ Use when given angles with axes and asked to find the vector, or asked what angle a vector makes with an axis.
✗ Not for 2D angles — in 2D, just use θ = arctan(v₂/v₁).
Angles with axes. Reconstruct: v = r(cosα,cosβ,cosγ).
Vector Projection
\[\text{proj}_{\htmlData{t=v — the direction being projected onto}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{v}}}}\,\htmlData{t=u — the vector being projected}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{u}}}=\frac{\htmlData{t=u — vector being projected}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{u}}}\cdot\htmlData{t=v — projection direction}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{v}}}}{\htmlData{t=|v|² — squared magnitude of v}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|^2}}}\;\htmlData{t=v — multiply by direction vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{v}}}\]

Vector projection finds the piece of u that lies along v — like a shadow cast onto v. Two versions: scalar projection comp_v u = u·v/|v| (a number, positive if same direction), and vector projection proj_v u = (u·v/|v|²)v (a vector parallel to v). The leftover u − proj_v u is perpendicular to v.

Where it comes from: We want the closest point on the line through v to the tip of u. Perpendicularity condition (u − tv) ⊥ v gives t = u·v/|v|².

✓ Use when asked to project, find a component along a direction, or decompose a force into parallel and perpendicular parts.
✗ Not for the perpendicular component directly — get it by subtracting: u_perp = u − proj_v u.
Shadow of u onto v. Scalar version: u·v/|v|. Vector version: (u·v/|v|²)v.
Work
\[\htmlData{t=W — work done (scalar quantity)}{\color{#5D7A20}{W}}=\htmlData{t=F — force vector}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{F}}}\cdot\htmlData{t=d — displacement vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{d}}}=\htmlData{t=|F| — magnitude of force}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{F}|}}\,\htmlData{t=|d| — magnitude of displacement}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{d}|}}\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle between force and displacement}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}\]

Work measures energy transferred by a force over a displacement: W = F·d = |F||d|cosθ. Only the component of force along the direction of motion contributes. Pushing forward → max work. Pushing perpendicular → zero work. Pushing against motion → negative work (like friction).

Where it comes from: Work = (force component along displacement) × distance = |F|cosθ · |d| = F·d.

✓ Use when computing work done by a constant force over a displacement.
✗ Not for torque — that uses the cross product: τ = r × F.
Work = F·d. Only the component of force along displacement matters.
Cross Product
\[\htmlData{t=v — first vector}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\times\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\\htmlData{t=v₁ — x-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_1}}&\htmlData{t=v₂ — y-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_2}}&\htmlData{t=v₃ — z-component of v}{\color{#D45D00}{v_3}}\\\htmlData{t=w₁ — x-component of w}{\color{#0B7D7D}{w_1}}&\htmlData{t=w₂ — y-component of w}{\color{#0B7D7D}{w_2}}&\htmlData{t=w₃ — z-component of w}{\color{#0B7D7D}{w_3}}\end{vmatrix}\]

The cross product produces a vector perpendicular to both inputs. Compute via a 3×3 determinant with î, ĵ, k̂ in row 1, v's components in row 2, w's in row 3. The direction follows the right-hand rule. Order matters: v×w = −(w×v). Magnitude |v×w| = parallelogram area. v×w = 0 means v and w are parallel.

Where it comes from: The determinant is constructed so the result is perpendicular to both input vectors (cofactor expansion along row 1).

✓ Use when you need a normal vector to a plane, parallelogram area, or to test if vectors are parallel (cross = 0).
✗ Not for testing perpendicularity (use dot = 0). Cross product only works in 3D and is NOT commutative.
Vector ⊥ to both. |v×w| = parallelogram area. Right-hand rule for direction.
Cross Product — Magnitude
\[|\htmlData{t=v — first vector}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\times\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}}|=\htmlData{t=|v| — magnitude of v}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{v}|}}\,\htmlData{t=|w| — magnitude of w}{\color{#2050A0}{|\vec{w}|}}\sin\htmlData{t=θ — angle between v and w}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}\]

The magnitude of the cross product equals the area of the parallelogram spanned by the two vectors: |v×w| = |v||w|sinθ. When parallel (θ = 0), sinθ = 0 so the area collapses to zero. When perpendicular (θ = 90°), the area is maximized at |v||w|.

Where it comes from: Parallelogram base = |v|, height = |w|sinθ. Area = base × height.

✓ Use when you need the parallelogram area or the length of the cross product without computing the full determinant.
✗ Not for finding the direction of v×w — this gives magnitude only.
|v×w| = parallelogram area = |v||w|sinθ.
Triangle Area
\[\text{Area}=\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\htmlData{t=u — first edge from shared vertex}{\color{#5D7A20}{\vec{u}}}\times\htmlData{t=v — second edge from shared vertex}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}|\]

A triangle formed by two vectors from a shared vertex is half the parallelogram they span: Area = ½|u×v|. Given three points A, B, C, form edge vectors AB = B−A and AC = C−A from one vertex, then compute ½|AB × AC|. Works in 2D and 3D without finding base and height separately.

Where it comes from: A diagonal cuts a parallelogram into two congruent triangles, each with half the area.

✓ Use when you have 3 points or two edge vectors and need the triangle area.
✗ Not for simple 2D cases where ½|base × height| is faster. Both vectors must share a vertex.
Half the parallelogram area: ½|u×v|.
Scalar Triple Product (Volume)
\[V=|\htmlData{t=u — height direction vector}{\color{#5D7A20}{\vec{u}}}\cdot(\htmlData{t=v — first base edge}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\times\htmlData{t=w — second base edge}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}})|\]

The scalar triple product u·(v×w) computes the volume of the parallelepiped (3D box) formed by three vectors. First v×w gives a vector with magnitude = base area, then dotting with u gives the height. Take absolute value for volume. If the triple product is zero, the vectors are coplanar (the box is flat). Equals the 3×3 determinant with u, v, w as rows.

Where it comes from: Volume = base area × height = |v×w| × |projection of u onto v×w direction|.

✓ Use when finding parallelepiped volume or testing coplanarity (triple product = 0). Tetrahedron volume = ⅙|u·(v×w)|.
✗ Not for tetrahedra directly — divide by 6.
|u·(v×w)| = parallelepiped volume. = 0 ⟺ coplanar.
Quick Geometry Tests
\[\text{Perp: }\htmlData{t=v — first vector (perp test)}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\cdot\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}}=0\quad\text{Parallel: }\htmlData{t=v — first vector (parallel test)}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\times\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}}=\mathbf{0}\quad\text{Coplanar: }\htmlData{t=u — third vector (coplanar test)}{\color{#5D7A20}{\vec{u}}}\cdot(\htmlData{t=v — first vector}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{v}}}\times\htmlData{t=w — second vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{w}}})=0\]

Three quick yes/no tests: (1) Perpendicular? v·w = 0 (dot product, since cos90° = 0). (2) Parallel? v×w = 0 (cross product, since sin0° = 0; equivalently, one is a scalar multiple of the other). (3) Coplanar? u·(v×w) = 0 (triple product = 0 means the box is flat). Memory trick: "⊥ uses DOT, ∥ uses CROSS" — counterintuitive but correct.

✓ Use when asked "are these vectors perpendicular / parallel / coplanar?"
✗ Not for mixing up the tests. ⊥ → dot = 0, ∥ → cross = 0, coplanar → triple = 0.
Three yes/no tests: dot=0 (⊥), cross=0 (∥), triple=0 (coplanar).

Chapter 2 — Lines, Planes & Parametric Curves

Line (Parametric)
\[\vec{r}(t)=\htmlData{t=P — a known point on the line}{\color{#D45D00}{P}}+\htmlData{t=t — parameter (real number)}{\color{#5D7A20}{t}}\,\htmlData{t=v⃗ — direction vector parallel to the line}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{v}}}\]

A parametric line needs a point P and direction v. As t varies, r(t) = P + tv traces the line. At t=0 you're at P, at t=1 you're at P+v. In components: x = p₁+tv₁, y = p₂+tv₂, z = p₃+tv₃. Line through two points P, Q: direction = Q−P, giving r(t) = (1−t)P + tQ (at t=0 you're at P, at t=1 you're at Q).

Where it comes from: "Start at P, go in direction v" — the parameter t scales how far.

✓ Use when given a point + direction or two points. Restrict t ∈ [0,1] for a segment. Two lines → set r₁(t) = r₂(s) to find intersections.
✗ Not for planes — planes use a normal vector, not a direction vector.
Point + direction vector → parametric line. t ∈ ℝ for full line, [0,1] for segment.
Plane Equation
\[\htmlData{t=a — x-component of normal}{\color{#D45D00}{a}}x+\htmlData{t=b — y-component of normal}{\color{#D45D00}{b}}y+\htmlData{t=c — z-component of normal}{\color{#D45D00}{c}}z=\htmlData{t=d — constant found by substituting a known point}{\color{#5D7A20}{d}}\]

A plane is defined by a normal vector n = (a,b,c) and a point. Equation: ax+by+cz = d, where d is found by substituting a known point. To find a plane through 3 points P, Q, R: (1) form PQ and PR, (2) cross them: n = PQ × PR, (3) use n's components and substitute one point to find d.

Where it comes from: "Point X is on the plane" means n·(X−P) = 0. Expanding gives ax+by+cz = n·P = d.

✓ Use when given 3 points or a point + normal. For two-plane intersection: cross normals → direction of line.
✗ Not for lines — lines use direction vectors, not normal vectors.
Normal vector determines the plane. Cross product of two in-plane vectors gives it.
Parametric Derivatives
\[\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\htmlData{t=dy/dt — derivative of y(t) w.r.t. parameter}{\color{#0B7D7D}{dy/dt}}}{\htmlData{t=dx/dt — derivative of x(t) w.r.t. parameter}{\color{#D45D00}{dx/dt}}},\quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)}{\htmlData{t=dx/dt — must be nonzero}{\color{#D45D00}{dx/dt}}}\]

Parametric slope: dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) via the chain rule. Three cases: horizontal tangent when dy/dt = 0 (dx/dt ≠ 0), vertical tangent when dx/dt = 0 (dy/dt ≠ 0), both zero → cusp or corner. Second derivative: d²y/dx² = [d/dt(dy/dx)]/(dx/dt). This is NOT (d²y/dt²)/(d²x/dt²) — the #1 exam mistake.

Where it comes from: Chain rule: dy/dx = (dy/dt)·(dt/dx) = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt). Second derivative requires differentiating the first derivative w.r.t. t, then dividing by dx/dt again.

✓ Use when finding slopes, tangent lines, or concavity for parametric curves.
✗ Not for curves given as y = f(x) — just use regular differentiation.
Slope = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt). Second derivative: differentiate slope w.r.t. t, then divide by dx/dt.
Arc Length (Parametric)
\[L=\int_{\htmlData{t=α — start of parameter range}{\color{#A82870}{\alpha}}}^{\htmlData{t=β — end of parameter range}{\color{#A82870}{\beta}}}\sqrt{\left[\htmlData{t=x'(t)}{\color{#D45D00}{x'(t)}}\right]^2+\left[\htmlData{t=y'(t)}{\color{#0B7D7D}{y'(t)}}\right]^2}\,dt\]

Arc length measures distance along a curve. Chop into tiny pieces ds = √(dx²+dy²), substitute dx = x'dt, dy = y'dt, and integrate: L = ∫√((x')²+(y')²) dt. The integrand is the speed — how fast the point moves. For y = f(x), set x = t to get L = ∫√(1+(dy/dx)²) dx. Key results: cycloid arch = 8r, circle = 2πR.

Where it comes from: Pythagorean theorem on each infinitesimal piece: ds² = dx² + dy². Factor out dt².

✓ Use when asked for curve length. Ensure the curve is traced exactly once (no backtracking).
✗ Not for polar curves — use ∫√((r')²+r²) dθ instead.
Length = ∫ speed dt. Cycloid arch = 8r.
Polar Area
\[A=\int_{\htmlData{t=α — starting angle}{\color{#A82870}{\alpha}}}^{\htmlData{t=β — ending angle}{\color{#A82870}{\beta}}}\frac{1}{2}\htmlData{t=r — polar radius function}{\color{#D45D00}{r}}^2\,d\theta\]

In polar coordinates, area uses sectors (pie slices), not rectangles. A thin sector at radius r spanning dθ has area dA = ½r²dθ. Integrate: A = ½∫r² dθ. Between two curves: ½∫(r_out² − r_in²) dθ. Always find intersections first for bounds — some only show up when r = 0 at different θ values.

Where it comes from: Circular sector of radius r and angle Δθ has area ½r²Δθ. Sum infinitesimal sectors → integral.

✓ Use when finding area enclosed by a polar curve or between two polar curves. Sketch first.
✗ Not for Cartesian regions. Don't forget the ½ in front.
½∫r² dθ. Between curves: ½∫(r_out² − r_in²) dθ.
Polar Arc Length
\[L=\int_{\htmlData{t=α — starting angle}{\color{#A82870}{\alpha}}}^{\htmlData{t=β — ending angle}{\color{#A82870}{\beta}}}\sqrt{\htmlData{t=r' — derivative dr/dθ}{\color{#0B7D7D}{(r'_\theta)}}^2+\htmlData{t=r — polar radius}{\color{#D45D00}{r}}^2}\;d\theta\]

Polar arc length measures distance along a polar curve r = f(θ). The formula parallels the parametric version but uses r and r' = dr/dθ instead of x' and y'. The integrand √(r² + (r')²) represents the speed along the curve in polar coordinates. Cardioid r = 1+cosθ has total perimeter 8.

Where it comes from: Start with x = rcosθ, y = rsinθ. Compute x'(θ) and y'(θ) via product rule, plug into √((x')²+(y')²), and simplify using cos²+sin² = 1.

✓ Use when finding the length of a polar curve. The identity 1+cosθ = 2cos²(θ/2) often simplifies cardioid integrals.
✗ Not for parametric curves — use ∫√((x')²+(y')²) dt directly.
√(r'²+r²) dθ. Simpler than converting to Cartesian.
Line Segment
\[\vec{r}(t)=(1-\htmlData{t=t — parameter from 0 to 1}{\color{#5D7A20}{t}})\,\htmlData{t=P — start point}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{P}}}+\htmlData{t=t — parameter}{\color{#5D7A20}{t}}\,\htmlData{t=Q — end point}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{Q}}},\quad 0\le t\le 1\]

A line segment from P to Q is the parametric line r(t) = (1−t)P + tQ restricted to t ∈ [0,1]. At t=0 you're at P, at t=1 you're at Q. Values outside [0,1] extend the line past the endpoints. This is a convex combination of P and Q — every point on the segment is a weighted average.

Where it comes from: Rewrite P + t(Q−P) = (1−t)P + tQ. The parameter t gives the fraction of the way from P to Q.

✓ Use when parameterizing a segment for line integrals (Ch 4) or finding midpoints (t=½).
✗ Not for infinite lines — remove the t ∈ [0,1] restriction for those.
Weighted average of two endpoints. t=½ gives midpoint.
Angle Between Planes
\[\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle between the planes}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}=\frac{\htmlData{t=n⃗₁ — normal to first plane}{\color{#D45D00}{\vec{n}_1}}\cdot\htmlData{t=n⃗₂ — normal to second plane}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\vec{n}_2}}}{|\vec{n}_1|\,|\vec{n}_2|}\]

The angle between two planes is the angle between their normal vectors: cosθ = |n₁·n₂|/(|n₁||n₂|). Use absolute value in the numerator because plane normals can point either way — we want the acute angle between the planes. Parallel planes: n₁ × n₂ = 0. Perpendicular planes: n₁ · n₂ = 0.

Where it comes from: The dihedral angle between two planes equals the angle between their normals (or its supplement — absolute value picks the acute one).

✓ Use when finding the angle between two planes, or checking if planes are parallel/perpendicular.
✗ Not for the angle between a line and a plane — that uses sinα = |v·n|/(|v||n|) instead.
Angle between planes is the angle between their normal vectors.
Rotation Matrices
\[\text{Active: }A=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\htmlData{t=θ — CCW rotation angle}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\quad\text{Passive: }A^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&\sin\theta\\-\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\]

Active rotation rotates points by angle θ counterclockwise: A = [cosθ, −sinθ; sinθ, cosθ]. Passive rotation (rotating axes instead) uses the transpose: Aᵀ = [cosθ, sinθ; −sinθ, cosθ]. Combined with translation (x,y) → (x−h, y−k), you can handle rotated conics and coordinate changes.

Where it comes from: Apply trigonometric addition formulas to the rotated coordinates. The columns of A are the images of î and ĵ.

✓ Use when rotating points, eliminating the Bxy cross-term in a conic (rotate by cot2θ = (A−C)/B), or converting between rotated coordinate systems.
✗ Not for 3D rotations — those require 3×3 matrices and rotation axis specification.
Active: rotate point. Passive: rotate axes (flip sin signs). Translation first, then rotate.
Parametric Area
\[A=\int_{\htmlData{t=α — start parameter}{\color{#A82870}{\alpha}}}^{\htmlData{t=β — end parameter}{\color{#A82870}{\beta}}}\htmlData{t=y(t) — height function}{\color{#0B7D7D}{y(t)}}\,\htmlData{t=x'(t) — derivative of x-component}{\color{#D45D00}{x'(t)}}\,dt\]

Parametric area under (or enclosed by) a parametric curve comes from substituting dx = x'(t)dt into ∫y dx, giving A = ∫y(t)·x'(t) dt. For a closed curve, use |∫g(t)·f'(t) dt|. Key results: cycloid arch area = 3πr², ellipse area = πab.

Where it comes from: Substitution in ∫y dx: replace dx with x'(t)dt, change bounds to t-values.

✓ Use when finding area under or enclosed by a parametric curve. Watch bounds — they're in t, not x.
✗ Not for polar curves — those use ½∫r² dθ. Don't mix the formulas.
Replace dx → x'(t)dt. Cycloid arch gives 3πr². Ellipse gives πab.
Polar ↔ Cartesian
\[\htmlData{t=x — Cartesian horizontal}{\color{#D45D00}{x}}=\htmlData{t=r — distance from origin}{\color{#5D7A20}{r}}\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle from positive x-axis}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}},\quad \htmlData{t=y — Cartesian vertical}{\color{#0B7D7D}{y}}=r\sin\theta;\quad r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\]

Polar coordinates describe a point by distance r from the origin and angle θ from the positive x-axis. Convert: x = rcosθ, y = rsinθ. Reverse: r = √(x²+y²), θ = arctan(y/x) — but check the quadrant, since arctan only returns (−π/2, π/2). Convention: (−r, θ) = (r, θ+π).

Where it comes from: Right triangle trigonometry: horizontal leg = rcosθ, vertical leg = rsinθ. Pythagoras gives r = √(x²+y²).

✓ Use when converting between coordinate systems or simplifying equations with circular symmetry (x²+y² = r²).
✗ Not for 3D — use cylindrical (r,θ,z) or spherical (ρ,θ,φ).
Polar→Cart: x=rcosθ, y=rsinθ. Cart→Polar: r=√(x²+y²), check quadrant for θ.
Polar Slope
\[\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\htmlData{t=r' — derivative dr/dθ}{\color{#5D7A20}{r'}}\sin\htmlData{t=θ — polar angle}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}+\htmlData{t=r — polar radius}{\color{#D45D00}{r}}\cos\theta}{\htmlData{t=r' — derivative dr/dθ}{\color{#5D7A20}{r'}}\cos\theta-\htmlData{t=r — polar radius}{\color{#D45D00}{r}}\sin\theta}\]

The slope of a polar curve r = f(θ) is NOT dr/dθ. Since x = rcosθ and y = rsinθ, use the parametric formula with θ as parameter: dy/dx = (r'sinθ + rcosθ)/(r'cosθ − rsinθ). After finding the slope, convert (r,θ) to Cartesian (x,y) for the tangent line equation.

Where it comes from: Product rule on x = rcosθ and y = rsinθ, then (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ).

✓ Use when finding the tangent line to a polar curve. Always convert to Cartesian for the final line equation.
✗ Not for parametric curves x(t), y(t) — use (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) directly.
Slope of polar curve. Don't forget to convert (r,θ) → (x,y) for the line equation.
Conic Discriminant
\[\htmlData{t=B² − 4AC — the conic discriminant}{\color{#D45D00}{B^2-4AC}}:\quad {\lt}0\;\text{ellipse},\quad {\gt}0\;\text{hyperbola},\quad {=}0\;\text{parabola}\]

The conic discriminant B²−4AC classifies the general second-degree equation Ax²+Bxy+Cy²+Dx+Ey+F = 0. Negative → ellipse (or circle if A=C, B=0). Zero → parabola. Positive → hyperbola. When B ≠ 0 (cross-term), the conic is rotated; eliminate it by rotating axes by θ where cot2θ = (A−C)/B.

Where it comes from: B²−4AC is invariant under rotation — a deep result from linear algebra.

✓ Use when asked to classify a conic. Just compute B²−4AC and check the sign.
✗ Not for finding the standard form — that requires completing the square or rotating axes.
B²−4AC classifies: negative→ellipse, positive→hyperbola, zero→parabola.

Ch 3 — Partial Derivatives & Vector Fields

Partial Derivative (Definition)
\[\frac{\partial \htmlData{t=f — the multivariable function}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}}{\partial \htmlData{t=x — variable being differentiated}{\color{#D45D00}{x}}}(\htmlData{t=x₀ — x-coordinate of the point}{\color{#D45D00}{x_0}},\htmlData{t=y₀ — y-coordinate held constant}{\color{#5D7A20}{y_0}})=\lim_{\htmlData{t=h — small increment in x}{\color{#A82870}{h}}\to 0}\frac{f(x_0+h,y_0)-f(x_0,y_0)}{h}\]

Partial derivatives measure the rate of change of a multivariable function when you vary one variable and hold all others constant. For ∂f/∂x, treat y as a constant and differentiate with respect to x using all the usual single-variable rules. The geometric meaning: ∂f/∂x is the slope of the curve you get by slicing the surface z = f(x,y) with the plane y = y₀.

Where it comes from: Same definition as the ordinary derivative, but applied to one variable at a time while freezing the others.

✓ Use when asked to find ∂f/∂x or fₓ of any function of two or more variables.
✗ Not for implicit differentiation (§3.6.2) — that's a different procedure where z depends on x,y through an equation f(x,y,z)=0.
Treat all other variables as constants and differentiate normally.
Clairaut's Theorem
\[\htmlData{t=fxy — diff w.r.t. x then y}{\color{#D45D00}{f_{xy}}}=\htmlData{t=fyx — diff w.r.t. y then x}{\color{#5D7A20}{f_{yx}}}\]

Mixed partial derivatives are equal when f and its partials up to order 2 are continuous. The order of differentiation doesn't matter: differentiating first by x then y gives the same result as y then x.

Where it comes from: Proved using the limit definition and the mean value theorem. The continuity requirement is essential — without it, counterexamples exist.

✓ Use when computing mixed partials — you can choose the easier order. Also used to prove identities like curl(∇f) = 0.
✗ Not for functions with discontinuous second partials — check continuity first.
Order of mixed partials doesn't matter (when continuous).
Directional Derivative
\[\htmlData{t=Dᵤf — directional derivative}{\color{#D45D00}{D_{\mathbf{u}}f(P)}}=\htmlData{t=∇f — gradient vector}{\color{#5D7A20}{\nabla f(P)}}\cdot\htmlData{t=u — direction vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\mathbf{u}}}=\htmlData{t=|∇f| — gradient magnitude}{\color{#5D7A20}{|\nabla f|}}\,\htmlData{t=|u| — length of u}{\color{#0B7D7D}{|\mathbf{u}|}}\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle between ∇f and u}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}\]

The directional derivative gives the rate of change of f in any direction, not just along the axes. Compute the gradient ∇f = (fₓ, f_y), then dot it with the direction vector u. When u is a unit vector, this gives the rate of change per unit distance. The formula D = ∇f · u = |∇f||u|cosθ shows the directional derivative depends on the angle between the gradient and the direction.

Where it comes from: Expand the limit definition of D_u f using the linearity of derivatives. The result factors as the dot product of the gradient with u.

✓ Use when asked for the rate of change in a specific direction, or finding the direction of maximum/minimum change.
✗ Not for finding the derivative along a curved path — that needs the chain rule with parametric curves.
Rate of change in direction u. Dot the gradient with u.
Gradient — Direction of Steepest Ascent
\[\max \htmlData{t=Dᵤf — directional derivative}{\color{#D45D00}{D_{\mathbf{u}}f}}=\htmlData{t=|∇f| — max rate of change}{\color{#5D7A20}{|\nabla f|}}\;\text{ in direction }\;\frac{\htmlData{t=∇f — gradient vector}{\color{#5D7A20}{\nabla f}}}{\htmlData{t=|∇f| — gradient magnitude}{\color{#5D7A20}{|\nabla f|}}}\]

The function f increases fastest in the direction of the gradient ∇f, and the rate of that fastest increase equals the magnitude |∇f|. It decreases fastest in the direction −∇f. Perpendicular to the gradient (along level curves), the directional derivative is zero.

Where it comes from: D_u f = |∇f|cosθ is maximized when cosθ = 1 (u parallel to ∇f) and minimized when cosθ = −1.

✓ Use when asked "in which direction does f increase fastest?" or "what is the maximum rate of change?"
✗ Not for finding where f itself is maximum — that's optimization (Ch 7). The gradient gives direction of increase, not the location of extrema.
Max rate of change = |∇f|, in direction of ∇f. Zero along level curves.
Chain Rule (One Parameter)
\[\frac{d\htmlData{t=z — the output variable}{\color{#0B7D7D}{z}}}{d\htmlData{t=t — parameter that x and y depend on}{\color{#A82870}{t}}}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial \htmlData{t=x — first intermediate variable}{\color{#D45D00}{x}}}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{\partial z}{\partial \htmlData{t=y — second intermediate variable}{\color{#5D7A20}{y}}}\frac{dy}{dt}\]

When z = f(x,y) and both x, y depend on a parameter t, the total derivative dz/dt combines both paths of influence. Each term is a product: the partial of z w.r.t. an intermediate variable times that variable's derivative w.r.t. t. Add all such terms. For three intermediate variables, add a third term (∂z/∂w · dw/dt).

Where it comes from: The gradient form d/dt f(r(t)) = ∇f · r'(t), expanded into components.

✓ Use when z depends on x,y which themselves depend on t. Also for related rates in multivariable settings.
✗ Not for cases where x,y are independent — then just compute partial derivatives directly.
Sum ∂z/∂(each var) × d(var)/dt over all intermediate variables.
Implicit Partial Derivatives
\[\frac{\partial \htmlData{t=z — the dependent variable}{\color{#0B7D7D}{z}}}{\partial \htmlData{t=x — first independent variable}{\color{#D45D00}{x}}}=-\frac{\htmlData{t=fₓ — partial of f w.r.t. x}{\color{#D45D00}{f_x}}}{\htmlData{t=fz — partial of f w.r.t. z (nonzero)}{\color{#0B7D7D}{f_z}}},\qquad\frac{\partial z}{\partial \htmlData{t=y — second independent variable}{\color{#5D7A20}{y}}}=-\frac{\htmlData{t=fy — partial of f w.r.t. y}{\color{#5D7A20}{f_y}}}{f_z}\]

When a surface is defined implicitly by f(x,y,z) = 0 and you can't solve for z explicitly, these formulas give the partial derivatives. Differentiate f = 0 with respect to x (treating z as a function of x,y), then solve for ∂z/∂x.

Where it comes from: Apply the chain rule to f(x,y,z(x,y)) = 0: fₓ + f_z · ∂z/∂x = 0, then solve.

✓ Use when the surface equation f(x,y,z)=0 can't easily be solved for z. Quick shortcut: just compute the three partials of f and take ratios.
✗ Not for cases where f_z = 0 at the point — the formula breaks down (z can't be expressed as a function of x,y near that point).
∂z/∂x = −fₓ/f_z. Requires f_z ≠ 0.
Tangent Plane
\[\htmlData{t=fₓ — partial w.r.t. x at P}{\color{#D45D00}{f_x(P)}}(x-x_0)+\htmlData{t=fy — partial w.r.t. y at P}{\color{#5D7A20}{f_y(P)}}(y-y_0)+\htmlData{t=fz — partial w.r.t. z at P}{\color{#0B7D7D}{f_z(P)}}(z-z_0)=0\]

The tangent plane to a surface f(x,y,z)=0 at a point P is the plane that best approximates the surface near P. The gradient ∇f(P) = (fₓ, f_y, f_z) is the normal vector to this plane. For z = g(x,y), rewrite as g(x,y)−z = 0, so f_z = −1 and the plane becomes z − z₀ = gₓ(x−x₀) + g_y(y−y₀).

Where it comes from: Any curve on the surface through P has a tangent vector perpendicular to ∇f. The plane of all such tangent vectors is the tangent plane.

✓ Use when asked to find the tangent plane or normal vector to a surface at a point.
✗ Not for curves in 3D — those have tangent lines (using r'(t)), not tangent planes.
Normal = ∇f. For z=g(x,y): z−z₀ = gₓ(x−x₀)+g_y(y−y₀).
Curl (∇ × F)
\[\text{curl}\;\htmlData{t=F — the vector field}{\color{#B8336A}{\mathbf{F}}}=\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\\partial_x&\partial_y&\partial_z\\\htmlData{t=P — i-component of F}{\color{#D45D00}{P}}&\htmlData{t=Q — j-component of F}{\color{#5D7A20}{Q}}&\htmlData{t=R — k-component of F}{\color{#0B7D7D}{R}}\end{vmatrix}\]

The curl measures the rotational tendency of a vector field at a point. Think of it as "how much the field swirls." It's computed as a 3×3 determinant (formal expansion along the first row). The result is a vector: curl points in the axis of rotation (by the right-hand rule), and its magnitude measures the rotation rate. In 2D: curl F = (Qₓ − P_y)k.

Where it comes from: The notation ∇ × F treats the del operator ∇ = (∂ₓ, ∂_y, ∂_z) as a vector and takes its cross product with F = (P,Q,R).

✓ Use when testing if a field is conservative (curl = 0 ⟹ conservative), or computing rotation in fluid/EM applications.
✗ Not for scalar fields — curl only applies to vector fields. The result is a vector, not a scalar.
Determinant with ∇ in row 2, F in row 3. curl = 0 ⟹ conservative.
Conservative Field Test
\[\htmlData{t=F — the vector field}{\color{#B8336A}{\mathbf{F}}}\text{ conservative}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\text{curl}\;\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\htmlData{t=Qₓ — must equal Py}{\color{#5D7A20}{Q_x}}=\htmlData{t=Py — must equal Qₓ}{\color{#D45D00}{P_y}},\;\htmlData{t=Rₓ — must equal Pz}{\color{#0B7D7D}{R_x}}=P_z,\;R_y=Q_z\]

A field F = (P,Q,R) is conservative if and only if its curl is zero everywhere. In 2D, this reduces to checking one condition: Qₓ = P_y. To find the potential function f, integrate fₓ = P w.r.t. x, then use f_y = Q to determine the unknown function g(y).

Where it comes from: If F = ∇f, then P = fₓ, Q = f_y, R = f_z. Clairaut's theorem gives f_{xy} = f_{yx}, i.e., P_y = Qₓ. Similarly for the other pairs.

✓ Use when determining if a vector field is conservative or finding a potential function.
✗ Not for fields defined on regions with holes — curl = 0 doesn't guarantee a global potential in non-simply-connected domains.
Check all mixed-partial equalities. If all match, F is conservative.
Divergence (∇ · F)
\[\text{div}\;\htmlData{t=F — the vector field}{\color{#B8336A}{\mathbf{F}}}=\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}=\frac{\partial \htmlData{t=P — i-component of F}{\color{#D45D00}{P}}}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial \htmlData{t=Q — j-component of F}{\color{#5D7A20}{Q}}}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial \htmlData{t=R — k-component of F}{\color{#0B7D7D}{R}}}{\partial z}\]

The divergence measures the net outward flux of a vector field at a point. Positive divergence means the field is expanding outward (a source, like a garden hose nozzle). Negative means contracting inward (a sink, like a drain). Zero means incompressible (what flows in equals what flows out).

Where it comes from: The notation ∇ · F treats del as a vector and dots it with F — sum of component-wise partial derivatives.

✓ Use when computing sources/sinks in fluid flow or electromagnetic fields. Also needed for the Divergence Theorem (Ch 6).
✗ Not for scalar fields — divergence applies to vector fields only. The result is a scalar, not a vector.
Sum of each component's partial w.r.t. its own variable. Measures source/sink.

Chapter 4 — Line Integrals

Line Integral (Arc Length)
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\htmlData{t=f — scalar field on the curve}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}(x{,}y{,}z)\,ds=\int_{\htmlData{t=a — start of parameter interval}{\color{#A82870}{a}}}^{\htmlData{t=b — end of parameter interval}{\color{#A82870}{b}}}\htmlData{t=f — evaluated along the curve}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}(\mathbf{r}(t))\,\htmlData{t=|r'| — speed along the curve}{\color{#2050A0}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}}\,dt\]

Line integral w.r.t. arc length sums f along a curve weighted by ds. Think of f as density: the integral gives total mass. Parametrize the curve then multiply f(r(t)) by the speed |r'(t)|. Result is orientation-independent — reversing the curve gives the same answer.

Where it comes from: Replace ds with |r'(t)| dt from the arc-length formula (recall from §2.10).

✓ Use when the integrand involves ds (arc length element) or when computing mass/charge along a wire.
✗ Not for vector field integrals (∫F·dr) — those use the dot product instead of the speed.
Sum f along a curve. Multiply by |r'(t)| dt. Orientation-independent.
Vector Field Line Integral
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\htmlData{t=F — vector field}{\color{#D45D00}{\mathbf{F}}}\cdot d\htmlData{t=r — position vector along curve}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\mathbf{r}}}=\int_{\htmlData{t=a — start parameter}{\color{#A82870}{a}}}^{\htmlData{t=b — end parameter}{\color{#A82870}{b}}}\htmlData{t=F — field evaluated on curve}{\color{#D45D00}{\mathbf{F}}}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\htmlData{t=r' — tangent vector}{\color{#0B7D7D}{\mathbf{r}'}}(t)\,dt\]

Line integral of a vector field measures how much F pushes along the curve. The dot product F·r' extracts only the tangential component. This gives the work done by force F on a particle moving along C. Unlike scalar line integrals this IS orientation-dependent: reversing C negates the answer.

Where it comes from: Work = force × distance in the force direction. Infinitesimally: dW = F·dr = F·r'dt.

✓ Use when computing work done by a force or circulation of a field around a closed curve.
✗ Not for mass or charge computations — those use the scalar (ds) version above.
Work integral. F·r' dt. Orientation-dependent (reversing negates).
FTCLI (Fundamental Theorem)
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\nabla\htmlData{t=f — potential function for field F}{\color{#5D7A20}{f}}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\htmlData{t=f at endpoint — evaluate potential here}{\color{#5D7A20}{f}}(\mathbf{r}(\htmlData{t=b — end of parameter}{\color{#A82870}{b}}))-\htmlData{t=f at start — evaluate potential here}{\color{#5D7A20}{f}}(\mathbf{r}(\htmlData{t=a — start of parameter}{\color{#A82870}{a}}))\]

If F is conservative (F = ∇f) then the line integral depends ONLY on the endpoints — not on the path! Just evaluate the potential f at the start and end points and subtract. For a closed curve (start = end) the integral is always zero. This is the multivariable version of ∫f'dx = f(b)−f(a).

Where it comes from: Chain rule: F(r(t))·r'(t) = (d/dt)f(r(t)). Integrate both sides from a to b.

✓ Use when F is conservative. Saves enormous computation — just evaluate f at two points!
✗ Not for non-conservative fields. Must verify curl F = 0 first.
Conservative fields: integral = f(end) − f(start). Path-independent.
Potential via Line Integral
\[\htmlData{t=f — potential function to find}{\color{#5D7A20}{f}}(x{,}y{,}z)=\int_{\mathscr{C}}\htmlData{t=F — conservative vector field}{\color{#D45D00}{\mathbf{F}}}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\]

To find the potential function of a conservative field use a line segment from a fixed point P₀ to a general point (x-y-z). Parametrize as r(t) = (1−t)P₀ + t(x-y-z) for t ∈ [0-1]. During integration treat x-y-z as constants. Don't forget to add +C.

Where it comes from: By FTCLI: f(P) − f(P₀) = ∫F·dr. Setting f(P₀) = 0 gives f(P) directly.

✓ Use when the component-integration method is messy (non-polynomial fields).
✗ Not for non-conservative fields — verify curl = 0 first.
Find f by integrating F along a segment from P₀ to (x-y-z). Treat coordinates as constants.

Chapter 5 — Double Integrals

Fubini's Theorem (Rectangle)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}\htmlData{t=f — continuous function on R}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\,dA=\int_{\htmlData{t=a — left x-bound}{\color{#D45D00}{a}}}^{\htmlData{t=b — right x-bound}{\color{#D45D00}{b}}}\int_{\htmlData{t=c — bottom y-bound}{\color{#5D7A20}{c}}}^{\htmlData{t=d — top y-bound}{\color{#5D7A20}{d}}}\htmlData{t=f — integrand}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\,dy\,dx=\int_{\htmlData{t=c — bottom y-bound}{\color{#5D7A20}{c}}}^{\htmlData{t=d — top y-bound}{\color{#5D7A20}{d}}}\int_{\htmlData{t=a — left x-bound}{\color{#D45D00}{a}}}^{\htmlData{t=b — right x-bound}{\color{#D45D00}{b}}}\htmlData{t=f — integrand}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\,dx\,dy\]

Fubini's theorem lets you compute a double integral as two nested single integrals — called an iterated integral. For a rectangle both orders (dy dx or dx dy) give the same answer. The inner integral is evaluated first while treating the outer variable as a constant.

Where it comes from: The double Riemann sum factors into a product of single sums when the region is a rectangle and f is continuous.

✓ Use when the region is a rectangle [a-b] × [c-d] with constant limits on both integrals.
✗ Not for non-rectangular regions — use the general version with variable limits below.
Rectangle: either order works. Inner integral first — hold outer variable constant.
Fubini (General Region)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}\htmlData{t=f — integrand}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\,dA=\int_{\htmlData{t=a — left x-bound; constant}{\color{#4A8060}{a}}}^{\htmlData{t=b — right x-bound; constant}{\color{#4A8060}{b}}}\int_{\htmlData{t=f₁ — bottom boundary curve}{\color{#5D7A20}{f_1(x)}}}^{\htmlData{t=f₂ — top boundary curve}{\color{#5D7A20}{f_2(x)}}}\htmlData{t=f — integrand}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\,dy\,dx\]

For non-rectangular regions describe R using vertical slices (x-simple): the outer x-limits are constants while the inner y-limits are functions of x. The subscript R_yx reminds you: y first then x → dy dx. To reverse the order: sketch the region and re-describe with horizontal slices.

Where it comes from: Same as rectangular Fubini but with x-dependent limits on the inner integral.

✓ Use when the region is x-simple (bounded above/below by curves in y).
✗ Not for regions that aren't x-simple — try y-simple (dx dy) or split the region.
Vertical slices: outer x (constants) inner y (functions of x). R_yx → dy dx.
Polar Double Integral
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}\htmlData{t=f — function in Cartesian coords}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}(x{,}y)\,dA=\iint_{\mathscr{S}}\htmlData{t=f — rewritten in polar}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}(\htmlData{t=r — radial distance}{\color{#0B7D7D}{r}}\cos\htmlData{t=θ — angle from x-axis}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}},\;\htmlData{t=r — radial distance}{\color{#0B7D7D}{r}}\sin\htmlData{t=θ — angle}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}})\;\htmlData{t=r — Jacobian factor; don't forget!}{\color{#0B7D7D}{r}}\,d\htmlData{t=r — radial variable}{\color{#0B7D7D}{r}}\,d\htmlData{t=θ — angular variable}{\color{#A82870}{\theta}}\]

Converting a double integral to polar coordinates. The key: dA = r dr dθ (NOT just dr dθ). The extra factor of r is the Jacobian of the polar transformation. Replace x²+y² with r² and simplify. Best for circular regions and integrands containing x²+y².

Where it comes from: Jacobian of (x-y) = (r cosθ - r sinθ) is det[cosθ sinθ; −r sinθ r cosθ] = r.

✓ Use when the region involves circles (x²+y²=R²) or the integrand has x²+y². Converts nasty square roots to clean r.
✗ Not for rectangular regions or integrands with no circular symmetry — rectangular coords are simpler.
dA = r dr dθ. Replace x²+y² → r². Don't forget the extra r!
Change of Variables (General)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}\htmlData{t=f — original integrand}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\,dA=\iint_{\mathscr{S}}\htmlData{t=f — rewritten in new variables}{\color{#D45D00}{f}}\!\left|\frac{\partial(x{,}y)}{\partial(\htmlData{t=u — first new variable}{\color{#0B7D7D}{u}},\htmlData{t=v — second new variable}{\color{#A82870}{v}})}\right|\,d\htmlData{t=u — new variable 1}{\color{#0B7D7D}{u}}\,d\htmlData{t=v — new variable 2}{\color{#A82870}{v}}\]

The general change of variables formula for double integrals. The Jacobian determinant |∂(x-y)/∂(u-v)| scales the area element — it measures how much the transformation stretches or compresses area. Polar coordinates are a special case with Jacobian = r.

Where it comes from: Analogous to single-variable substitution ∫f(g(t))g'(t)dt where g' plays the role of the Jacobian.

✓ Use when the region or integrand simplifies under a specific substitution (polar - elliptic - rotational).
✗ Not for problems already simple in rectangular coords. Don't over-complicate.
2D substitution rule. Multiply by |Jacobian|. Polar is the special case J = r.
Sphere Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(\htmlData{t=u — longitude angle [0 to 2π]}{\color{#0B7D7D}{u}},\htmlData{t=v — latitude angle [−π/2 to π/2]}{\color{#A82870}{v}})=\big(\htmlData{t=R — radius}{\color{#2050A0}{R}}\cos\htmlData{t=v — latitude}{\color{#A82870}{v}}\cos\htmlData{t=u — longitude}{\color{#0B7D7D}{u}},\;\htmlData{t=R — radius}{\color{#2050A0}{R}}\cos\htmlData{t=v — latitude}{\color{#A82870}{v}}\sin\htmlData{t=u — longitude}{\color{#0B7D7D}{u}},\;\htmlData{t=R — radius}{\color{#2050A0}{R}}\sin\htmlData{t=v — latitude}{\color{#A82870}{v}}\big)\]

Parametrize a sphere of radius R centered at the origin using longitude u and latitude v. This is identical to the geographic coordinate system: u = longitude (around the equator) and v = latitude (above/below equator). Every point on the sphere corresponds to exactly one (u-v) pair (except the poles).

Where it comes from: Project a point P on the sphere onto the xy-plane. The angle from the xy-plane is v (latitude) and the angle in the xy-plane is u (longitude). Use trig to recover x-y-z.

✓ Use when parametrizing spheres for surface integrals or when converting to spherical coordinates.
✗ Not for cylinders or cones — each has its own parametrization.
Sphere: (R cos v cos u - R cos v sin u - R sin v). u = longitude - v = latitude.

Ch 1 — Vectors ✓

Builds the vector toolkit in ℝ² and ℝ³ — definitions, operations, and geometric applications that underpin all of multivariable calculus.

Vector Basics
What:
Vectors defined by magnitude and direction, represented as ordered components in ℝ² or ℝ³.
Why:
The fundamental objects everything else builds on — displacements, forces, velocities.
How:
\(\vec{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle\), \(|\vec{v}|=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2}\)
Trap:
Equality means same magnitude AND direction — position in space doesn't matter.
Vector Arithmetic
What:
Addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication — all component-wise.
Why:
Combines forces, displacements, and velocities algebraically; parallelogram law gives geometric intuition.
How:
\(\vec{v}+\vec{w}=\langle v_1+w_1,\,v_2+w_2,\,v_3+w_3\rangle\), \(c\vec{v}=\langle cv_1,cv_2,cv_3\rangle\)
Unit Vectors & Direction
What:
Normalization to length 1; standard basis î, ĵ, k̂; direction cosines and inclination angles.
Why:
Separates direction from magnitude — essential for projections and coordinate decomposition.
How:
\(\hat{v}=\vec{v}/|\vec{v}|\), direction cosines satisfy \(\cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1\)
Trap:
The zero vector has no unit vector — can't normalize it.
Dot Product
What:
Scalar product measuring how aligned two vectors are.
Why:
Unlocks angles between vectors, orthogonality tests, and projections.
How:
\(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}=v_1w_1+v_2w_2+v_3w_3=|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\cos\theta\), angle: \(\theta=\arccos\!\bigl(\frac{\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}}{|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|}\bigr)\)
Trap:
Result is a SCALAR, not a vector. Dot = 0 means perpendicular, not that a vector is zero.
Projections & Work
What:
Scalar and vector projection of one vector onto another; work as force dotted with displacement.
Why:
Decomposes vectors into parallel/perpendicular parts — central to physics and later integration.
How:
Scalar: \(\text{comp}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}=\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\), Vector: \(\text{proj}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}=\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|^2}\vec{v}\), Work: \(W=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\)
Cross Product
What:
A vector perpendicular to both inputs whose magnitude equals the parallelogram area.
Why:
Produces normal vectors to planes, computes torque, and measures area.
How:
\(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}\) via 3×3 determinant with î, ĵ, k̂ in the first row. Magnitude: \(|\vec{v}\times\vec{w}|=|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\sin\theta\)
Trap:
NOT commutative — \(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}=-(\vec{w}\times\vec{v})\). Use the right-hand rule for direction.
Geometric Applications
What:
Parallelogram area, triangle area, and parallelepiped volume from cross and triple products.
Why:
Turns abstract vector algebra into measurable geometry — areas, volumes, coplanarity.
How:
Area△ = \(\tfrac{1}{2}|\vec{u}\times\vec{v}|\), Volume = \(|\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})|\)
Trap:
Triple product = 0 means the vectors are coplanar, not that there is "no volume."
Connections → Dot product → scalar projections → work integrals (Ch 6) · Cross product → normal vectors → planes (Ch 2) → surface integrals (Ch 7) · Magnitude & distance → arc length & curvature (Ch 3)

Ch 2 — Lines, Planes & Parametric Curves ✓

Extends vectors to describe lines, planes, and curves in space. Introduces rotations, conic sections, parametric and polar representations with their full calculus toolkit.

Lines in Space
What:
A line is defined by a point P and a direction vector v⃗. A line segment restricts the parameter to [0, 1]. Two lines in 3D can be parallel (same direction), intersecting (meet at a point), or skew (neither parallel nor meeting — only in 3D).
Why:
Lines are the simplest curves and the foundation for parametric thinking — every more complex curve builds on this "point + direction" idea.
How:
Line: \(\vec{r}(t)=P+t\vec{v}\). Segment: \(\vec{r}(t)=(1-t)P+tQ\), \(0\le t\le 1\). Midpoint at \(t=\tfrac{1}{2}\). Parallel test: \(\vec{v}_1\times\vec{v}_2=\vec{0}\). Skew test: not parallel AND z-values disagree after solving x, y.
Trap:
Skew ≠ "don't intersect." Non-parallel lines that don't meet are skew. Parallel lines that don't meet are just parallel. Always check BOTH conditions.
Planes
What:
A plane is a flat surface defined by a point and a normal vector (perpendicular arrow). Three non-collinear points determine a unique plane. Two planes can be parallel (same normal direction) or intersect in a line.
Why:
Planes describe surfaces in 3D. Their normals are essential for tangent planes (Ch 3), surface integrals (Ch 6), and flux calculations.
How:
Equation: \(ax+by+cz=d\), normal \(\vec{n}=(a,b,c)\). From 3 points: \(\vec{n}=\overrightarrow{PQ}\times\overrightarrow{PR}\). Angle between planes: \(\cos\theta=\frac{\vec{n}_1\cdot\vec{n}_2}{|\vec{n}_1||\vec{n}_2|}\). Two-plane intersection: direction = \(\vec{n}_1\times\vec{n}_2\), find one point by setting a variable to 0.
Trap:
Lines use direction vectors (parallel to the line). Planes use normal vectors (perpendicular to the plane). Don't mix these up — they're fundamentally different.
Rotations & Translations
What:
Active rotation moves a point by angle θ (matrix A). Passive rotation rotates the axes instead (matrix A⁻¹ = Aᵀ). Translation shifts the origin to (h, k) by substituting (x−h, y−k). Combined: translate first, then rotate.
Why:
Used to eliminate cross-terms (Bxy) in conic equations, aligning a rotated conic with the coordinate axes for easier analysis.
How:
Active: \(A=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\). Passive: flip sin signs. Translation: \((x',y')=(x-h,\,y-k)\). Combined: translate → rotate.
Trap:
Active vs passive differ only by the sign on sinθ — using the wrong one rotates in the opposite direction.
Conic Sections
What:
The three classical conics — parabola, ellipse, hyperbola — classified by the discriminant of the general second-degree equation \(Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0\).
Why:
Conics appear everywhere: planetary orbits (ellipses), projectile paths (parabolas), satellite dishes (paraboloids). The discriminant gives instant classification without completing the square.
How:
Discriminant \(B^2-4AC\): negative → ellipse, zero → parabola, positive → hyperbola. Parametrizations: ellipse \((a\cos t,\,b\sin t)\), circle \((r\cos t,\,r\sin t)\).
Trap:
Degenerate cases exist: the "ellipse" x²+y²=−1 is empty, and B²−4AC=0 could give two parallel lines instead of a parabola.
Parametric Calculus
What:
Derivatives, tangent classification, area, and arc length for curves written as x(t), y(t). The chain rule adapts all standard calculus tools to parametric form.
Why:
Many curves (cycloids, epicycloids, Lissajous figures) can't be written as y=f(x). Parametric form handles self-intersections, loops, and multi-valued curves naturally.
How:
Slope: \(dy/dx=(dy/dt)/(dx/dt)\). Second deriv: \(d^2y/dx^2=\frac{d/dt(dy/dx)}{dx/dt}\). H-tangent: dy/dt=0. V-tangent: dx/dt=0. Area: \(\int y\,x'\,dt\). Arc length: \(\int\sqrt{(x')^2+(y')^2}\,dt\).
Trap:
The second derivative is NOT (d²y/dt²)/(d²x/dt²) — this is the #1 exam mistake. You must differentiate dy/dx with respect to t, then divide by dx/dt again.
Key Parametric Results
What:
Named results for classic curves that appear repeatedly on exams and in later chapters.
How:
Cycloid \(x=r(t-\sin t),\,y=r(1-\cos t)\): arch area = \(3\pi r^2\), arch length = \(8r\). Ellipse \(x=a\cos t,\,y=b\sin t\): area = \(\pi ab\). Key identity: \(1-\cos t=2\sin^2(t/2)\).
Trap:
Cycloid area = 3× the rolling circle (not 2×, not π×). The identity 1−cosθ = 2sin²(θ/2) is essential for simplifying cycloid integrals — memorize it.
Polar Coordinates
What:
Points described by distance r and angle θ from the origin. Common curves: cardioids \(r=1+\sin\theta\), limaçons \(r=a+b\cos\theta\), roses \(r=\cos(n\theta)\) with n petals (or 2n if n is even).
Why:
Curves with circular symmetry (spirals, petals, hearts) are simple in polar but nightmarish in Cartesian. Polar form also simplifies double integrals over circular regions (Ch 5).
How:
Conversion: \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta,\;r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\). Convention: \((-r,\theta)=(r,\theta+\pi)\). Quadrant check required for θ = arctan(y/x).
Trap:
arctan(y/x) only returns (−π/2, π/2). A point in Q3 like (−1,−1) gives arctan(1)=π/4, but the correct angle is 5π/4. Always check the quadrant.
Polar Calculus
What:
Slope, area, and arc length formulas adapted for polar curves r = f(θ). Area uses sectors (pie slices), not rectangles.
Why:
Computing area/length of polar curves directly — without converting to Cartesian — is usually much simpler.
How:
Slope: \(\frac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}\). Area: \(\frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta\). Between curves: \(\frac{1}{2}\int(r_{\text{out}}^2-r_{\text{in}}^2)\,d\theta\). Arc length: \(\int\sqrt{(r')^2+r^2}\,d\theta\). Cardioid perimeter = 8.
Trap:
For area between polar curves, find ALL intersections first — some only show up as r=0 at different θ values (both curves pass through the origin but at different angles).

Ch 3 — Partial Derivatives & Vector Fields (§3.1–3.7)

Extends calculus to functions of several variables, introduces the gradient as the unifying concept, and defines the three fundamental vector field operators.

Limits & Continuity (Multivariable)
What:
Limits of f(x,y) as (x,y) → (x₀,y₀). Continuity requires the limit to exist and equal the function value. Unlike 1D, approach can come from infinitely many directions.
Why:
Foundation for all multivariable calculus. If a function isn't continuous, derivatives may not exist or behave as expected.
How:
Limit exists: ε-δ proof (bound \(|f-L|\) using \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}<\delta\)). Limit DNE: find two paths giving different values. Continuous: all polynomials in x,y.
Trap:
Two paths agreeing does NOT prove the limit exists — there could be a third path that disagrees. ε-δ is the only way to prove existence.
Partial Derivatives
What:
The rate of change of f(x,y) when one variable changes and the others are held constant. Notation: ∂f/∂x, fₓ, f₍₁₎, Dₓf.
Why:
Building block for everything in multivariable calculus — gradients, chain rules, tangent planes, and optimization all use partial derivatives.
How:
For ∂f/∂x: treat y as a constant, differentiate w.r.t. x. Clairaut: \(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\) when second partials are continuous.
Trap:
Partial derivatives existing does NOT guarantee continuity. A function can have both partials at a point yet be discontinuous there.
Gradient & Directional Derivatives
What:
The gradient ∇f = (fₓ, f_y) is the vector of partials. The directional derivative D_u f = ∇f · u gives the rate of change in any direction u.
Why:
∇f points in the direction of steepest ascent, perpendicular to level curves. Max rate = |∇f|. Essential for optimization and physics.
How:
\(D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\nabla f||\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta\). Max when u ∥ ∇f. Zero along level curves.
Trap:
D_u f requires u to be a unit vector for the "directional derivative." If u is not unit, normalize first — or use D_u f = |u| · D_v f.
Chain Rule & Implicit Differentiation
What:
Chain rule for z = f(x,y) where x,y depend on other variables. Implicit differentiation for surfaces f(x,y,z)=0.
How:
One parameter: \(dz/dt = z_x\,x' + z_y\,y'\). Implicit: \(\partial z/\partial x = -f_x/f_z\). Tree diagrams help: multiply partials along each path, sum all paths to the target variable.
Trap:
In the chain rule with two parameters, use ∂ (partial), not d (total). Don't forget the tree diagram has multiple paths.
Tangent Planes & Normal Lines
What:
The tangent plane approximates a surface near a point. The normal line is perpendicular to it, in the direction of ∇f.
How:
Plane: \(f_x(x-x_0)+f_y(y-y_0)+f_z(z-z_0)=0\). Normal line: \(\mathbf{r}(t) = P + t\nabla f\). For z=g(x,y): \(z-z_0 = g_x(x-x_0)+g_y(y-y_0)\).
Trap:
For z=g(x,y), rewrite as g−z=0, so f_z = −1. Don't forget the negative sign on the z term.
Conservative Fields, Curl & Divergence
What:
Vector fields F = (P,Q,R). Conservative: F = ∇f for some scalar f. Curl measures rotation. Divergence measures source/sink behavior.
Why:
Conservative fields simplify line integrals (Ch 4). Curl and div appear in Green's, Stokes', and Divergence theorems (Ch 6).
How:
Test: curl F = 0 ⟹ conservative. Curl = \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\) (determinant). Div = \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = P_x+Q_y+R_z\). Identities: curl(∇f)=0, div(curl F)=0.
Trap:
To check if F is conservative, you DON'T take the gradient of F. You check if F IS someone's gradient by verifying curl F = 0.
Connections → Partial derivatives → chain rule → implicit differentiation · Gradient → directional derivatives → steepest ascent (optimization, Ch 7) · Tangent planes ← recall plane equation from Ch 2 · Conservative fields → line integrals (Ch 4) · Curl/Div → Green's, Stokes', Divergence theorems (Ch 6)

Ch 4 — Line Integrals

Integrating scalar and vector functions along curves. Work, circulation, and path independence via conservative fields.
Scalar Line Integrals (§4.1)
What:
Integrate a scalar field f along a curve C weighted by arc length ds. Result: total mass, charge, or surface area of a curtain.
Why:
Extends single-variable integration from intervals to curves. Mass of a wire = ∫f ds where f is density.
How:
Parametrize C: r(t), compute |r'(t)|, substitute, integrate: \(\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\). For piecewise curves, use different parameter symbols per segment.
Trap:
Don't confuse |r'(t)| (speed — for ds integrals) with r'(t) (tangent vector — for F·dr integrals). Scalar line integrals are orientation-independent.
Vector Line Integrals & Work (§4.2)
What:
Integrate a vector field F along C via the dot product F·dr. Measures how much F pushes along C. Result: work done by a force.
Why:
Work = ∫F·dr. Circulation = ∮F·dr (closed loop). Foundation for Green's and Stokes' theorems.
How:
Parametrize C, compute F(r(t))·r'(t), integrate: \(\int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)\,dt\). Or use component form: \(\int P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz\).
Trap:
Unlike scalar integrals, these ARE orientation-dependent: reversing C negates the answer. Don't forget the sign!
Fundamental Theorem & Path Independence (§4.3)
What:
If F = ∇f (conservative), the line integral depends only on endpoints: ∫F·dr = f(end) − f(start). Closed loops give zero.
Why:
Massive shortcut: skip parametrization entirely for conservative fields. Key to conservation of energy in physics.
How:
Verify curl F = 0 (from §3.7). Find f by component integration or line integral method. Then f(Q) − f(P) = answer.
Trap:
FTCLI only works for conservative fields in simply connected regions. A region with a hole (like a disk minus its center) is NOT simply connected — the theorem may fail.
Connections → Conservative fields (§3.7) → FTCLI → path independence · Work integral → kinetic energy theorem · Circulation → Green's theorem (Ch 6) · Potential functions → electrostatic and gravitational potential

Ch 5 — Double Integrals & Surfaces

Double integrals over planar regions via Fubini's theorem, polar coordinates, and sketching/parameterizing 3D surfaces.
Region Descriptions & Fubini's Theorem (§5.1–5.2)
What:
Describe regions as x-simple (R_yx: vertical slices → dy dx) or y-simple (R_xy: horizontal slices → dx dy). Fubini reduces ∬f dA to iterated integrals.
Why:
Converts intimidating 2D integrals into two nested 1D integrals you already know how to do.
How:
Sketch region. Choose slices (vertical or horizontal). Write inner limits as functions of outer variable and outer limits as constants. Integrate inside-out.
Trap:
Inner limits must be functions of the outer variable only! If you write ∫₀¹∫₀ˣ f dy dx, the y-limits depend on x (outer). Never let inner limits depend on the inner variable.
Polar Double Integrals (§5.4)
What:
Change of variables to polar: x = r cosθ, y = r sinθ, dA = r dr dθ. The Jacobian |∂(x,y)/∂(r,θ)| = r.
Why:
Circular regions and x²+y² integrands become trivial in polar. Converts double square roots to clean polynomials in r.
How:
Replace x²+y² with r², describe R in polar (r-limits then θ-limits), multiply integrand by r, integrate ∬f(r cosθ, r sinθ) r dr dθ.
Trap:
FORGETTING THE EXTRA r! dA = r dr dθ, NOT dr dθ. This is the #1 error on exams with polar double integrals.
Quadric Surfaces & Parametrization (§5.5–5.6)
What:
Quadric surfaces (ellipsoid, paraboloid, hyperboloid, cone, cylinder) identified by traces. Surfaces parametrized by r(u,v) with two parameters.
Why:
Surface integrals (Ch 6) require parametrized surfaces. Quadric recognition is essential for setting up integrals.
How:
Traces: set x=c, y=c, or z=c and identify the 2D curve. Count squared terms and signs to classify. Parametrize: sphere uses (u,v) = (longitude, latitude), cylinder uses (θ, z).
Trap:
Cone vs hyperboloid of one sheet: both have circular z-traces, but cone has RHS = 0 (vertex at origin) while hyperboloid has RHS = 1 (no vertex).
Connections → Region descriptions (§5.1) ← polar coords (§2.12) · Fubini ← single-variable FTC · Jacobian ← partial derivatives (§3.2) · Quadric surfaces → surface integrals (Ch 6) · Parametric surfaces → Stokes'/Divergence theorems (Ch 6)
