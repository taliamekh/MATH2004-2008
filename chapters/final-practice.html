Q7 — Polar Area (Cardioid, Half-Angle Identity) · \(\frac{3\pi}{2}\)
📄 Question

Find the area enclosed by the cardioid \(r=1+\cos\theta\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Polar area \(A=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}r^2\,d\theta\) Exam Sheet #10: "Area between Polar Curves"
2
Half-angle \(\cos^2\theta=\frac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2}\) Exam Sheet #1: "Double-Angle: cos²x"
📝 Full Solution
1
Expand \((1+\cos\theta)^2\)

\(= 1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta = 1+2\cos\theta+\frac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2} = \frac{3}{2}+2\cos\theta+\frac{\cos 2\theta}{2}\)

Use the half-angle identity on cos²θ to make everything integrable.

2
Integrate

\[A=\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{3}{2}\theta+2\sin\theta+\frac{\sin 2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{2\pi}=\frac{1}{2}\cdot 3\pi = \boxed{\frac{3\pi}{2}}\]

sin and sin 2θ terms vanish over [0,2π]. Only the constant term 3/2 survives: (3/2)(2π) = 3π.

✅ Answer: \(\frac{3\pi}{2}\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Polar Area (Cardioid)
1\(A=\frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta\), expand r², use half-angle for cos²θ
2Over [0,2π]: sin/cos terms → 0, only constants survive
Q8 — Polar Arc Length (Spiral) · \(\pi\sqrt{\pi^2+1}+\ln(\pi+\sqrt{\pi^2+1})\)
📄 Question

Find the arc length of \(r=2\theta\) for \(0\le\theta\le\pi\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Polar arc length \(L=\int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{r^2+r'^2}\,d\theta\) Exam Sheet #12: "Arc Length (Polar)"

This integral formula is on most tables. sinh⁻¹(u) = ln(u+√(u²+1)).

✅ Answer: \(\pi\sqrt{\pi^2+1}+\ln(\pi+\sqrt{\pi^2+1})\)

Q12 — Double Integral (Polynomial, Triangle Region) · \(\frac{16}{3}\)
📄 Question

Evaluate \(\int_0^2\int_0^x(x^2+y^2)\,dy\,dx\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Double integral Exam Sheet #26: "Double Integral (Iterated)"
📝 Full Solution
1
Inner integral (y from 0 to x)

\[\int_0^x(x^2+y^2)\,dy = \left[x^2y+\frac{y^3}{3}\right]_0^x = x^3+\frac{x^3}{3} = \frac{4x^3}{3}\]

x² is constant w.r.t. y → x²·y. Then evaluate at y=x and y=0.

2
Outer integral

\[\int_0^2\frac{4x^3}{3}\,dx = \frac{4}{3}\cdot\frac{x^4}{4}\Big|_0^2 = \frac{1}{3}\cdot 16 = \boxed{\frac{16}{3}}\]

The 4's cancel: (4/3)(x⁴/4) = x⁴/3.

✅ Answer: \(\frac{16}{3}\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Double Integral (Polynomial)
1Integrate inner variable first (treat outer as constant)
2Evaluate at bounds → single-variable integral → evaluate

Q16 — Change of Variables (Simplify Integrand) · \(\frac{8}{3}\)
📄 Question

Evaluate \(\int_0^2\int_0^{2y}(x-y)^2\,dx\,dy\) using \(u=x-y\), \(v=y\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Change of variables Exam Sheet #28: "Change of Variables (2D)"
📝 Full Solution
1
Inverse: \(x=u+v\), \(y=v\). Jacobian \(|J|=1\).

Bounds: \(x=0\Rightarrow u=-v\); \(x=2y=2v\Rightarrow u=v\); \(v\in[0,2]\).

Solve for x,y in terms of u,v. The Jacobian is 1 (rotation preserves area).

2
Evaluate

\[\int_0^2\int_{-v}^v u^2\,du\,dv = \int_0^2\left[\frac{u^3}{3}\right]_{-v}^v dv = \int_0^2\frac{2v^3}{3}\,dv = \frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{v^4}{4}\Big|_0^2 = \frac{2}{3}\cdot 4 = \boxed{\frac{8}{3}}\]

u² is even, so ∫_{-v}^{v} u²du = 2∫_0^v u²du = 2v³/3.

✅ Answer: \(\frac{8}{3}\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Change of Variables
1Choose substitution to simplify integrand
2Compute Jacobian, transform bounds
3Integrate in new coordinates

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Green's Theorem Exam Sheet #35: "Green's Theorem"
📝 Full Solution
1
\(P=y\), \(Q=-x\). Compute \(Q_x-P_y\)

\(Q_x=-1\), \(P_y=1\). \(Q_x-P_y = -1-1 = -2\).

2\(Q_x-P_y\) constant → multiply by area

Q24 — Green's Theorem (Non-Constant Integrand, Square) · \(\frac{5}{2}\)
📄 Question

Evaluate \(\oint_C(x^2-y)\,dx+(xy+x)\,dy\) where C is the unit square \([0,1]^2\) (CCW).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Green's Theorem Exam Sheet #35: "Green's Theorem"
📝 Full Solution
1
Compute \(Q_x-P_y\)

\(Q_x=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy+x)=y+1\). \(P_y=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2-y)=-1\).

\(Q_x-P_y = (y+1)-(-1) = y+2\).

Not constant — we need to actually integrate (can't use constant × area shortcut).

2
Integrate over the unit square

\[\iint_{[0,1]^2}(y+2)\,dA = \int_0^1\int_0^1(y+2)\,dy\,dx = \int_0^1\left[\frac{y^2}{2}+2y\right]_0^1 dx = \int_0^1\frac{5}{2}\,dx = \boxed{\frac{5}{2}}\]

The x-integral is trivial since the integrand (after y-integration) is constant.

✅ Answer: \(\frac{5}{2}\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Green's Theorem (Non-Constant)
1Green's: compute \(Q_x-P_y\)
2If not constant → set up and evaluate the double integral
Q25 — Stokes' Theorem (Choose Easier Surface) · \(-2\pi\)
📄 Question

Use Stokes' to evaluate \(\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS\) for \(\mathbf{F}=(y,-x,z)\) on the upper hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\), \(z\ge 0\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Stokes' Theorem Exam Sheet #36: "Stokes' Theorem"
📝 Full Solution
1
Compute curl F

\(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=(0,0,-1-1)=(0,0,-2)\).

k-component: Q_x − P_y = (−1) − (1) = −2.

2
Key insight: use a FLAT disk instead of the hemisphere

Both surfaces share the same boundary (unit circle at z=0). By Stokes', the integral is the same over ANY surface with that boundary.

On the flat disk z=0: \(\hat{n}=\mathbf{k}\). \((0,0,-2)\cdot\mathbf{k}=-2\).

\[\iint(-2)\,dA = -2\cdot\pi = \boxed{-2\pi}\]

Tip: always choose the EASIEST surface with the same boundary. A flat disk beats a curved hemisphere every time.

✅ Answer: \(-2\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Stokes' Theorem (Choose Easier Surface)
1Compute curl F
2Stokes' says: ANY surface with same boundary gives same answer
3Choose the flat disk → constant integrand → multiply by area

Q27 — Critical Points (Monkey Saddle, SDT Inconclusive) · \((0,0)\), saddle
📄 Question

Find and classify the critical points of \(f=x^3-3xy^2\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Second Derivative Test Exam Sheet #19: "Second Derivative Test"
📝 Full Solution
1
Find critical points

\(f_x=3x^2-3y^2=0 \Rightarrow x^2=y^2\). \(f_y=-6xy=0 \Rightarrow x=0\) or \(y=0\).

If \(x=0\): \(y^2=0\Rightarrow y=0\). If \(y=0\): \(x^2=0\Rightarrow x=0\). Only CP: \((0,0)\).

Both equations force x = y = 0. There's only one critical point.

2
Apply SDT

\(f_{xx}=6x=0\), \(f_{yy}=-6x=0\), \(f_{xy}=-6y=0\). \(D=0\cdot 0-0=0\).

SDT is inconclusive.

D = 0 means the test gives no information. We need another method.

3
Check behavior along different directions

Along \(y=0\): \(f=x^3\) — changes sign at 0 (positive for x>0, negative for x<0).

Therefore (0,0) is NOT an extremum. This is a monkey saddle — a saddle with three "seats" instead of two.

Tip: when D=0, check f along lines through the critical point. If f changes sign → saddle. If same sign → might be a flat extremum.

✅ Answer: Only CP is \((0,0)\); SDT inconclusive (\(D=0\)). It is a saddle point (monkey saddle).
⚡ Quick Guide — Critical Points (SDT Inconclusive)
1Solve \(f_x=0\), \(f_y=0\)
2SDT: D > 0 → extremum. D < 0 → saddle. D = 0 → inconclusive
3If D = 0: check f along lines through the CP
Q28 — Lagrange Multipliers (xy on Unit Circle) · Max \(\frac{1}{2}\), Min \(-\frac{1}{2}\)
📄 Question

Find the extrema of \(f=xy\) subject to \(x^2+y^2=1\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Lagrange multipliers \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 7 Formula Sheet: "Lagrange Multiplier Method"
📝 Full Solution
1
Lagrange equations

\(\nabla f=(y,x)\), \(\nabla g=(2x,2y)\). So \(y=2\lambda x\) and \(x=2\lambda y\).

Two equations, two unknowns (plus λ and the constraint).

2
Eliminate λ: multiply the equations

\(xy = 4\lambda^2 xy\). If \(xy\ne 0\): \(\lambda^2=\frac{1}{4}\), so \(\lambda=\pm\frac{1}{2}\).

Multiplying equation 1 by equation 2 gives xy = (2λx)(2λy) = 4λ²xy.

3
Case \(\lambda=\frac{1}{2}\): \(y=x\)

Constraint: \(2x^2=1\Rightarrow x=\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\). \(f=\frac{1}{2}\) → max.

4
Case \(\lambda=-\frac{1}{2}\): \(y=-x\)

Constraint: \(2x^2=1\Rightarrow x=\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\). \(f=-\frac{1}{2}\) → min.

Two values of λ give two pairs of points. Compare f-values.

✅ Answer: Max = \(\frac{1}{2}\) at \((\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})\). Min = \(-\frac{1}{2}\) at \((\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})\) and \((-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Lagrange Multipliers (Circle Constraint)
1Set up \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\)
2Eliminate λ by dividing/multiplying equations
3Apply constraint for each λ → find points
4Compare f-values → classify
