Chapter 6

The Three Big Theorems

Surface area, surface integrals, flux, and the three capstone theorems of multivariable calculus: Green's, Stokes', and the Divergence Theorem. Also triple integrals and coordinate transforms (cylindrical & spherical).

★ Big Picture

Everything in Chapters 1–5 was building toward this chapter. Line integrals (Ch4), double integrals (Ch5), partial derivatives and curl/divergence (Ch3) all come together in three powerful theorems that relate integrals of different dimensions. Green's theorem connects a line integral around a closed curve to a double integral over the enclosed region. Stokes' theorem generalizes Green's to 3D surfaces. The Divergence theorem connects a surface integral (flux) to a triple integral over the enclosed solid.

§6.1 — Surface Integrals

Recall from Ch5 that a double integral integrates a function over a flat region \(\mathscr{R}\) in the \(xy\)-plane. A surface integral is the natural next step: it integrates a function over a curved surface \(\mathscr{S}\) in 3D space. Think of it as a "2D version of a line integral" — instead of integrating along a curve, we integrate over a surface.

The key idea: for a double integral we partition the flat region \(\mathscr{R}\) into small rectangles with area \(\Delta A_{ij}\). For a surface integral we partition the surface \(\mathscr{S}\) itself into small curved patches with surface area \(\Delta S_{ij}\). Then we form the Riemann sum and take the limit.

Surface Integral (Scalar)

Let \(F(x,y,z)\) be continuous on a smooth surface \(\mathscr{S}\). The surface integral of \(F\) over \(\mathscr{S}\) is:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F(x,y,z)\,dS = \lim_{\|\mathscr{P}\|\to 0} \sum_{j=1}^{n}\sum_{i=1}^{m} F(x_i, y_j, f(x_i,y_j))\,\Delta S_{ij}\]

where \(\Delta S_{ij}\) is the surface area of the \((i,j)\)-th patch and \(\|\mathscr{P}\|\) is the mesh of the partition.

When \(F(x,y,z) = 1\), the surface integral gives the surface area of \(\mathscr{S}\).

Computing Surface Integrals

The surface integral reduces to a double integral over a flat region \(\mathscr{R}\) (the projection of \(\mathscr{S}\) onto a coordinate plane). The "correction factor" accounts for how tilted the surface is:

Surface Integral — Graph Form

Case 1: If \(z = f(x,y)\) defines \(\mathscr{S}\), project onto the \(xy\)-plane:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} F\!\big(x, y, f(x,y)\big)\,\sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2}\;dA\]
\(F\) = integrand evaluated on the surface \(f_x, f_y\) = partial derivatives of the surface equation \(\mathscr{R}\) = projection of \(\mathscr{S}\) onto the \(xy\)-plane

Case 2: If \(y = g(x,z)\), project onto the \(xz\)-plane: use \(\sqrt{1 + g_x^2 + g_z^2}\,dA\).

Case 3: If \(x = h(y,z)\), project onto the \(yz\)-plane: use \(\sqrt{1 + h_y^2 + h_z^2}\,dA\).

Surface Integral — Parametric Form

If \(\mathscr{S}\) is parametrized by \(\mathbf{r}(u,v) = \big(x(u,v),\,y(u,v),\,z(u,v)\big)\) for \((u,v) \in \mathscr{R}^*\):

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS = \iint_{\mathscr{R}^*} F\!\big(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\big)\,|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|\;dA\]
\(\mathbf{r}_u\) = partial derivative of position vector w.r.t. \(u\) \(\mathbf{r}_v\) = partial derivative w.r.t. \(v\) \(|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|\) = area magnification factor

Setting \(F = 1\) gives the surface area: \(\displaystyle\text{Area}(\mathscr{S}) = \iint_{\mathscr{R}^*}|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|\,dA\).

★ Evaluation Strategy

To evaluate \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS\):

1. Write the surface as \(z = f(x,y)\), \(y = g(x,z)\), or \(x = h(y,z)\) — whichever is easiest.

2. Choose the coordinate plane to project onto and describe the projection \(\mathscr{R}\).

3. Compute the partial derivatives and the square root factor.

4. Evaluate the resulting double integral (Fubini — you may need to swap integration order).

If the surface has a natural parametrization (sphere, cylinder), use the parametric form instead.

Fig 6.1 — A surface \(\mathscr{S}\) with a small patch \(\Delta S_{ij}\) projected down to \(\Delta A_{ij}\) on the flat region \(\mathscr{R}\). The surface integral sums \(F \cdot \Delta S_{ij}\) over the curved surface.

Worked Examples

Example 6.1 — Surface Integral over a Plane

Problem: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS\) where \(F(x,y,z) = x+y+z\) over the surface given by the plane \(x+y+z=2\) in the first octant.

Solution

What we need to find: A scalar surface integral — the integral of \(F\) weighted by surface area over a triangular piece of a plane.

Thought process: The surface is given as a graph \(z = f(x,y)\), so we use the Surface Integral — Graph Form (Formula Sheet #49): \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} F(x,y,f(x,y))\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}\,dA\).

Step 1 — Express the surface as \(z = f(x,y)\): From \(x+y+z=2\), we get \(f(x,y) = 2-x-y\).

Step 2 — Compute the tilt factor: \(f_x = -1\), \(f_y = -1\), so:

\[\sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2} = \sqrt{1 + (-1)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{3}\]

Step 3 — Evaluate \(F\) on the surface: Substitute \(z = 2-x-y\) into \(F\):

\[F(x,y,f(x,y)) = x + y + (2-x-y) = 2 \quad \text{(constant!)}\]

Step 4 — Describe the projection \(\mathscr{R}\): In the first octant, set \(z=0\) in \(x+y+z=2\): the boundary is the line \(x+y=2\). So \(\mathscr{R}\) is the triangle with vertices \((0,0)\), \((2,0)\), \((0,2)\). Its area is \(\frac{1}{2}(2)(2) = 2\).

Step 5 — Assemble and evaluate: Since \(F \cdot \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} = 2\sqrt{3}\) is constant, the integral is just the constant times the area of \(\mathscr{R}\):

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} 2\sqrt{3}\,dA = 2\sqrt{3} \cdot \text{Area}(\mathscr{R}) = 2\sqrt{3} \cdot 2 = \boxed{4\sqrt{3}}\]
★ Tip

When \(F\) simplifies to a constant on the surface, the integral becomes constant \(\times\) Area\((\mathscr{R}) \times \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}\). Always substitute and simplify \(F\) on \(\mathscr{S}\) before setting up the double integral — you may avoid integrating entirely.

📎 Uses: Surface Integral — Graph Form (Formula Sheet #49)

Example 6.2 — Surface Integral on a Cylinder (Three Methods)

Problem: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_{\mathscr{S}} xyz\,dS\) where \(\mathscr{S}\) is the part of the cylinder \(x^2+z^2=4\) in the first octant between \(y=0\) and \(y=1\).

Solution

What we need to find: A scalar surface integral over a cylindrical surface. Three approaches are possible — we show all three to illustrate the choice of method.

Thought process: The cylinder \(x^2+z^2=4\) can be solved for \(x\) (project onto \(yz\)-plane), for \(z\) (project onto \(xy\)-plane), or parametrized as a cylinder. Each uses the Surface Integral — Graph Form (Formula Sheet #49) or the Surface Integral — Parametric Form (Formula Sheet #50).

Method 1 — Project onto the \(yz\)-plane (Formula Sheet #49, Case 3: \(x = h(y,z)\)):

Step 1: Solve for \(x\): \(x = h(y,z) = \sqrt{4-z^2}\) (first octant, so \(x \geq 0\)).

Step 2: Compute partials: \(h_y = 0\), \(h_z = -z/\sqrt{4-z^2}\).

Step 3: Tilt factor: \(\sqrt{1+h_y^2+h_z^2} = \sqrt{1 + z^2/(4-z^2)} = \sqrt{4/(4-z^2)} = 2/\sqrt{4-z^2}\).

Step 4: The projection is the rectangle \(\mathscr{R} = \{0 \leq y \leq 1,\;0 \leq z \leq 2\}\). On \(\mathscr{S}\): \(F = \sqrt{4-z^2}\cdot y\cdot z\).

Step 5: The \(\sqrt{4-z^2}\) in \(F\) cancels with the \(1/\sqrt{4-z^2}\) in the tilt factor:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} xyz\,dS = \int_0^2\!\int_0^1 \sqrt{4-z^2}\cdot y\cdot z\cdot \frac{2}{\sqrt{4-z^2}}\,dy\,dz = 2\int_0^2 z\,dz \cdot \int_0^1 y\,dy = 2\cdot 2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2} = \boxed{2}\]

Method 2 — Project onto the \(xy\)-plane (Formula Sheet #49, Case 1: \(z = f(x,y)\)):

Step 1: \(z = f(x,y) = \sqrt{4-x^2}\). Partials: \(f_x = -x/\sqrt{4-x^2}\), \(f_y = 0\).

Step 2: Tilt factor: \(2/\sqrt{4-x^2}\). Projection: \(\mathscr{R} = \{0 \leq x \leq 2,\;0 \leq y \leq 1\}\).

Step 3: Again the square roots cancel:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} xyz\,dS = 2\int_0^2 x\,dx\cdot\int_0^1 y\,dy = 2\cdot 2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2} = \boxed{2}\]

Method 3 — Parametric (Formula Sheet #50):

Step 1: Parametrize the cylinder: \(x = 2\cos u\), \(y = v\), \(z = 2\sin u\) with \(0 \leq u \leq \pi/2\), \(0 \leq v \leq 1\).

Step 2: Compute \(\mathbf{r}_u = (-2\sin u, 0, 2\cos u)\) and \(\mathbf{r}_v = (0, 1, 0)\).

Step 3: Cross product: \(\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v = (-2\cos u, 0, -2\sin u)\), so \(|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| = 2\).

Step 4: \(F(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) = (2\cos u)(v)(2\sin u) = 2v\sin 2u\).

Step 5: Integrate:

\[\iint F\,dS = \int_0^1\!\int_0^{\pi/2} 2v\sin 2u\cdot 2\,du\,dv = 4\int_0^1 v\,dv\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin 2u\,du = 4\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 1 = \boxed{2}\]
★ Tip

All three methods give the same answer. Notice how the square root cancels in Methods 1 and 2 — this is common for cylinders. Parametric (Method 3) is often cleanest because \(|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|\) comes out as a constant.

📎 Uses: Surface Integral — Graph Form (#49) and Parametric Form (#50)

§6.2 — Flux & Oriented Surfaces

The surface integral \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} F\,dS\) integrates a scalar function over a surface. But what if we want to integrate a vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) over a surface? This gives the flux — the net flow of \(\mathbf{F}\) through \(\mathscr{S}\).

Orientable Surfaces

To define flux, we need a consistent notion of "which side is outside." A surface is orientable if we can define a unit normal vector \(\mathbf{n}\) that varies continuously over the entire surface. Most surfaces we encounter (planes, spheres, paraboloids, cylinders) are orientable. The classic non-orientable surface is the Möbius strip — if you walk a normal vector around it, you return to the start pointing the opposite direction.

Open and Closed Surfaces

Open surface: has a boundary curve (perimeter). Examples: a hemisphere, a piece of a plane, a bowl.

Closed surface: has no boundary — it completely encloses a 3D region. Examples: a sphere, a cube, an ellipsoid. A closed surface divides \(\mathbb{R}^3\) into an interior and an exterior.

For an open surface \(z = f(x,y)\), the upward-pointing outer unit normal is:

\[\mathbf{n} = \frac{(-f_x,\,-f_y,\,1)}{\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}}\]

For a closed surface, the outer normal points away from the enclosed region; the inner normal points inward.

Flux (Net Flow Through a Surface)

The flux of \(\mathbf{F}\) through \(\mathscr{S}\) is the surface integral:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\]
\(\mathbf{F}\) = vector field (e.g. velocity of a fluid) \(\mathbf{n}\) = outer unit normal \(d\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{n}\,dS\)

Physically: if \(\mathbf{F}\) is the velocity of a fluid, the flux measures the volume of fluid passing through \(\mathscr{S}\) per unit time. Flux > 0 means net outward flow; flux < 0 means net inward flow.

Computing Flux

Flux Formula — Graph Form

If \(z = f(x,y)\) defines \(\mathscr{S}\) and \(\mathbf{F} = (P, Q, R)\), then with the upward orientation:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} \big(-Pf_x - Qf_y + R\big)\,dA\]
\(P\) \(Q\) \(R\) = components of \(\mathbf{F}\) evaluated at \(\big(x\;y\;f(x{,}y)\big)\) \(f_x\) \(f_y\) = partial derivatives of the surface

Similarly: if \(y = g(x,z)\), then \(\iint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \pm\iint_{\mathscr{R}} (Pg_x - Q + Rg_z)\,dA\). If \(x = h(y,z)\), then \(\pm\iint_{\mathscr{R}} (P - Qh_y - Rh_z)\,dA\).

Flux Formula — Parametric Form

If \(\mathscr{S}\) is parametrized by \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\):

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{\mathscr{R}^*} \mathbf{F}\!\big(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\big)\cdot(\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v)\,dA\]

Note: the sign depends on whether \(\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v\) points outward. If it points inward, negate it.

Example 6.3 — Flux Through a Paraboloid

Problem: Find the flux of \(\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, z)\) through the paraboloid \(z = 1 - x^2 - y^2\) above the \(xy\)-plane (upward normal).

Solution

What we need to find: The flux \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}\) — the net upward flow of the vertical field \(\mathbf{F}\) through the bowl-shaped surface.

Thought process: The surface is \(z = f(x,y) = 1-x^2-y^2\) (a downward-opening paraboloid). Use the Flux — Graph Form (Formula Sheet #51): \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}(-Pf_x - Qf_y + R)\,dA\).

Step 1 — Identify components: \(\mathbf{F} = (P,Q,R) = (0, 0, z)\). On the surface: \(R = z = 1-x^2-y^2\).

Step 2 — Compute partials: \(f_x = -2x\), \(f_y = -2y\).

Step 3 — Apply flux formula: Since \(P = 0\) and \(Q = 0\), the terms \(-Pf_x\) and \(-Qf_y\) vanish:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} \big(0 - 0 + (1-x^2-y^2)\big)\,dA = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} (1-x^2-y^2)\,dA\]

Step 4 — Describe \(\mathscr{R}\): The paraboloid meets \(z=0\) when \(x^2+y^2=1\), so the projection is the unit disk.

Step 5 — Switch to polar (recall from §5.4: \(dA = r\,dr\,d\theta\)):

\[= \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^1 (1-r^2)\,r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^1(r - r^3)\,dr = 2\pi\left[\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{4} = \boxed{\frac{\pi}{2}}\]
★ Tip

When \(\mathbf{F}\) only has a \(z\)-component \((P=Q=0)\), the flux formula becomes just \(\iint R\,dA\). The partial derivative terms vanish because a purely vertical field isn't affected by horizontal tilt. This simplification happens often — check for it before computing \(f_x\) and \(f_y\).

📎 Uses: Flux Through a Surface (#51), Polar Double Integral (#45)

§6.3 — Green's Theorem

Green's theorem is the first of the three "big theorems." It connects a line integral around a closed curve in the plane to a double integral over the enclosed region. Think of it as the 2D version of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — it relates the "boundary integral" (the line integral around the edge) to the "interior integral" (the double integral over the inside).

Positive Orientation

A simple closed curve \(\mathscr{C}\) in the \(xy\)-plane is positively oriented if, as you walk along it in the direction of increasing parameter, the enclosed region \(\mathscr{R}\) is always on your left. This is the counterclockwise direction.

Green's Theorem

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be a positively oriented piecewise smooth simple closed curve enclosing a region \(\mathscr{R}\). If \(\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (P(x,y),\,Q(x,y))\) has continuous partial derivatives, then:

\[\oint_{\mathscr{C}} P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_{\mathscr{R}}\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA\]
\(\oint\) = integral around a closed curve \(Q_x - P_y\) = the 2D scalar curl \(\mathscr{R}\) = region enclosed by \(\mathscr{C}\)

Equivalently: \(\displaystyle\oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} (\text{curl}\;\mathbf{F})_z\,dA\).

★ Why It's Powerful

Green's theorem lets you convert a hard line integral into an easy double integral (or vice versa). If the line integral has nasty terms like \(e^{y^2}\) but \(Q_x - P_y\) simplifies nicely, use Green's to evaluate the double integral instead. Conversely, if the double integral is hard but the boundary is simple, convert to a line integral.

Fig 6.2 — Green's theorem: the line integral around the positively oriented (CCW) boundary \(\mathscr{C}\) equals the double integral of \(Q_x - P_y\) over the enclosed region \(\mathscr{R}\).

Key Consequences

Area via Green's Theorem

Setting \(P = -y\) and \(Q = x\) gives \(Q_x - P_y = 1 + 1 = 2\), so:

\[\text{Area of } \mathscr{R} = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\mathscr{C}} x\,dy - y\,dx\]

This converts an area computation into a line integral around the boundary — useful when the boundary has a nice parametrization but the region is hard to describe for a double integral.

Divergence Form of Green's Theorem

Let \(\mathbf{n}\) be the outward unit normal to \(\mathscr{C}\). Then Green's theorem can be rewritten as:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}} \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dA = \oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds\]

This is the 2D Divergence Theorem — it foreshadows the 3D Divergence Theorem in §6.8.

★ Regions with Holes

Green's theorem extends to regions with holes (like an annulus). Break the region into simply connected pieces, apply Green's to each, and the line integrals along the shared internal boundaries cancel. The result: the outer boundary is traversed CCW and inner boundaries CW.

Example 6.4 — Green's Theorem Simplifies a Hard Line Integral

Problem: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\oint_{\mathscr{C}} (\cos x - y^3)\,dx + e^{y^2}\,dy\) where \(\mathscr{C}\) is the boundary of the region between \(y = x^2\) and \(y = \sqrt{x}\), oriented CCW.

Solution

What we need to find: A line integral around a closed curve. The integrand contains \(e^{y^2}\), which has no elementary antiderivative — so direct evaluation is impossible.

Thought process: Since the curve is closed and positively oriented, apply Green's Theorem (Formula Sheet #52): \(\oint P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint(Q_x - P_y)\,dA\). If \(Q_x - P_y\) simplifies, the double integral will be tractable.

Step 1 — Identify \(P\) and \(Q\): \(P = \cos x - y^3\), \(Q = e^{y^2}\).

Step 2 — Compute \(Q_x - P_y\):

\[Q_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(e^{y^2}) = 0, \qquad P_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(\cos x - y^3) = -3y^2\] \[Q_x - P_y = 0 - (-3y^2) = 3y^2 \quad\checkmark\ \text{(much simpler!)}\]

Step 3 — Describe the region \(\mathscr{R}\): Between \(y = x^2\) and \(y = \sqrt{x}\). These curves intersect at \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\). Using vertical slices: \(x^2 \leq y \leq \sqrt{x}\) for \(0 \leq x \leq 1\).

Step 4 — Evaluate the double integral:

\[\oint_{\mathscr{C}} P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_{\mathscr{R}} 3y^2\,dA = \int_0^1\!\int_{x^2}^{\sqrt{x}} 3y^2\,dy\,dx\]

Inner integral: \(\int_{x^2}^{\sqrt{x}} 3y^2\,dy = \big[y^3\big]_{x^2}^{\sqrt{x}} = x^{3/2} - x^6\).

Outer integral: \(\int_0^1 (x^{3/2} - x^6)\,dx = \left[\frac{2x^{5/2}}{5} - \frac{x^7}{7}\right]_0^1 = \frac{2}{5} - \frac{1}{7} = \frac{14 - 5}{35} = \boxed{\frac{9}{35}}\)

★ Tip

Green's theorem turned an impossible line integral (containing \(e^{y^2}\)) into a routine polynomial double integral. Strategy: always compute \(Q_x - P_y\) first. If it simplifies, use the double integral side. If the double integral is hard but the boundary is simple, go the other direction.

📎 Uses: Green's Theorem (#52), Fubini — General (#44)

Example 6.5 — Area of an Annulus via Green's

Problem: Use \(\oint x\,dy - y\,dx\) to find the area of the annular region between the circles \(x^2+y^2=1\) and \(x^2+y^2=4\).

Solution

What we need to find: The area of a ring-shaped region using the Area via Green's Theorem formula (Formula Sheet #53): \(\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}\oint x\,dy - y\,dx\).

Thought process: The region has a hole (not simply connected). We traverse the outer boundary CCW and the inner boundary CW, then add the contributions. This is the standard extension of Green's to regions with holes.

Step 1 — Outer circle (\(r=2\), CCW): Parametrize as \(x = 2\cos t\), \(y = 2\sin t\), \(0 \leq t \leq 2\pi\).

Compute: \(dx = -2\sin t\,dt\), \(dy = 2\cos t\,dt\).

\[\oint_{\text{outer}} x\,dy - y\,dx = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\big(2\cos t \cdot 2\cos t - 2\sin t\cdot(-2\sin t)\big)\,dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 4(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t)\,dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 4\,dt = 8\pi\]

Step 2 — Inner circle (\(r=1\), CW): Same parametrization but with \(r=1\) and traversed clockwise, which negates the CCW result. CCW would give \(2\pi\), so CW gives \(-2\pi\).

Step 3 — Combine:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}\big(8\pi + (-2\pi)\big) = \frac{1}{2}(6\pi) = \boxed{3\pi}\]

Verification: Direct computation: \(\pi(2^2) - \pi(1^2) = 4\pi - \pi = 3\pi\). ✓

★ Tip

For regions with holes: outer boundary goes CCW (positive contribution) and inner boundary goes CW (negative contribution — subtracts the hole). Forgetting to reverse the inner circle's orientation is a common error.

📎 Uses: Area via Green's Theorem (#53)

§6.4 — Stokes' Theorem

Stokes' theorem generalizes Green's theorem from flat regions in the plane to curved surfaces in 3D. Where Green's relates a line integral around a closed curve to a double integral over the enclosed flat region, Stokes' relates a line integral around the boundary of a surface to a surface integral of the curl over that surface.

Stokes' Theorem

Let \(\mathscr{S}\) be an oriented smooth surface bounded by a simple closed curve \(\mathscr{C}\), with \(\mathbf{n}\) the unit normal to \(\mathscr{S}\) compatible with the orientation of \(\mathscr{C}\) (right-hand rule). If \(\mathbf{F}\) is a continuously differentiable vector field, then:

\[\oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_{\mathscr{S}} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\]
\(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\) = curl of \(\mathbf{F}\) (recall from §3.7) \(\mathbf{n}\) = outward unit normal to \(\mathscr{S}\) \(\mathscr{C}\) = boundary curve of \(\mathscr{S}\)
★ Right-Hand Rule for Orientation

Curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of \(\mathscr{C}\). Your thumb points in the direction of \(\mathbf{n}\). If you walk along \(\mathscr{C}\) in its positive direction with your head pointing in the direction of \(\mathbf{n}\), the surface is on your left.

Fig 6.3 — Stokes' theorem: the line integral of \(\mathbf{F}\) around the boundary curve \(\mathscr{C}\) equals the surface integral of the curl of \(\mathbf{F}\) over any surface \(\mathscr{S}\) bounded by \(\mathscr{C}\).

Key Consequences

1. Green's theorem is a special case. If \(\mathscr{S}\) is a flat region in the \(xy\)-plane, then \(\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k}\) and Stokes' reduces to Green's. Set \(R = 0\) — all \(z\)-terms vanish and you recover \(\oint P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint (Q_x - P_y)\,dA\).

2. Surface independence. If two different surfaces \(\mathscr{S}_1\) and \(\mathscr{S}_2\) share the same boundary curve \(\mathscr{C}\), then:

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}_1} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iint_{\mathscr{S}_2} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\]

Both equal \(\oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\). This is incredibly useful: if one surface is hard to integrate over, pick an easier one with the same boundary.

3. Conservative fields revisited. If \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) everywhere, Stokes' gives \(\oint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0\) for every closed curve — confirming that curl-free fields are conservative (recall from §3.7 and §4.3).

Example 6.6 — Verifying Stokes' Theorem

Problem: Let \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (y, -x, z^2)\). Verify Stokes' theorem where \(\mathscr{S}\) is the upper hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\), \(z \geq 0\), and \(\mathscr{C}\) is the circle \(x^2+y^2=4\) on the \(xy\)-plane.

Solution

What we need to find: Show that \(\oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_{\mathscr{S}} (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) — both sides of Stokes' Theorem (Formula Sheet #54).

Thought process: Compute both sides independently and verify they match. For the surface integral, we can use surface independence — replace the curved hemisphere with the flat disk (same boundary but much easier).

Side 1 — Line integral \(\oint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\):

Step 1: Parametrize \(\mathscr{C}\): \(x = 2\cos t\), \(y = 2\sin t\), \(z = 0\), \(0 \leq t \leq 2\pi\).

Step 2: \(\mathbf{r}'(t) = (-2\sin t, 2\cos t, 0)\) and \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = (2\sin t, -2\cos t, 0)\).

Step 3: Dot product: \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}' = (2\sin t)(-2\sin t) + (-2\cos t)(2\cos t) + 0 = -4\sin^2 t - 4\cos^2 t = -4\).

Step 4: Integrate: \(\oint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi}(-4)\,dt = -8\pi\).

Side 2 — Surface integral \(\iint(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\):

Step 1: Compute the curl using the determinant formula (recall from §3.7):

\[\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \det\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\\partial_x&\partial_y&\partial_z\\y&-x&z^2\end{pmatrix} = (0-0)\,\mathbf{i} - (0-0)\,\mathbf{j} + (-1-1)\,\mathbf{k} = (0,0,-2)\]

Step 2 (surface independence): Instead of integrating over the hemisphere, use the flat disk \(x^2+y^2 \leq 4\) in the \(xy\)-plane where \(\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k} = (0,0,1)\).

Step 3: \((\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n} = (0,0,-2)\cdot(0,0,1) = -2\) (constant).

Step 4: \(\iint_{\text{disk}} (-2)\,dA = -2 \cdot \text{Area}(\text{disk}) = -2 \cdot \pi(2^2) = -8\pi\).

Both sides give \(\boxed{-8\pi}\). ✓ Stokes' theorem verified.

★ Tip

We used surface independence — any surface with the same boundary gives the same curl flux. Replacing the hemisphere with the flat disk made the integral trivial. Always look for a simpler surface with the same boundary curve.

📎 Uses: Stokes' Theorem (#54), Curl (§3.7)

Example 6.7 — Stokes' on an Elliptic Surface

Problem: Let \(\mathbf{F} = x^2\,\mathbf{i} + 2x\,\mathbf{j} + z^2\,\mathbf{k}\). Evaluate \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \text{curl}\,\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) where \(\mathscr{S}\) is the surface interior to the ellipse \(4x^2+y^2=4\) on \(z=0\).

Solution

What we need to find: The surface integral of curl \(\mathbf{F}\) — i.e. the curl flux through the elliptic disk.

Thought process: By Stokes' Theorem (Formula Sheet #54), this equals the line integral \(\oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) around the ellipse boundary. The line integral is often easier than computing the curl and integrating over the surface.

Step 1 — Parametrize the ellipse \(\mathscr{C}\): \(4x^2+y^2=4\) gives \(x^2+y^2/4=1\), so \(x = \cos t\), \(y = 2\sin t\), \(z = 0\), \(0 \leq t \leq 2\pi\) (CCW).

Step 2 — Compute derivatives: \(dx = -\sin t\,dt\), \(dy = 2\cos t\,dt\), \(dz = 0\).

Step 3 — Evaluate \(\mathbf{F}\) on \(\mathscr{C}\): \(\mathbf{F}(\cos t, 2\sin t, 0) = (\cos^2 t,\;2\cos t,\;0)\).

Step 4 — Compute the dot product \(\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\):

\[\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \cos^2 t \cdot (-\sin t)\,dt + 2\cos t \cdot 2\cos t\,dt + 0 = (-\cos^2 t\sin t + 4\cos^2 t)\,dt\]

Step 5 — Integrate over \([0, 2\pi]\):

First term: \(\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2 t\sin t\,dt\). Substituting \(u = \cos t\): over a full period the net change is zero, so this integral is \(0\).

Second term: \(4\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2 t\,dt = 4\cdot\pi = 4\pi\) (using the half-angle identity \(\cos^2 t = \frac{1+\cos 2t}{2}\)).

Final answer: \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \text{curl}\,\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = 0 + 4\pi = \boxed{4\pi}\)

★ Tip

Stokes' converts a surface integral of the curl into a boundary line integral. For flat surfaces (like \(z=0\)), the line integral is usually simpler. Watch for odd-function integrals over full periods — they always vanish.

📎 Uses: Stokes' Theorem (#54)

§6.5 — Triple Integrals

Just as double integrals extend single integrals to 2D regions, triple integrals extend to 3D solid regions. Where a double integral gives area (when \(f=1\)) or volume under a surface, a triple integral gives volume (when \(f=1\)) or "hypervolume" in 4D (for general \(f\)).

Triple Integral

Let \(f(x,y,z)\) be continuous on a bounded solid region \(\mathscr{T} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3\). The triple integral is:

\[\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} f(x,y,z)\,dV = \lim_{\|\mathscr{P}\|\to 0} \sum_{j=1}^{n}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{k=1}^{p} f(x_i, y_j, z_k)\,\Delta V_{ijk}\]

where \(\Delta V_{ijk}\) is the volume of each sub-box in the partition.

Setting \(f = 1\) gives \(\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} dV = \text{Volume of }\mathscr{T}\).

Fubini's Theorem for Triple Integrals

A triple integral can be evaluated as a nested iterated integral. There are 6 possible orderings, depending on which variable you integrate first. A typical form:

\[\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} f\,dV = \int_a^b\!\int_{f_1(x)}^{f_2(x)}\!\int_{g(x,y)}^{h(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx\]
\(a \leq x \leq b\) = outermost bounds (constants) \(f_1 \leq y \leq f_2\) = middle bounds (functions of \(x\)) \(g \leq z \leq h\) = innermost bounds (functions of \(x\) and \(y\))

Always evaluate from the inside out. The inner integral has variable limits; the outer integral always has constant limits.

★ Setting Up Limits

To set up a triple integral \(dz\,dy\,dx\): 1. Fix \(x\) and \(y\), find the \(z\)-bounds by solving for where the solid's top and bottom surfaces are. 2. Project the solid onto the \(xy\)-plane to get a 2D region; set up the \(dy\,dx\) bounds as a double integral over that region. 3. The outermost bounds are always constants (the overall range of the outermost variable).

Applications

Mass: If \(\delta(x,y,z)\) is density, then \(M = \iiint_{\mathscr{T}} \delta\,dV\).

Center of mass: \(\bar{x} = \frac{1}{M}\iiint x\,\delta\,dV\), \(\bar{y} = \frac{1}{M}\iiint y\,\delta\,dV\), \(\bar{z} = \frac{1}{M}\iiint z\,\delta\,dV\).

Example 6.8 — Triple Integral over a Tetrahedron

Problem: Evaluate \(\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} x\,dV\) where \(\mathscr{T}\) is the tetrahedron bounded by \(x+y+z=1\) and the coordinate planes in the first octant.

Solution

What we need to find: A triple integral of \(f = x\) over a solid tetrahedron — this could represent a moment calculation if \(x\) is a distance and we're finding the center of mass.

Thought process: The tetrahedron has flat faces along coordinate planes, so rectangular coordinates with Fubini for Triple Integrals (Formula Sheet #55) is natural. We integrate in the order \(dz\,dy\,dx\) — from inside out.

Step 1 — Find the limits:

Outermost (\(x\)): \(x\) ranges from 0 to 1 (where the plane meets the \(x\)-axis).

Middle (\(y\)): Fix \(x\). Set \(z=0\) in \(x+y+z=1\): \(y\) goes from 0 to \(1-x\).

Innermost (\(z\)): Fix \(x\) and \(y\). \(z\) goes from 0 (coordinate plane) to \(1-x-y\) (the slanted plane).

Step 2 — Write the iterated integral:

\[\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} x\,dV = \int_0^1\!\int_0^{1-x}\!\int_0^{1-x-y} x\,dz\,dy\,dx\]

Step 3 — Inner integral (integrate w.r.t. \(z\), treating \(x\) and \(y\) as constants):

\[\int_0^{1-x-y} x\,dz = x\big[z\big]_0^{1-x-y} = x(1-x-y)\]

Step 4 — Middle integral (integrate w.r.t. \(y\)):

\[\int_0^{1-x} x(1-x-y)\,dy = x\left[(1-x)y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{1-x} = x\left[(1-x)^2 - \frac{(1-x)^2}{2}\right] = \frac{x(1-x)^2}{2}\]

Step 5 — Outer integral (integrate w.r.t. \(x\)):

\[\int_0^1 \frac{x(1-x)^2}{2}\,dx = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1(x - 2x^2 + x^3)\,dx = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{2x^3}{3}+\frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^1\] \[= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{2}{3}+\frac{1}{4}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{6-8+3}{12} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{12} = \boxed{\frac{1}{24}}\]
★ Tip

For tetrahedra bounded by a plane \(ax+by+cz=d\) and coordinate planes: the inner limits come from the plane and the outer limits come from the axis intercepts. Always expand from inside out — the innermost integral has the most variable-dependent limits.

📎 Uses: Triple Integral — Fubini (#55)

§6.6 — Cylindrical & Spherical Coordinates

Just as polar coordinates simplified double integrals over circular regions (recall from §5.4), cylindrical and spherical coordinates simplify triple integrals over cylindrical and spherical regions. The key is the Jacobian — the volume magnification factor when changing coordinates.

Cylindrical Coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates extend polar coordinates to 3D by adding a \(z\)-axis:

\[x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta, \quad z = z\]

Jacobian: \(J = r\). The volume element is \(dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\).

\[\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} f\,dV = \iiint_{\mathscr{T}'} f(r\cos\theta,\,r\sin\theta,\,z)\;r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\]
\(r \geq 0\) = distance from \(z\)-axis \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\) = angle in \(xy\)-plane \(z \in \mathbb{R}\) = height

Use when: the region or integrand involves \(x^2+y^2\) (circles/cylinders around the \(z\)-axis).

Spherical Coordinates

For regions involving spheres or cones:

\[x = \rho\sin\varphi\cos\theta, \quad y = \rho\sin\varphi\sin\theta, \quad z = \rho\cos\varphi\]

Jacobian: \(J = \rho^2\sin\varphi\). The volume element is \(dV = \rho^2\sin\varphi\,d\rho\,d\theta\,d\varphi\).

\[\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} f\,dV = \iiint_{\mathscr{T}'} f(\rho\sin\varphi\cos\theta,\,\rho\sin\varphi\sin\theta,\,\rho\cos\varphi)\;\rho^2\sin\varphi\,d\rho\,d\theta\,d\varphi\]
\(\rho \geq 0\) = distance from origin \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\) = azimuthal angle (longitude) \(0 \leq \varphi \leq \pi\) = polar angle from positive \(z\)-axis (colatitude)

Use when: the region or integrand involves \(x^2+y^2+z^2\) (spheres and cones).

Fig 6.4 — Cylindrical coordinates \((r, \theta, z)\) and spherical coordinates \((\rho, \theta, \varphi)\). Note: \(\varphi\) is measured from the positive \(z\)-axis (colatitude), not from the equator.
★ Don't Forget the Jacobian!

\(dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\) in cylindrical. \(dV = \rho^2\sin\varphi\,d\rho\,d\theta\,d\varphi\) in spherical. Forgetting the factor \(r\) or \(\rho^2\sin\varphi\) is one of the most common exam mistakes.

Example 6.9 — Volume of a Sphere (Spherical)

Problem: Find the volume of a sphere of radius \(R\) using spherical coordinates.

Solution

What we need to find: \(V = \iiint_{\mathscr{T}} dV\) where \(\mathscr{T}\) is the ball \(x^2+y^2+z^2 \leq R^2\).

Thought process: The region is a full sphere centered at the origin — a textbook case for Spherical Coordinates (Formula Sheet #57): \(dV = \rho^2\sin\varphi\,d\rho\,d\theta\,d\varphi\).

Step 1 — Set limits in spherical: \(0 \leq \rho \leq R\) (origin to surface), \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\) (full revolution), \(0 \leq \varphi \leq \pi\) (north pole to south pole).

Step 2 — Write the integral with the Jacobian \(\rho^2\sin\varphi\):

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^{\pi}\!\int_0^R \rho^2\sin\varphi\,d\rho\,d\varphi\,d\theta\]

Step 3 — Separate variables (integrand and limits factor independently):

\[V = \underbrace{\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta}_{=\,2\pi}\cdot\underbrace{\int_0^{\pi}\sin\varphi\,d\varphi}_{=\,[-\cos\varphi]_0^{\pi}\,=\,2}\cdot\underbrace{\int_0^R\rho^2\,d\rho}_{=\,R^3/3}\]

Step 4 — Multiply:

\[V = 2\pi \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{R^3}{3} = \boxed{\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}}\]
★ Tip

When the integrand and limits all factor into single-variable pieces, the triple integral splits into a product of three single integrals. Memorize: \(\int_0^{\pi}\sin\varphi\,d\varphi = 2\) and \(\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta = 2\pi\). These appear in almost every spherical coordinate problem.

📎 Uses: Spherical Coordinates (#57)

Example 6.10 — Cylindrical Coordinates for a Cylinder Volume

Problem: Find the volume of the solid inside the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=4\) between \(z=0\) and \(z = 2 - r\cos\theta\) (i.e. \(z = 2-x\)).

Solution

What we need to find: Volume of a solid bounded below by \(z=0\), above by the tilted plane \(z=2-x\), and laterally by the cylinder \(r=2\).

Thought process: The cylinder \(x^2+y^2=4\) becomes \(r=2\) in cylindrical. The plane \(z=2-x\) becomes \(z=2-r\cos\theta\). Use Cylindrical Coordinates (Formula Sheet #56): \(dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\).

Step 1 — Set limits: \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\), \(0 \leq r \leq 2\), \(0 \leq z \leq 2-r\cos\theta\).

Step 2 — Inner integral (\(z\)): \(\int_0^{2-r\cos\theta} r\,dz = r(2-r\cos\theta)\).

Step 3 — Middle integral (\(r\)): expand and integrate:

\[\int_0^2 r(2-r\cos\theta)\,dr = \int_0^2(2r - r^2\cos\theta)\,dr = \left[r^2 - \frac{r^3}{3}\cos\theta\right]_0^2 = 4 - \frac{8}{3}\cos\theta\]

Step 4 — Outer integral (\(\theta\)): since \(\int_0^{2\pi}\cos\theta\,d\theta = 0\):

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\left(4 - \frac{8}{3}\cos\theta\right)d\theta = 4(2\pi) - \frac{8}{3}(0) = \boxed{8\pi}\]
★ Tip

The \(\cos\theta\) term vanishes over a full revolution \([0, 2\pi]\) — tilting a plane doesn't change the total volume enclosed by a symmetric cylinder. If the upper surface were horizontal (\(z=2\)), you'd get \(\pi(2^2)(2) = 8\pi\) — the same answer! This is a great sanity check.

📎 Uses: Cylindrical Coordinates (#56)

§6.7 — Describing Solids

A critical skill for triple integrals is describing standard solid regions in cylindrical and spherical coordinates. Here's a reference guide:

Common Surfaces in Each Coordinate System

Vertical cylinder \(x^2+y^2=a^2\)\(r = a\)\(\rho\sin\varphi = a\)
Cone \(z = c\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\)\(z = cr\)\(\varphi = \varphi_0\) where \(\tan\varphi_0 = 1/c\)

★ Choosing Coordinates

Cylindrical: Use when the region has circular symmetry about the \(z\)-axis and the \(z\)-bounds are given by functions. The integrand or boundary involves \(x^2+y^2\).

Spherical: Use when the region is bounded by spheres or cones centered at the origin. The integrand or boundary involves \(x^2+y^2+z^2\).

Rectangular: Use for boxes, tetrahedra, and regions with flat boundaries parallel to coordinate planes.

§6.8 — The Divergence Theorem

The Divergence Theorem (also called Gauss's Theorem) is the 3D analogue of the 2D Divergence form of Green's theorem. Where Green's (divergence form) says \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}} \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dA = \oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds\), the Divergence Theorem extends this to 3D:

The Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem)

Let \(\mathscr{T}\) be a solid region in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with smooth closed boundary surface \(\mathscr{S}\) and outward unit normal \(\mathbf{n}\). If \(\mathbf{F}\) is continuously differentiable, then:

\[\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV = \iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\]
\(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = P_x + Q_y + R_z\) (recall from §3.7) \(\mathscr{S}\) = closed boundary of solid \(\mathscr{T}\) \(\mathbf{n}\) = outward normal

In words: the total "source strength" inside a region equals the net outward flux through its boundary.

Fig 6.5 — The Divergence Theorem: the triple integral of \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\) over the solid \(\mathscr{T}\) equals the flux of \(\mathbf{F}\) through its closed boundary \(\mathscr{S}\).

Key Consequences

1. Curl flux over a closed surface is zero: \(\displaystyle\iint_{\mathscr{S}} (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = 0\) for any closed surface, because \(\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0\) always (recall from §3.7).

2. Volume via flux: Setting \(\mathbf{F} = (x,0,0)\) or \((0,y,0)\) or \((0,0,z)\) (each has \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}=1\)) gives \(\text{Vol}(\mathscr{T}) = \iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\).

3. Regions with holes: The Divergence Theorem extends to regions bounded by two or more closed surfaces (like a hollow ball). The inner surface is traversed with inward normal.

The Three Big Theorems — Unified View

All three theorems have the same structure: an integral over a region equals an integral over its boundary.

TheoremInteriorBoundary
Green's\(\iint_{\mathscr{R}} (Q_x-P_y)\,dA\)\(\oint_{\mathscr{C}} P\,dx+Q\,dy\)
Stokes'\(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\)\(\oint_{\mathscr{C}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\)
Divergence\(\iiint_{\mathscr{T}} \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\)\(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\)

Each connects an integral one dimension higher (over the interior) to an integral one dimension lower (over the boundary). Green's: 2D↔1D. Stokes': 2D↔1D (curved). Divergence: 3D↔2D.

Example 6.11 — Divergence Theorem on a Tetrahedron

Problem: Let \(\mathbf{F} = (x^2+1)\,\mathbf{i} + (xz^2-2z)\,\mathbf{j} + (2xz+\cos y)\,\mathbf{k}\). Find the flux through the surface of the tetrahedron bounded by \(x+2y+3z=6\) and the coordinate planes.

Solution

What we need to find: The total outward flux \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) through a closed surface (4 triangular faces of a tetrahedron).

Thought process: Computing flux directly would require 4 separate surface integrals (one per face) — very tedious! Since \(\mathscr{S}\) is a closed surface, use the Divergence Theorem (Formula Sheet #58): \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_{\mathscr{T}} \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\). This converts 4 surface integrals into 1 triple integral.

Step 1 — Compute div \(\mathbf{F}\): (recall from §3.7: \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = P_x + Q_y + R_z\))

\[P_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2+1) = 2x, \quad Q_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xz^2-2z) = 0, \quad R_z = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2xz+\cos y) = 2x\] \[\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = 2x + 0 + 2x = 4x\]

Step 2 — Find the vertices: The tetrahedron is bounded by \(x+2y+3z=6\) and the three coordinate planes. Vertices: \((6,0,0)\), \((0,3,0)\), \((0,0,2)\), and the origin.

Step 3 — Set up limits (order \(dz\,dy\,dx\)):

Outer: \(0 \leq x \leq 6\). Middle: \(0 \leq y \leq (6-x)/2\). Inner: \(0 \leq z \leq (6-x-2y)/3\).

Step 4 — Inner integral (\(z\)): \(\int_0^{(6-x-2y)/3} 4x\,dz = 4x \cdot \frac{6-x-2y}{3}\).

Step 5 — Middle integral (\(y\)): \(\frac{4x}{3}\int_0^{(6-x)/2}(6-x-2y)\,dy\). Let \(u = 6-x-2y\):

\[= \frac{4x}{3}\cdot\frac{(6-x)^2}{4} = \frac{x(6-x)^2}{3}\]

Step 6 — Outer integral (\(x\)): expand \(x(6-x)^2 = x(36 - 12x + x^2) = 36x - 12x^2 + x^3\):

\[\frac{1}{3}\int_0^6(36x - 12x^2 + x^3)\,dx = \frac{1}{3}\left[18x^2 - 4x^3 + \frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^6 = \frac{1}{3}(648 - 864 + 324) = \frac{108}{3} = \boxed{36}\]
★ Tip

The Divergence Theorem is ideal for closed surfaces with many faces. Without it, this problem requires computing 4 separate surface integrals. With it, one triple integral does the job. Always check: is the surface closed? If yes, compute div \(\mathbf{F}\) and see if the triple integral is simpler.

📎 Uses: Divergence Theorem (#58), Divergence (§3.7), Triple Integral — Fubini (#55)

Example 6.12 — Divergence Theorem on a Sphere

Problem: Find the outward flux of \(\mathbf{F} = (x^3, y^3, z^3)\) through the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = a^2\).

Solution

What we need to find: The outward flux \(\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) through a sphere — a closed surface.

Thought process: The sphere is a closed surface, so use the Divergence Theorem (Formula Sheet #58). Compute div \(\mathbf{F}\) and integrate over the ball. Since the divergence involves \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2\), Spherical Coordinates (Formula Sheet #57) are the natural choice.

Step 1 — Compute div \(\mathbf{F}\):

\[\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^3) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(z^3) = 3x^2 + 3y^2 + 3z^2 = 3(x^2+y^2+z^2)\]

In spherical coordinates: \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = 3\rho^2\).

Step 2 — Set up the triple integral in spherical: The ball \(\mathscr{T}\): \(0 \leq \rho \leq a\), \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\), \(0 \leq \varphi \leq \pi\).

\[\iint_{\mathscr{S}} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_{\mathscr{T}} 3\rho^2 \cdot \underbrace{\rho^2\sin\varphi}_{\text{Jacobian}}\,d\rho\,d\varphi\,d\theta = \iiint 3\rho^4\sin\varphi\,d\rho\,d\varphi\,d\theta\]

Step 3 — Separate and evaluate (all limits and integrand factor):

\[= 3\underbrace{\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta}_{2\pi}\cdot\underbrace{\int_0^{\pi}\sin\varphi\,d\varphi}_{2}\cdot\underbrace{\int_0^a\rho^4\,d\rho}_{a^5/5}\]

Step 4 — Multiply:

\[= 3 \cdot 2\pi \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{a^5}{5} = \boxed{\frac{12\pi a^5}{5}}\]
★ Tip

When div \(\mathbf{F}\) simplifies to a function of \(\rho\) only (here \(3\rho^2\)), the spherical integral separates into three independent single integrals — making the computation fast. The key insight: \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2\) is the reason to use spherical coordinates.

📎 Uses: Divergence Theorem (#58), Spherical Coordinates (#57)

★ When to Use Which Theorem

Green's: Closed curve in the plane. Convert between \(\oint P\,dx+Q\,dy\) and \(\iint(Q_x-P_y)\,dA\).

Stokes': Open surface with boundary curve in 3D. Convert between \(\oint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) and \(\iint(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\). Or swap surfaces with the same boundary.

Divergence: Closed surface enclosing a solid. Convert between \(\iint\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) and \(\iiint\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\).

Keyword clue: If you see a closed surface → Divergence. If you see a surface with a boundary curve → Stokes'. If you see a closed curve in 2D → Green's.
