Chapter 5

Double Integrals & Surfaces

Double integrals over rectangular and general regions, change of variables to polar coordinates, and sketching/parameterizing surfaces in 3D.

§5.1 — Describing Planar Regions

Before integrating over a region, you need to describe it in a way that gives you integration limits. The key idea: describe the region using slices — either vertical (fixing \(x\)) or horizontal (fixing \(y\)).

x-simple Region (Type I)

A region \(\mathscr{R}\subseteq\mathbb{R}^2\) is x-simple if every vertical line through \(\mathscr{R}\) intersects it in at most two points. Equivalently:

\[\mathscr{R}_{yx}=\{(x,y): f_1(x)\leq y\leq f_2(x),\; a\leq x\leq b\}\]

The outer variable is \(x\) (constant limits), the inner is \(y\) (limits depend on \(x\)). Integrate dy dx.

y-simple Region (Type II)

A region \(\mathscr{R}\) is y-simple if every horizontal line through \(\mathscr{R}\) intersects it in at most two points:

\[\mathscr{R}_{xy}=\{(x,y): g_1(y)\leq x\leq g_2(y),\; c\leq y\leq d\}\]

The outer variable is \(y\), the inner is \(x\). Integrate dx dy.

★ Memory Trick: Subscript = Integration Order

\(\mathscr{R}_{yx}\) → integrate \(y\) first, then \(x\) → dy dx (vertical slices).

\(\mathscr{R}_{xy}\) → integrate \(x\) first, then \(y\) → dx dy (horizontal slices).

The innermost integral has limits that depend on the outer variable. The outer integral has constant limits.

Fig 5.1 — Left: x-simple region with vertical slices (integrate dy then dx). Right: y-simple region with horizontal slices (integrate dx then dy).
Example 5.1 — Region Bounded by Parabolas (Ex. 222)

Describe the region bounded by \(y=x^2\), \(y=-x^2\), and \(x=1\) using both vertical and horizontal slices.

Show Solution

Vertical slices (x-simple): Fix \(x\in[0,1]\). A vertical line enters at \(y=-x^2\) (bottom) and exits at \(y=x^2\) (top).

\(\mathscr{R}_{yx}=\{(x,y): -x^2\leq y\leq x^2,\; 0\leq x\leq 1\}\)

Horizontal slices (y-simple): Need to split into two regions since the boundary curves change form depending on whether \(y>0\) or \(y<0\).

For \(y\geq 0\): \(x\) ranges from \(\sqrt{y}\) to \(1\), with \(0\leq y\leq 1\).

For \(y<0\): \(x\) ranges from \(\sqrt{-y}\) to \(1\), with \(-1\leq y\leq 0\).

\(\mathscr{R}_{xy}=\{(x,y):\sqrt{y}\leq x\leq 1,\;0\leq y\leq 1\}\cup\{(x,y):\sqrt{-y}\leq x\leq 1,\;-1\leq y\leq 0\}\)

★ Takeaway: Vertical slices are much simpler here — one description vs. a union of two.

§5.2 — Double Integrals and Iterated Integrals

The double integral generalizes the definite integral to two dimensions. Just as a single integral sums \(f(x)\Delta x\) over an interval, a double integral sums \(f(x,y)\Delta A\) over a region in the plane.

Definition — Double Integral

Partition a region \(\mathscr{R}\) into small rectangles \(\mathscr{R}_{ij}\) of area \(\Delta A_{ij}\). Pick \((x_i,y_j)\in\mathscr{R}_{ij}\). If the limit exists:

Double Integral
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\lim_{\|\mathscr{P}\|\to 0}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\sum_{i=1}^{m}f(x_i,y_j)\,\Delta A_{ij}\]
dA — area element (dx dy or dy dx)‖𝒫‖ — mesh of partition → 0𝒬 — region of integration

Geometric Meaning

If \(f(x,y)\geq 0\), then \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f\,dA=\) volume under the surface \(z=f(x,y)\) above \(\mathscr{R}\).

If \(f(x,y)=1\), then \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}1\,dA=\) area of \(\mathscr{R}\).

Fubini's Theorem

Fubini's Theorem (Rectangles)

If \(f\) is continuous on a rectangle \(\mathscr{R}=\{(x,y):a\leq x\leq b,\;c\leq y\leq d\}\), then:

Fubini — Rectangular Region
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx=\int_c^d\!\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\]

For rectangles, the order of integration doesn't matter — you can integrate in either order.

Fubini's Theorem (General Regions)

For non-rectangular regions, use the region description to set limits:

Fubini — x-simple (vertical slices)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f\,dA=\int_a^b\!\int_{f_1(x)}^{f_2(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\]
Fubini — y-simple (horizontal slices)
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f\,dA=\int_c^d\!\int_{g_1(y)}^{g_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\]
★ Anatomy of an Iterated Integral

In \(\int_a^b\int_{f_1(x)}^{f_2(x)}f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\):

• Inner integral: integrate w.r.t. \(y\) while holding \(x\) constant. Limits depend on \(x\).

• Outer integral: integrate the result w.r.t. \(x\). Limits are constants.

• Rule: the innermost differential (\(dy\)) matches the subscript order in \(\mathscr{R}_{yx}\).

Example 5.2 — Iterated Integral over a Rectangle (Ex. 231)

Evaluate \(\int_{-1}^{1}\int_0^3(3x^2+y)\,dx\,dy\).

Show Solution
1
Inner integral (w.r.t. \(x\), hold \(y\) constant): \(\int_0^3(3x^2+y)\,dx=[x^3+yx]_0^3=27+3y\).
2
Outer integral: \(\int_{-1}^{1}(27+3y)\,dy=[27y+\frac{3y^2}{2}]_{-1}^{1}=(27+\frac{3}{2})-(-27+\frac{3}{2})=54\).

Answer: 54

Note: the \(3y\) term vanishes because \(y\) is odd on \([-1,1]\).

Example 5.3 — Double Integral with Variable Limits (Ex. 232)

Evaluate \(\int_0^{1/2}\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x}}2xy\,dy\,dx\).

Show Solution
1
Inner: \(\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x}}2xy\,dy=2x\cdot\frac{y^2}{2}\Big|_0^{\sqrt{1-x}}=x(1-x)\).
2
Outer: \(\int_0^{1/2}(x-x^2)\,dx=[\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^3}{3}]_0^{1/2}=\frac{1}{8}-\frac{1}{24}=\frac{1}{12}\).

Answer: \(\frac{1}{12}\)

Reversing the Order of Integration

Sometimes one order produces an integral you can't evaluate, but reversing the order makes it tractable. To reverse: sketch the region, then re-describe it using the other slice direction.

Example 5.4 — Reversing Integration Order (Ex. 247)

Evaluate \(\int_0^1\int_0^{\text{Arccos}\,y}\sin(\pi\sin x)\,dx\,dy\) by interchanging the order.

Show Solution

Original: horizontal slices, \(\mathscr{R}_{xy}=\{(x,y): 0\leq x\leq\text{Arccos}\,y,\;0\leq y\leq 1\}\).

Reverse: vertical slices. Since \(x\leq\text{Arccos}\,y\iff y\leq\cos x\), and \(x\in[0,\pi/2]\):

\(\mathscr{R}_{yx}=\{(x,y): 0\leq y\leq\cos x,\; 0\leq x\leq\pi/2\}\).

1
\(\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\cos x}\sin(\pi\sin x)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin(\pi\sin x)\cos x\,dx\).
2
Let \(u=\sin x\), \(du=\cos x\,dx\). Limits: \(0\to 1\). So \(=\int_0^1\sin(\pi u)\,du=-\frac{1}{\pi}\cos(\pi u)\Big|_0^1=\frac{2}{\pi}\).

Answer: \(\frac{2}{\pi}\)

§5.3 — Volume Under a Surface

Volume computation is a direct application of double integrals:

Volume Under a Surface
\[V=\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA\]
f(x,y) ≥ 0 — height of the surface above the xy-plane𝒬 — projection of the surface onto the xy-plane

For volume between two surfaces \(z=f(x,y)\) (top) and \(z=g(x,y)\) (bottom) over \(\mathscr{R}\):

\[V=\iint_{\mathscr{R}}[f(x,y)-g(x,y)]\,dA\]

Example 5.5 — Volume Under a Paraboloid (Ex. 248)

Find the volume of the solid under \(z=4-x^2-y^2\) and above the \(xy\)-plane.

Show Solution

\(z=0\) when \(x^2+y^2=4\), so \(\mathscr{R}\) is the disk of radius 2. Using vertical slices:

\(V=\int_{-2}^{2}\int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}(4-x^2-y^2)\,dy\,dx\)

This is much easier in polar coordinates (§5.4): \(V=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(4-r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta=2\pi\int_0^2(4r-r^3)\,dr=2\pi[2r^2-\frac{r^4}{4}]_0^2=2\pi(8-4)=8\pi\).

Answer: \(V=8\pi\)

§5.4 — Change of Variables / Polar Coordinates

Just as single-variable integrals use \(u\)-substitution, double integrals use a change of variables. The key extra ingredient: the Jacobian determinant, which accounts for how the transformation stretches or compresses area.

Jacobian Matrix and Determinant

For a transformation \(x=g(u,v),\;y=h(u,v)\), the Jacobian matrix is:

\[\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}&\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}\\\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}&\frac{\partial y}{\partial v}\end{pmatrix}\]

The Jacobian determinant \(J(u,v)=\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\) is the absolute value of the determinant of this matrix.

Change of Variables Formula
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\iint_{\mathscr{S}}f\!\big(g(u,v),h(u,v)\big)\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,dA\]
𝒬 — original region (xy-plane)𝒮 — transformed region (uv-plane)|∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)| — Jacobian (area scaling factor)

Polar Coordinates — The Most Important Special Case

For regions with circular symmetry, use \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\). The Jacobian is:

\[\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)}=\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&\sin\theta\\-r\sin\theta&r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}=r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta=r\]

Double Integral in Polar Coordinates
\[\iint_{\mathscr{R}}f(x,y)\,dA=\iint_{\mathscr{S}}f(r\cos\theta,\,r\sin\theta)\;\boldsymbol{r}\,dr\,d\theta\]
r — the Jacobian (DON'T forget!)dA = r dr dθ — polar area elementx²+y² = r² — simplifies circular regions
★ When to Use Polar Coordinates

Switch to polar when:

• The region involves circles or arcs (\(x^2+y^2=R^2\))

• The integrand contains \(x^2+y^2\)

• The region is a sector, annulus, or cardioid

Common trap: forgetting the extra factor of \(\boldsymbol{r}\) in \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\)!

Fig 5.2 — The polar area element dA = r dr dθ. The extra factor of r comes from the arc length of the angular side: width = dr, height = r dθ.
Area in Polar Coordinates
\[\text{Area}(\mathscr{R})=\iint_{\mathscr{R}}r\,dr\,d\theta\]
Example 5.6 — Polar Double Integral (Ex. 253)

Evaluate \(\iint_{\mathscr{R}}(x^2+y^2)\,dA\) where \(\mathscr{R}\) is the disk \(x^2+y^2\leq 4\).

Show Solution

In polar: \(x^2+y^2=r^2\), \(\mathscr{R}:\;0\leq r\leq 2,\;0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi\).

1
\(\iint r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\,dr\,d\theta\).
2
Inner: \(\int_0^2 r^3\,dr=\frac{r^4}{4}\Big|_0^2=4\). Outer: \(\int_0^{2\pi}4\,d\theta=8\pi\).

Answer: \(8\pi\)

Example 5.7 — Area of a Cardioid (Ex. 266)

Find the area enclosed by the cardioid \(r=2(1-\cos\theta)\).

Show Solution

\(\mathscr{R}=\{(r,\theta): 0\leq r\leq 2(1-\cos\theta),\; 0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi\}\).

1
\(A=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{2(1-\cos\theta)}r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{r^2}{2}\Big|_0^{2(1-\cos\theta)}d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}2(1-\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta\).
2
Expand: \(2(1-2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)=2-4\cos\theta+2\cos^2\theta=2-4\cos\theta+1+\cos 2\theta=3-4\cos\theta+\cos 2\theta\).
3
\(\int_0^{2\pi}(3-4\cos\theta+\cos 2\theta)\,d\theta=6\pi-0+0=6\pi\).

Answer: \(A=6\pi\)

§5.5 — Three-Dimensional Plots / Quadric Surfaces

Surfaces in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) are sketched by computing their traces: the curves of intersection with coordinate planes (or planes parallel to them). Set one variable to a constant and identify the resulting 2D curve.

Trace (Cross-Section)

A trace of a surface \(f(x,y,z)=0\) on the plane \(z=c\) is the curve \(\{(x,y,c): f(x,y,c)=0\}\). Similarly for \(x=c\) or \(y=c\) traces. Sketch traces at several values of \(c\) and "stack" them to visualize the surface.

Elliptic Paraboloid\(z=\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}\)EllipsesBowl (opens up)

Elliptic Cone\(\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}-\frac{z^2}{c^2}=0\)Ellipses (z≠0), point (z=0)Double cone

★ How to Identify a Quadric

Step 1: Complete the square if needed to put in standard form.

Step 2: Count the number of squared terms and check their signs.

• All same sign = ellipsoid or cylinder. • Two same, one opposite = hyperboloid. • Two squared + one linear = paraboloid. • One negative on RHS = two sheets. • RHS = 0 → cone.

Example 5.8 — Identifying and Sketching a Surface (Ex. 275)

Identify and sketch the surface \(z=x^2+4y^2\).

Show Solution

Form: \(z=\frac{x^2}{1}+\frac{y^2}{1/4}\) — this is an elliptic paraboloid opening upward.

z-traces: Set \(z=c\geq 0\): \(x^2+4y^2=c\), which is an ellipse with semi-axes \(\sqrt{c}\) and \(\sqrt{c}/2\). At \(z=0\): the origin (vertex). The ellipses grow as \(z\) increases.

x-traces: Set \(x=c\): \(z=c^2+4y^2\), parabola opening upward.

y-traces: Set \(y=c\): \(z=x^2+4c^2\), parabola opening upward.

§5.6 — Parametric Equations of Surfaces

A curve is a 1D object, so one parameter \(t\) is enough to trace it out. A surface is a 2D object living in 3D space — you need to move in two independent directions to cover it. That means two parameters.

Think of it like an address system: on a curve, a single number \(t\) tells you where you are. On a surface, you need a pair \((u,v)\) — like latitude and longitude on Earth, or row and column on a grid.

Definition — Parametric Surface

A parametric surface is a vector-valued function of two variables:

Parametric Surface
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=\big(x(u,v),\;y(u,v),\;z(u,v)\big),\quad (u,v)\in D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^2\]
u, v — two independent parametersD — parameter domain (rectangle, disk, etc.)r(u,v) — position vector of a point on the surface

As \((u,v)\) ranges over the domain \(D\), the tip of \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\) traces out the surface. Fixing \(u=c\) and varying \(v\) gives a v-curve on the surface; fixing \(v=c\) and varying \(u\) gives a u-curve. Together these form a coordinate grid on the surface.

Common Parametrizations

1. Sphere of Radius R

To parametrize a sphere, use the same idea as geographic coordinates. Every point on the sphere is uniquely determined by two angles:

• \(u\) = longitude — the angle measured around the equator (like going east/west). Ranges over \([0,2\pi]\).

• \(v\) = latitude — the angle measured above or below the equator (like going north/south). Ranges over \([-\pi/2,\pi/2]\).

Sphere Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(R\cos v\cos u,\;R\cos v\sin u,\;R\sin v)\]
u ∈ [0, 2π] — longitude (around equator)v ∈ [−π/2, π/2] — latitude (pole to pole)R — radius

How to derive it: Start with a point \(P\) on the sphere. Drop it to the \(xy\)-plane: its projection \(Q\) is at distance \(R\cos v\) from the origin (the equatorial radius shrinks as you move toward the poles). In the \(xy\)-plane, \(Q\) makes angle \(u\) with the \(x\)-axis, so \(x=R\cos v\cos u\) and \(y=R\cos v\sin u\). The height is simply \(z=R\sin v\).

Fig 5.3 — Sphere parametrization. Longitude u sweeps around the equator; latitude v measures height above it. The equatorial radius at height v is R cos v.
★ Why Not Spherical Coordinates?

This parametrization uses latitude \(v\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]\), not the physics convention of polar angle \(\phi\in[0,\pi]\) measured from the north pole. The textbook's convention matches geography: \(v=0\) is the equator, \(v=\pi/2\) is the north pole, \(v=-\pi/2\) is the south pole.

2. Graph Surface \(z=f(x,y)\)

If the surface is already given as \(z=f(x,y)\) — i.e., you can solve for \(z\) explicitly — then the parametrization is trivial: just let \(u=x\) and \(v=y\).

Graph Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(u,\,v,\,f(u,v)),\quad (u,v)\in\mathscr{R}\]

The parameter domain \(\mathscr{R}\) is just the region in the \(xy\)-plane over which the surface is defined. This is the simplest possible parametrization — you're using \(x\) and \(y\) themselves as the two parameters.

Example: The paraboloid \(z=x^2+y^2\) over the unit disk becomes \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(u,v,u^2+v^2)\) for \(u^2+v^2\leq 1\).

3. Surface of Revolution

Take a profile curve \(y=g(x)\geq 0\) in the \(xy\)-plane and spin it around the \(x\)-axis through a full \(2\pi\) rotation. Each point \((x,g(x),0)\) traces out a circle of radius \(g(x)\) centered on the \(x\)-axis.

Revolution Parametrization
\[\mathbf{r}(u,v)=\big(u,\;g(u)\cos v,\;g(u)\sin v\big)\]
u ∈ [a, b] — position along the axisv ∈ [0, 2π] — rotation angle around the axisg(u) — radius of the cross-sectional circle

How it works: Parameter \(u\) slides along the \(x\)-axis (controlling which cross-section you're at). Parameter \(v\) rotates around the axis (controlling where on that circle you are). The radius of each circle is \(g(u)\) — determined by the profile curve.

Fig 5.4 — Surface of revolution. The profile curve y = g(x) is spun around the x-axis. Parameter u slides along the axis; parameter v rotates around it. Each blue ellipse is a cross-section of radius g(u).

Example: Revolving \(y=\sqrt{x}\) for \(x\in[0,4]\) gives a trumpet-shaped surface: \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)=(u,\sqrt{u}\cos v,\sqrt{u}\sin v)\), and the cross-sections are circles of radius \(\sqrt{u}\) that grow as \(u\) increases.

★ How to Choose a Parametrization

Given as z = f(x,y)? → Use the graph parametrization. Simplest option.

Sphere or piece of a sphere? → Use latitude/longitude (the sphere parametrization above).

Cylinder \(x^2+y^2=R^2\)? → Let \(x=R\cos\theta,\;y=R\sin\theta\), use \(z\) as the second parameter.

Surface of revolution? → Identify the profile curve and axis of rotation, then apply the formula.

General rule: look for the natural "grid" on the surface — the two families of curves that cover it.

Example 5.9 — Parametrizing a Cylinder (Ex. 282)

Find a parametric representation of the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=4\) for \(0\leq z\leq 3\).

Show Solution

Thought process: The cylinder has circular cross-sections of radius 2. At any fixed height \(z\), the circle \(x^2+y^2=4\) is parametrized by the angle \(\theta\). That gives us one parameter. The second parameter is just \(z\) itself — it slides up and down the cylinder.

1
Circle at height \(z\): \(x=2\cos\theta,\;y=2\sin\theta\).
2
Let \(z\) be the second parameter (it ranges independently of \(\theta\)).

Answer: \(\mathbf{r}(\theta,z)=(2\cos\theta,\;2\sin\theta,\;z),\quad \theta\in[0,2\pi],\;z\in[0,3]\)

★ Tip: The \(\theta\)-curves (fix \(z\)) are horizontal circles. The \(z\)-curves (fix \(\theta\)) are vertical lines — the "rulings" of the cylinder.
