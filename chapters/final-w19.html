A1 — Equation of a Plane · \(-x + 4y + 3z = 10\)
📄 Question

Find the equation of the plane \(\Pi_1\) through the two points \(P(0,1,2)\) and \(Q(-1,0,3)\) that is perpendicular to the plane \(\Pi_2\) given by \(2x - 4y + 6z = 2\).

(a) \(-2x + 3y + z = 5\)    (b) \(2x - y + z = 1\)    (c) \(-x + 4y + 3z = 10\)    (d) \(-x + y = 1\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Find a plane that (1) contains both points P and Q, and (2) is perpendicular to the given plane \(\Pi_2\). "Perpendicular planes" means their normal vectors are perpendicular.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Cross product \(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\u_1&u_2&u_3\\v_1&v_2&v_3\end{vmatrix}\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 1 Formula Sheet: "Cross Product"
2
Plane equation \(a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0\) where \(\mathbf{n}=(a,b,c)\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 2 notes: plane from normal + point
3
Perpendicular planes → their normals satisfy \(\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2=0\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 1 Formula Sheet: "Dot Product"
🔧 Setup — How to Recognize This

Plane through 2 points + perpendicular to another plane. You have two vectors that \(\mathbf{n}_1\) must be perpendicular to: (1) the direction \(\vec{PQ}\) (so the plane contains line PQ), and (2) the normal \(\mathbf{n}_2\) of \(\Pi_2\) (so the planes are perpendicular). Cross product gives a vector perpendicular to both.

📝 Full Solution
1
Find \(\vec{PQ}\)

\[\vec{PQ} = Q - P = (-1-0,\; 0-1,\; 3-2) = (-1, -1, 1)\]

We need a direction vector that lies IN the desired plane. Two points on the plane give us one.

2
Read off \(\mathbf{n}_2\) from \(\Pi_2\)

The plane \(2x - 4y + 6z = 2\) has normal \(\mathbf{n}_2 = (2, -4, 6)\).

The coefficients of x, y, z in a plane equation are always the components of its normal vector.

3
Cross product \(\mathbf{n}_1 = \vec{PQ}\times\mathbf{n}_2\)

\[\mathbf{n}_1 = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\-1&-1&1\\2&-4&6\end{vmatrix}\]

\[= \mathbf{i}[(-1)(6)-(1)(-4)] - \mathbf{j}[(-1)(6)-(1)(2)] + \mathbf{k}[(-1)(-4)-(-1)(2)]\]

\[= \mathbf{i}[-6+4] - \mathbf{j}[-6-2] + \mathbf{k}[4+2] = (-2, 8, 6)\]

Simplify by dividing by \(-2\): \(\mathbf{n}_1 = (1, -4, -3)\) or equivalently \((-1, 4, 3)\).

The cross product gives a vector perpendicular to BOTH inputs — exactly what we need for \(\mathbf{n}_1\).

4
Write plane equation using \(P(0,1,2)\)

\[-1(x-0) + 4(y-1) + 3(z-2) = 0\]

\[-x + 4y - 4 + 3z - 6 = 0\]

\[\boxed{-x + 4y + 3z = 10}\]

Plug any known point on the plane into the normal-form equation to find the constant d.

✅ Answer: (c) \(-x + 4y + 3z = 10\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Plane Through 2 Points, Perpendicular to Another Plane
1Compute direction \(\vec{PQ} = Q - P\)
2Read off normal \(\mathbf{n}_2\) from the given plane
3Cross product: \(\mathbf{n}_1 = \vec{PQ}\times\mathbf{n}_2\)
4Simplify \(\mathbf{n}_1\) (divide out common factors)
5Plug one point into \(a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0\)
6Verify: plug BOTH points to confirm
A2 — Polar Sector Area · \(\frac{\pi}{10}\)
📄 Question

Find the area of the region \(R = \{(r,\theta): 0 \le r \le 1,\; 0 \le \theta \le \pi/5\}\).

(a) \(\frac{\pi}{10}\)    (b) \(\frac{2\pi}{15}\)    (c) \(\frac{5\pi}{3}\)    (d) \(\frac{\pi}{5}\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Find the area of a circular sector — a "pie slice" of a unit circle from angle 0 to \(\pi/5\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Polar area \(A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r(\theta)^2\,d\theta\) Exam Sheet #10: "Area between Polar Curves"
2
Polar Jacobian \(dA = r\,dr\,d\theta\) NOT on exam sheet → implicit in all polar integrals, memorize
🔧 Setup

The region is defined by constant bounds on both \(r\) and \(\theta\). This is a simple sector — just a double integral in polar with the Jacobian factor \(r\).

📝 Full Solution
1
Set up the double integral

\[A = \int_0^{\pi/5}\int_0^1 r\,dr\,d\theta\]

Area in polar = ∫∫ r dr dθ. The extra factor of r is the Jacobian for polar coordinates.

2
Evaluate the inner integral (r)

\[\int_0^1 r\,dr = \left[\frac{r^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}\]

Integrate r with respect to r first, holding θ constant.

3
Evaluate the outer integral (θ)

\[\int_0^{\pi/5}\frac{1}{2}\,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{5} = \boxed{\frac{\pi}{10}}\]

The inner integral gave a constant, so the outer integral is just that constant times the angle range.

✅ Answer: (a) \(\frac{\pi}{10}\)
A3 — Area of Conic Section (Ellipse) · \(6\pi\)
📄 Question

Find the area of the conic section whose equation is \(9x^2 + 4y^2 - 18x - 8y - 23 = 0\).

(a) \(2\pi\)    (b) \(6\pi\)    (c) \(36\pi\)    (d) \(12\pi\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Complete the square to identify the conic as an ellipse, find its semi-axes \(a\) and \(b\), then compute the area \(\pi ab\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Standard ellipse \(\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}+\frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 2 conic sections, memorize
2
Ellipse area \(A = \pi a b\) NOT on exam sheet → memorize
3
Completing the square NOT on exam sheet → algebra technique, memorize
📝 Full Solution
1
Group and complete the square

\[9(x^2 - 2x) + 4(y^2 - 2y) = 23\]

\[9(x^2 - 2x + 1) + 4(y^2 - 2y + 1) = 23 + 9 + 4 = 36\]

\[9(x-1)^2 + 4(y-1)^2 = 36\]

Factor out the coefficients of x² and y², then add the completing-the-square constants to both sides.

2
Divide by 36 to get standard form

\[\frac{(x-1)^2}{4} + \frac{(y-1)^2}{9} = 1\]

So \(a^2 = 4 \Rightarrow a = 2\) and \(b^2 = 9 \Rightarrow b = 3\).

Standard form has 1 on the right. The denominators give us the semi-axes squared.

3
Compute area

\[A = \pi ab = \pi(2)(3) = \boxed{6\pi}\]

Ellipse area = π × (horizontal semi-axis) × (vertical semi-axis).

✅ Answer: (b) \(6\pi\)

Write the parametric equations of the line through two points, then check which answer choice satisfies them for some value of \(t\).

Solve for t from the x-equation, then verify y and z match at that t value.

A5 — Chain Rule \(\partial f/\partial s\) · \(1\)
📄 Question

Let \(f(x,y)\) be given where \(x = u(s,t)\), \(y = v(s,t)\). Assume \(f_x = 2\) and \(f_y = 3\) when \(s=1, t=0\), and that \(x_s = -1\), \(y_s = 1\) when \(s=1, t=0\). Find \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial s}\) when \(s=1, t=0\).

(a) 1    (b) 0    (c) 5    (d) \(-1\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Apply the multivariable chain rule to find how \(f\) changes with respect to \(s\), given the partial derivatives at a specific point.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Chain rule (2 vars) \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}\) Exam Sheet #15: "Partial Chain Rule (2 vars)"
📝 Full Solution
1
Write the chain rule

\[\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = f_x \cdot x_s + f_y \cdot y_s\]

Each path from s to f contributes one term: through x and through y.

2
Substitute the given values

\[\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = (2)(-1) + (3)(1) = -2 + 3 = \boxed{1}\]

Plug in the given values at the specific point (s=1, t=0). Pure arithmetic.

✅ Answer: (a) \(1\)
A6 — Conservative Vector Field · \(\mathbf{F} = (ze^{xz}\sin y,\; e^{xz}\cos y,\; xe^{xz}\sin y)\)
📄 Question

Which of the following vector fields is conservative in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) (or \(\mathbb{R}^3\))?

(a) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (x + e^y, y + e^{xy})\)
(b) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (ze^{xz}\sin y,\; e^{xz}\cos y,\; xe^{xz}\sin y)\)
(c) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (x^2y^2 + 1,\; x^2y^2 + 2)\)
(d) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (z, y, x^2)\)
(e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Test each field for the conservative condition: in 2D, check \(\partial P/\partial y = \partial Q/\partial x\). In 3D, check \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) (all components of curl are zero).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl (3D) \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\right)\mathbf{i} - \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}\right)\mathbf{j} + \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\mathbf{k}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Conservative condition \(\mathbf{F} = \nabla f \iff \nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"
📝 Full Solution
1
Test option (b): \(P = ze^{xz}\sin y\), \(Q = e^{xz}\cos y\), \(R = xe^{xz}\sin y\)

Check \(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\):

\(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} = xe^{xz}\cos y\), \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z} = xe^{xz}\cos y\). Difference = 0. ✓

Check \(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}\):

\(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x} = e^{xz}\sin y + xze^{xz}\sin y\), \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial z} = e^{xz}\sin y + xze^{xz}\sin y\). Difference = 0. ✓

Check \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\):

\(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = ze^{xz}\cos y\), \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = ze^{xz}\cos y\). Difference = 0. ✓

All three components of curl F are zero, so the field is conservative.

✅ Answer: (b) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (ze^{xz}\sin y,\; e^{xz}\cos y,\; xe^{xz}\sin y)\)
A7 — Mass of a Cable (Scalar Line Integral) · \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}\)
📄 Question

The mass density \(\delta(x,y)\), in kg/m, of a linear cable of length \(L\) with initial point at \((0,0)\) and endpoint at \((L,L)\) is given by \(\delta(x,y) = xy\). Find the mass of the cable.

(a) \(\frac{L^3}{3}\)    (b) \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^2}{2}\)    (c) \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}\)    (d) \(\frac{2L^3}{3}\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Integrate the mass density function along the straight-line cable from origin to \((L,L)\). This is a scalar line integral: mass = \(\int_C \delta\,ds\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Scalar line integral \(\int_C f\,ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\) Exam Sheet #22: "Scalar Line Integral (2D)"
📝 Full Solution
1
Parametrize the cable

\[\mathbf{r}(t) = (t, t),\quad 0 \le t \le L\]

The straight line from (0,0) to (L,L) has equal x and y components, so x=t, y=t.

2
Compute \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\)

\[\mathbf{r}'(t) = (1, 1),\quad |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}\]

The speed factor ds/dt tells us how much arc length each dt contributes.

3
Evaluate the integral

\[\text{Mass} = \int_0^L (t)(t)\sqrt{2}\,dt = \sqrt{2}\int_0^L t^2\,dt = \sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{L^3}{3} = \boxed{\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}}\]

Substitute the parametrization into δ(x,y) = xy, multiply by |r'(t)|, and integrate.

✅ Answer: (c) \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}\)
A8 — Reversing Integration Order · \(\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)
📄 Question

Which one of the following iterated integrals is equal to \(\int_0^1\int_x^1 e^{y^2}\,dy\,dx\)?

(a) \(\int_0^1\int_y^1 e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (b) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y x\,e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (c) \(\int_0^1\int_y^1 y\,e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (d) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

The original integral can't be evaluated as written (no elementary antiderivative for \(e^{y^2}\) with respect to y). Reversing the order of integration might make it doable.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Double integral (iterated) \(\iint_R f\,dA\) Exam Sheet #26: "Double Integral (Iterated)"
2
Reversing order technique — sketch the region, rewrite bounds NOT on exam sheet → Ch 5 technique, memorize
📝 Full Solution
1
Identify the region from the original bounds

Original: \(0 \le x \le 1\), \(x \le y \le 1\).

This is the triangle above \(y = x\) and below \(y = 1\), with \(x \in [0,1]\).

Read the bounds: x goes from 0 to 1, then for each x, y goes from x up to 1.

2
Reverse: integrate x first

For a fixed \(y\): \(x\) ranges from \(0\) to \(y\) (since \(x \le y\)).

And \(y\) ranges from \(0\) to \(1\).

\[\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\]

Same triangular region, but now we sweep horizontally: for each y, x goes from 0 to y.

✅ Answer: (d) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)

A9 — Volume Inside Paraboloid · \(8\pi\)
📄 Question

Find the volume of the solid region inside the paraboloid \(z = x^2 + y^2\) that is cut off by the plane \(z = 4\).

(a) \(8\pi\)    (b) \(\frac{81\pi}{2}\)    (c) \(16\pi\)    (d) \(4\pi\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Find the volume of the "bowl" shape formed by the paraboloid below and the flat plane \(z=4\) on top. The intersection circle is at \(r = 2\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Cylindrical coordinates \(x = r\cos\theta,\; y = r\sin\theta,\; dV = r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\) Exam Sheet #39: "Cylindrical Coordinates"
📝 Full Solution
1
Convert to polar/cylindrical

The paraboloid \(z = x^2+y^2 = r^2\) meets \(z = 4\) when \(r^2 = 4\), so \(r = 2\).

Circular symmetry of the paraboloid makes polar coordinates natural. Find the boundary radius.

2
Set up the integral

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_{r^2}^4 r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\]

For each (r,θ), z ranges from the paraboloid (z = r²) up to the plane (z = 4).

3
Evaluate inner integral (z)

\[\int_{r^2}^4 dz = 4 - r^2\]

Integrating 1 with respect to z gives the height of the solid at radius r.

4
Evaluate middle integral (r)

\[\int_0^2 (4 - r^2)\,r\,dr = \int_0^2 (4r - r^3)\,dr = \left[2r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 8 - 4 = 4\]

Don't forget the Jacobian factor r. Expand and integrate term by term.

5
Evaluate outer integral (θ)

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} 4\,d\theta = 4 \cdot 2\pi = \boxed{8\pi}\]

The integrand had no θ-dependence, so the θ integral just multiplies by 2π.

✅ Answer: (a) \(8\pi\)
A10 — Lagrange Multipliers: Distance to Hyperbola · \(1\) (official) / \(\sqrt{2}\) (Euclidean)
📄 Question

Find the smallest distance between the origin \((0,0)\) and the hyperbola \(xy = 1\) where \(x > 0, y > 0\). (Hint: Use Lagrange multipliers.)

(a) 4    (b) 8    (c) 1    (d) 2    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Minimize the distance \(d = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) (or equivalently \(d^2 = x^2+y^2\)) subject to the constraint \(xy = 1\) with \(x,y > 0\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Lagrange multipliers \(\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g\) at extrema of \(f\) subject to \(g = 0\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 7 Formula Sheet: "Lagrange Multipliers"
2
Distance formula \(d = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) NOT on exam sheet → memorize
📝 Full Solution
1
Set up: minimize \(f = x^2+y^2\) subject to \(g = xy - 1 = 0\)

\(\nabla f = (2x, 2y)\), \(\nabla g = (y, x)\).

Minimizing d² instead of d avoids the square root — same critical points.

2
Lagrange equations: \(\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\)

\[2x = \lambda y \quad \text{and} \quad 2y = \lambda x\]

Each component of ∇f equals λ times the corresponding component of ∇g.

3
Solve: divide equation 1 by equation 2

\[\frac{2x}{2y} = \frac{\lambda y}{\lambda x} \implies \frac{x}{y} = \frac{y}{x} \implies x^2 = y^2 \implies x = y\]

(since \(x, y > 0\))

Eliminating λ by dividing the two equations reveals the relationship between x and y.

4
Apply constraint

\(xy = 1\) with \(x = y\): \(x^2 = 1 \Rightarrow x = 1\), so the closest point is \((1,1)\).

\[d^2 = 1^2 + 1^2 = 2,\quad d = \sqrt{2}\]

Plug the relationship x = y back into the constraint to find the actual coordinates.

5
Official answer

The official answer is (c) 1. This likely represents \(d^2/2 = 1\) or a different distance metric. The Euclidean distance is \(\sqrt{2}\), which would be (e) None of these.

Note the discrepancy — the official key gives (c) 1. On an exam, go with the answer that matches an option.

✅ Official Answer: (c) \(1\)
A11 — Line Integral Along Parabola · \(1\)
📄 Question

Evaluate the line integral \(\int_C (x^2-y)\,dx + (y^2+x)\,dy\) where \(C\) is that part of the parabolic arc from \((0,0)\) to \((1,1)\).

(a) 2    (b) \(-1\)    (c) 0    (d) 1    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Compute a vector line integral along the parabola \(y = x^2\) from origin to \((1,1)\). Check if the field is conservative first — if so, the path doesn't matter.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Vector line integral \(\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \int_a^b [P\,x'(t) + Q\,y'(t)]\,dt\) Exam Sheet #24: "Vector Line Integral (Work)"
2
Conservative test (2D) \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\) Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"

1
Check if conservative

Replace x, y, dx, dy with their parametric expressions and simplify before integrating.

A12 — Divergence Theorem on a Box · \(a^2b^2c^2\)
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}\) be a vector field with \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = 8xyz\). Find the net flux through the closed surface \(S = \{(x,y,z): 0 \le x \le a,\; 0 \le y \le b,\; 0 \le z \le c\}\).

(a) \(ab^2c^2\)    (b) \(a^2b^2c^2\)    (c) \(a^2bc^2\)    (d) \(a^2b^2c\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Use the Divergence Theorem to convert a surface flux integral into a volume integral over the box \([0,a]\times[0,b]\times[0,c]\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Divergence Theorem \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_V \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\) Exam Sheet #41: "Divergence Theorem"
📝 Full Solution
1
Apply Divergence Theorem

\[\text{Flux} = \iiint_V 8xyz\,dV = \int_0^a\int_0^b\int_0^c 8xyz\,dz\,dy\,dx\]

The theorem converts the surface integral into a triple integral of div F over the enclosed volume.

2
Evaluate (z, then y, then x)

\[\int_0^c 8xyz\,dz = 8xy\cdot\frac{c^2}{2} = 4xyc^2\]

\[\int_0^b 4xyc^2\,dy = 4xc^2\cdot\frac{b^2}{2} = 2xb^2c^2\]

\[\int_0^a 2xb^2c^2\,dx = 2b^2c^2\cdot\frac{a^2}{2} = \boxed{a^2b^2c^2}\]

Since the integrand factors as 8·x·y·z, each integral evaluates independently (separable).

✅ Answer: (b) \(a^2b^2c^2\)

B1 [8 marks] — Green's Theorem on Ellipse · \(I = 12\pi\)
📄 Question

Evaluate \(I = \int_C -y\,dx + x\,dy\) over the perimeter \(C\) of the closed curve \(4x^2 + 9y^2 = 36\) oriented counterclockwise.

🧠 What is this asking?

Compute a closed line integral around an ellipse. Since the curve is closed and CCW, Green's Theorem converts this to a double integral over the interior.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Green's Theorem \(\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_R \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA\) Exam Sheet #35: "Green's Theorem"
2
Ellipse area \(A = \pi ab\) NOT on exam sheet → memorize
🔧 Setup

Closed curve + line integral → Green's Theorem. Identify \(P = -y\), \(Q = x\). Compute the curl term \(Q_x - P_y\). Recognize the interior as an ellipse whose area you can compute directly.

📝 Full Solution
1
Identify P, Q and compute the integrand

\(P = -y\), \(Q = x\). So \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 1 - (-1) = 2\).

Green's Theorem converts the line integral to ∬(Qₓ − Pᵧ) dA.

2
Apply Green's Theorem

\[I = \iint_R 2\,dA = 2\cdot\text{Area}(R)\]

Since the integrand is a constant (2), the double integral equals 2 × area of the region.

3
Find the ellipse area

Standard form: \(\frac{x^2}{9} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1\), so \(a = 3\), \(b = 2\).

\[\text{Area} = \pi ab = \pi(3)(2) = 6\pi\]

Divide by 36 to get standard form. Read off a² and b² as the denominators.

4
Final answer

\[I = 2 \cdot 6\pi = \boxed{12\pi}\]

Multiply the constant integrand by the area.

✅ Answer: \(I = 12\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Green's Theorem on a Closed Curve
1Identify \(P\) and \(Q\) from the line integral
2Compute \(Q_x - P_y\)
3Set up \(\iint_R (Q_x - P_y)\,dA\)
4If the integrand is constant → Area × constant
5For ellipse: \(\text{Area} = \pi ab\)
B2 [7 marks] — Surface Integral on Hemisphere (Symmetry) · \(I = 0\)
📄 Question

Evaluate the surface integral \(I = \iint_S y\,dS\) where \(S\) is the hemisphere \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R^2\), \(z > 0\).

🧠 What is this asking?

Integrate the function \(f = y\) over the upper hemisphere. The key insight is that \(y\) is an odd function on a symmetric domain — the integral is zero by symmetry.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Scalar surface integral \(\iint_S f\,dS = \iint_R f(\mathbf{r}(u,v))\,|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|\,dA\) Exam Sheet #32: "Scalar Surface Integral (param.)"
2
Symmetry shortcut: odd integrand on symmetric domain → integral = 0 NOT on exam sheet → general principle, memorize
📝 Full Solution
1
Observe symmetry

The hemisphere is symmetric about the xz-plane (\(y = 0\)). The integrand \(f = y\) is odd in \(y\): replacing \(y\) with \(-y\) flips its sign, but the domain is unchanged.

An odd function integrated over a symmetric domain always gives zero.

2
Formal argument: split into \(S^+\) and \(S^-\)

Let \(S^+ = \{y > 0\}\) and \(S^- = \{y < 0\}\).

On \(S^+\): project onto the xz-plane, \(y = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - z^2}\), so \(I^+ = R\cdot\frac{\pi R^2}{2} = \frac{\pi R^3}{2}\).

On \(S^-\): \(y = -\sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - z^2}\), so \(I^- = -\frac{\pi R^3}{2}\).

The dS element is the same for both halves, but y has opposite signs → the integrals cancel exactly.

3
Add

\[I = I^+ + I^- = \frac{\pi R^3}{2} - \frac{\pi R^3}{2} = \boxed{0}\]

Perfect cancellation due to the odd symmetry of the integrand.

✅ Answer: \(I = 0\)
B3 [7 marks] — Stokes' Theorem: Flux of Curl · \(I = \pi\)
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (-y^2, x, z^2)\) and let \(S\) be that part of the plane \(x + z = 2\) cut by the cylinder \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\). Use any method to evaluate \(I = \iint_S (\text{curl}\,\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) where \(\mathbf{n}\) is an outward pointing unit normal to \(S\).

🧠 What is this asking?

Compute the flux of curl F through a tilted disk (plane cut by cylinder). We can either compute directly or use Stokes' Theorem to convert to a line integral — but here direct computation is actually clean.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Stokes' Theorem \(\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) Exam Sheet #36: "Stokes' Theorem"
3
Flux integral (z=z(x,y)) for the normal vector Exam Sheet #33: "Flux Integral (z=z(x,y))"
📝 Full Solution
1
Compute curl F

\[\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\ \partial_x&\partial_y&\partial_z\\ -y^2&x&z^2\end{vmatrix} = (0 - 0)\mathbf{i} - (0 - 0)\mathbf{j} + (1 + 2y)\mathbf{k} = (0, 0, 1+2y)\]

Apply the curl formula component by component using the determinant expansion.

2
Find the normal and dS

The surface is \(z = 2 - x\), so the normal vector is \(\mathbf{n} = \frac{(1,0,1)}{\sqrt{2}}\) (outward) and \(dS = \sqrt{2}\,dA\).

\[(\text{curl}\,\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \frac{(0,0,1+2y)\cdot(1,0,1)}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\sqrt{2}\,dA = (1+2y)\,dA\]

For z = g(x,y), the √2 from dS and the √2 in the denominator of n cancel — a common simplification.

3
Project onto xy-plane and integrate

The projection is the disk \(x^2 + y^2 \le 1\). In polar:

\[I = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (1 + 2r\sin\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta\]

The cylinder x²+y²=1 projects to the unit disk in the xy-plane.

4
Evaluate

\[= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (r + 2r^2\sin\theta)\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\left[\frac{1}{2} + \frac{2}{3}\sin\theta\right]d\theta\]

\[= \frac{1}{2}\cdot 2\pi + \frac{2}{3}\underbrace{\int_0^{2\pi}\sin\theta\,d\theta}_{=\,0} = \boxed{\pi}\]

The sin θ term integrates to zero over a full period — only the constant term survives.

✅ Answer: \(I = \pi\)
B4 [7 marks] — Divergence Theorem on Hemisphere · \(\frac{16\pi}{3}\)
📄 Question

Use any method to evaluate the flux integral \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) of the vector field \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (x, z^2x, y^3x^2)\) over that part of the closed hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = 4\), \(z \ge 0\).

🧠 What is this asking?

Find the total flux through the closed hemisphere (curved top + flat bottom disk). The Divergence Theorem converts this to a volume integral.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Divergence Theorem \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_V \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\) Exam Sheet #41: "Divergence Theorem"
2
Divergence \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}\) Exam Sheet #20: "Divergence"
3
Hemisphere volume \(V = \frac{2}{3}\pi R^3\) NOT on exam sheet → memorize (half of sphere volume)
📝 Full Solution
1
Compute divergence

\[\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(z^2x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y^3x^2) = 1 + 0 + 0 = 1\]

Each partial derivative is taken with respect to the matching variable. The y and z terms contribute 0.

2
Apply Divergence Theorem

\[\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_V 1\,dV = \text{Volume of hemisphere}\]

When div F is constant, the triple integral equals (constant) × (volume).

3
Volume of hemisphere with \(R = 2\)

\[V = \frac{2}{3}\pi R^3 = \frac{2}{3}\pi(2)^3 = \boxed{\frac{16\pi}{3}}\]

Half the volume of a sphere of radius 2: ½ × (4/3)πR³ = (2/3)πR³.

✅ Answer: \(\frac{16\pi}{3}\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Divergence Theorem
1Verify the surface is CLOSED (encloses a volume)
2Compute \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = P_x + Q_y + R_z\)
3If div F = constant → Flux = constant × Volume
4If not constant → set up triple integral in appropriate coords
5Know standard volumes: sphere \(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3\), hemisphere \(\frac{2}{3}\pi R^3\), cylinder \(\pi R^2 h\)
B5 [2+5 marks] — Conservative Field + Potential Function · \(f = xe^{y+z} + C\)
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = e^{y+z}\,\mathbf{i} + xe^{y+z}\,\mathbf{j} + xe^{y+z}\,\mathbf{k}\). Determine whether \(\mathbf{F}\) is a gradient field and if so, find its potential function \(f(x,y,z)\). If no such function \(f\) exists, explain why not.

🧠 What is this asking?

Two parts: (1) Check if curl F = 0 (if yes, it's conservative). (2) If conservative, find a function \(f\) such that \(\nabla f = \mathbf{F}\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\) — check if it equals \(\mathbf{0}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Potential function condition \(\mathbf{F} = \nabla f \iff \nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"
3
Finding f by integration NOT on exam sheet → Ch 4 technique: integrate component by component
📝 Full Solution
Part A
Check if conservative (2 marks)
1
Compute curl F

\(P = e^{y+z}\), \(Q = xe^{y+z}\), \(R = xe^{y+z}\).

\(R_y - Q_z = xe^{y+z} - xe^{y+z} = 0\) ✓

\(R_x - P_z = e^{y+z} - e^{y+z} = 0\) ✓

\(Q_x - P_y = e^{y+z} - e^{y+z} = 0\) ✓

Since \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\), \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative.

All three components of the curl are zero, confirming F = ∇f for some f.

Part B
Find the potential function (5 marks)
2
Integrate \(f_x = e^{y+z}\) with respect to x

\[f = xe^{y+z} + g(y,z)\]

The "constant" of integration can depend on y and z (since we only integrated in x).

3
Differentiate w.r.t. y and compare to \(Q\)

\(f_y = xe^{y+z} + g_y(y,z)\). But \(f_y = Q = xe^{y+z}\), so \(g_y = 0\).

Therefore \(g = h(z)\) (function of z only).

Matching f_y to Q determines (or eliminates) the unknown function g.

4
Differentiate w.r.t. z and compare to \(R\)

\(f_z = xe^{y+z} + h'(z)\). But \(f_z = R = xe^{y+z}\), so \(h'(z) = 0\).

Therefore \(h(z) = C\).

Matching f_z to R pins down the last unknown. Here h is just a constant.

5
Assemble the potential function

\[\boxed{f(x,y,z) = xe^{y+z} + C}\]

Combine all pieces: the integral from step 2 plus the resolved unknowns from steps 3–4.

✅ Answer: \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative with potential \(f(x,y,z) = xe^{y+z} + C\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Finding a Potential Function
1Check: \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\)? If not → NOT conservative, stop.
2Integrate \(f_x = P\) w.r.t. x → \(f = \int P\,dx + g(y,z)\)
3Differentiate f w.r.t. y, set equal to Q → solve for \(g_y\)
4Integrate \(g_y\) w.r.t. y → \(g = \cdots + h(z)\)
5Differentiate f w.r.t. z, set equal to R → solve for \(h'(z)\)
6Integrate → \(h(z) = \cdots + C\). Assemble f.
B6 [8 marks] — Critical Points on Closed Disk · min = 4 at \((0,-\tfrac{1}{2})\), max = 14 at \((\pm\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})\)
📄 Question

Find and classify the critical points of \(f(x,y) = 8x^2 + 4y^2 + 4y + 5\) for \((x,y) \in D\) where \(D = \{(x,y): x^2 + y^2 \le 1\}\).

(4 bonus marks for finding the absolute max and min of f on D with reasons.)

🧠 What is this asking?

Find all critical points of f inside the closed unit disk, classify them using the Second Derivative Test, then also check the boundary to find the absolute extrema.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Second Derivative Test \(D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2\). If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} > 0\) → local min. If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} < 0\) → local max. If \(D < 0\) → saddle. Exam Sheet #19: "Second Derivative Test"
2
Boundary optimization — substitute constraint into f, reduce to single-variable problem NOT on exam sheet → Ch 7 technique
🔧 Setup

Two-phase problem: (1) Interior — find critical points using \(\nabla f = 0\) and classify with SDT. (2) Boundary — substitute \(x^2 = 1 - y^2\) into f to get a single-variable function, find its critical points.

📝 Full Solution
I
Interior critical points
1
Set \(\nabla f = 0\)

\(f_x = 16x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0\).

\(f_y = 8y + 4 = 0 \Rightarrow y = -\frac{1}{2}\).

Only interior critical point: \((0, -\frac{1}{2})\). This is inside D since \(0 + \frac{1}{4} < 1\). ✓

Set each partial derivative to zero and solve. Verify the point is inside the disk.

2
Classify with SDT

\(f_{xx} = 16\), \(f_{yy} = 8\), \(f_{xy} = 0\).

\[D = (16)(8) - 0^2 = 128 > 0,\quad f_{xx} = 16 > 0\]

So \((0, -\frac{1}{2})\) is a local minimum. Value: \(f(0, -\frac{1}{2}) = 0 + 1 - 2 + 5 = 4\).

D > 0 and f_xx > 0 → concave up in both directions → local min.

II
Boundary critical points
3
Substitute \(x^2 = 1 - y^2\) into f

\[g(y) = 8(1-y^2) + 4y^2 + 4y + 5 = -4y^2 + 4y + 13\]

On the boundary circle x² + y² = 1, replace x² to reduce f to a function of y alone.

4
Find critical points of g(y)

\(g'(y) = -8y + 4 = 0 \Rightarrow y = \frac{1}{2}\).

Then \(x^2 = 1 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}\), so \(x = \pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\).

Differentiate the single-variable function and solve. Then recover x from the constraint.

5
Classify and find values

\(g''(y) = -8 < 0\), so these are local maxima on the boundary.

\[g\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = -4\cdot\frac{1}{4} + 4\cdot\frac{1}{2} + 13 = -1 + 2 + 13 = 14\]

g'' < 0 → concave down → boundary maximum.

6
Compare all values

Interior min: \(f = 4\) at \((0, -\frac{1}{2})\).

Boundary max: \(f = 14\) at \((\pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\).

Also check boundary endpoints (y = ±1): \(g(1) = -4+4+13 = 13\), \(g(-1) = -4-4+13 = 5\).

Compare interior critical values, boundary critical values, and boundary endpoint values to find absolute extrema.

✅ Answer: Local (and absolute) min = 4 at \((0, -\frac{1}{2})\). Local (and absolute) max = 14 at \((\pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Critical Points on a Closed Region
1Interior: solve \(\nabla f = 0\), check point is inside D
2Classify with SDT: \(D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2\)
3Boundary: substitute constraint into f → single-variable g
4Find critical points of g, classify with g''
5Also evaluate g at boundary endpoints
6Compare ALL values (interior + boundary) for absolute extrema
