LONG ANSWER — Stokes' Theorem

Confirmed Long Answer Q3 on W26. Appeared on every past final as long answer. The hardest LA question — but very formulaic once you know the pattern. Expect: compute curl F, get normal from plane/surface, evaluate the surface integral.

★ The Stokes' Pattern You'll See

Every Stokes' question follows this skeleton: (1) Compute \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\) via 3×3 determinant. (2) Find \(\mathbf{n}\) — if the surface is a plane \(ax+by+cz=d\), then \(\mathbf{n}=\frac{(a,b,c)}{|(a,b,c)|}\) instantly. (3) Dot them: \((\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\). (4) Integrate over the projected region (often a circle or ellipse → use area formula). The boundary curve is almost never parameterized directly — that's the whole point of Stokes'.

W19B3 [7 marks] — Stokes' / Direct (Flux of Curl Through Tilted Disk) · \(I = \pi\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (-y^2, x, z^2)\) and let \(S\) be that part of the plane \(x+z=2\) cut by the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=1\). Use any method to evaluate the flux integral \(I = \iint_S (\text{curl}\,\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) where \(\mathbf{n}\) is an outward pointing unit normal vector to \(S\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Flux integral (z=z(x,y)) Exam Sheet #33: "Flux Integral (z=z(x,y))"
📝 Full Solution
1
Compute curl F — all 6 partial derivatives

With \(P=-y^2\), \(Q=x\), \(R=z^2\):

\[\mathbf{i}: R_y - Q_z = \frac{\partial(z^2)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial(x)}{\partial z} = 0 - 0 = 0\]

\[\mathbf{j}: -(R_x - P_z) = -\left(\frac{\partial(z^2)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(-y^2)}{\partial z}\right) = -(0-0) = 0\]

\[\mathbf{k}: Q_x - P_y = \frac{\partial(x)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(-y^2)}{\partial y} = 1-(-2y) = 1+2y\]

\[\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, 1+2y)\]

Tip: the k-component is Q_x − P_y. Here P_y = ∂(−y²)/∂y = −2y, so the subtraction gives 1 − (−2y) = 1 + 2y. Watch the double negative.

2
Find the normal vector for \(z = 2-x\)

Write the surface as \(G(x,y,z) = x+z-2 = 0\). Then \(\nabla G = (1, 0, 1)\).

Unit normal: \(\hat{n} = \frac{(1,0,1)}{\sqrt{2}}\) (outward/upward).

Surface element: for \(z = g(x,y) = 2-x\), we have \(g_x = -1\), \(g_y = 0\).

\[dS = \sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA = \sqrt{1+1+0}\,dA = \sqrt{2}\,dA\]

For z = g(x,y), the normal is (−g_x, −g_y, 1)/|(−g_x, −g_y, 1)| and dS = √(1+g_x²+g_y²) dA. These formulas are on the exam sheet.

3
Combine: (curl F)·n dS — watch the √2 cancel

\[(\text{curl}\,\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = (0,0,1+2y)\cdot\frac{(1,0,1)}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\sqrt{2}\,dA\]

The dot product gives \(\frac{1+2y}{\sqrt{2}}\). Multiply by \(\sqrt{2}\,dA\):

\[= \frac{1+2y}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\sqrt{2}\,dA = (1+2y)\,dA\]

Tip: for any plane z = ax+by+c, the √(1+a²+b²) from dS and the same factor in the denominator of n always cancel. The result is just (curl F)·(−g_x, −g_y, 1) dA.

4
Project onto xy-plane — the disk \(x^2+y^2 \le 1\)

The cylinder \(x^2+y^2 = 1\) cuts the tilted plane, but its projection onto the xy-plane is just the unit disk.

\[I = \iint_{\text{disk}} (1+2y)\,dA\]

Convert to polar: \(y = r\sin\theta\), \(dA = r\,dr\,d\theta\).

\[= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (1+2r\sin\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (r+2r^2\sin\theta)\,dr\,d\theta\]

The projection is always a flat disk — the tilt of the plane doesn't affect what the shadow looks like.

5
Evaluate the r-integral

\[\int_0^1 r\,dr = \frac{1}{2}, \qquad \int_0^1 2r^2\,dr = \frac{2}{3}\]

\[\int_0^1 (r+2r^2\sin\theta)\,dr = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{2}{3}\sin\theta\]

Integrate each term in r separately. The sin θ is a constant with respect to r.

6
Evaluate the θ-integral

\[\int_0^{2\pi}\left(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{2}{3}\sin\theta\right)d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 2\pi + \frac{2}{3}\underbrace{\int_0^{2\pi}\sin\theta\,d\theta}_{=\;0}\]

\[= \pi + 0 = \boxed{\pi}\]

Tip: ∫₀^{2π} sin θ dθ = 0 always (full period of sine). Only the constant term 1/2 survives, giving ½ × 2π = π.

✅ Answer: \(I = \pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Flux of Curl Through a Tilted Surface
1Compute curl F (all 6 partials)
2For z=g(x,y): (curl F)·n dS simplifies to (curl F)·(−g_x, −g_y, 1) dA
3Project onto xy-plane → convert to polar
4∫sin θ over [0,2π] = 0 → only constant terms survive
🧩 Takeaway — W19 B3

What you learn: When the surface is a tilted disk (plane cutting through a sphere/cylinder), you can either use Stokes' or compute the line integral directly. Stokes' is almost always faster. The normal comes straight from the plane equation.
Variations to watch: The boundary could be a circle, ellipse, or triangle. The plane equation changes but the method is identical. If the answer involves \(\pi\), you're probably integrating over a circular projection.

F14B6 [8 marks] — Stokes' Theorem (curl F·n on Flat Ellipse) · \(4\pi\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}=x^2\,\mathbf{i}+2x\,\mathbf{j}+z^2\,\mathbf{k}\) and \(S\) be the surface interior to the ellipse \(4x^2+y^2=4\) on \(z=0\). Evaluate \(\iint_S \text{curl}\,\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Stokes' Theorem \(\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) Exam Sheet #36: "Stokes' Theorem"
2
Curl \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=(R_y-Q_z)\mathbf{i}-(R_x-P_z)\mathbf{j}+(Q_x-P_y)\mathbf{k}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
🔧 Setup

Two approaches work here. Direct method: compute curl F, dot with n, integrate over the flat elliptic region. Stokes' method: convert to a line integral around the ellipse boundary. The direct method is faster when curl F · n turns out to be a constant.

📝 Full Solution — Direct Method
1
Compute curl F component by component

With \(P = x^2\), \(Q = 2x\), \(R = z^2\):

\[\mathbf{i}\text{-component: } R_y - Q_z = \frac{\partial(z^2)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial(2x)}{\partial z} = 0 - 0 = 0\]

\[\mathbf{j}\text{-component: } -(R_x - P_z) = -\left(\frac{\partial(z^2)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(x^2)}{\partial z}\right) = -(0 - 0) = 0\]

\[\mathbf{k}\text{-component: } Q_x - P_y = \frac{\partial(2x)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(x^2)}{\partial y} = 2 - 0 = 2\]

\[\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, 2) = 2\,\mathbf{k}\]

Tip: for the k-component, it's Q_x − P_y (not P_y − Q_x). Getting this sign wrong flips the entire answer. Here: ∂(2x)/∂x = 2 and ∂(x²)/∂y = 0.

2
Identify the surface normal

The surface lies on \(z = 0\) (the xy-plane). For a flat horizontal surface, the outward unit normal is \(\hat{n} = \mathbf{k}\) (pointing upward).

Any surface on z = constant has normal ±k. "Outward" or "upward" means +k.

3
Dot product

\[(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n} = (0, 0, 2)\cdot(0, 0, 1) = 2\]

The integrand is a constant — no actual integration needed.

When curl F · n is constant, the surface integral equals (constant) × (area of surface). This is the best possible outcome.

4
Find the area of the elliptic region

The ellipse \(4x^2 + y^2 = 4\) in standard form:

\[\frac{x^2}{1} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1\]

So \(a = 1\) (x-semi-axis) and \(b = 2\) (y-semi-axis).

\[\text{Area} = \pi ab = \pi(1)(2) = 2\pi\]

Tip: divide the equation by the RHS to get standard form. The denominators are a² and b². Here: a² = 1 → a = 1, b² = 4 → b = 2.

5
Compute the surface integral

\[\iint_S \text{curl}\,\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = 2 \cdot 2\pi = \boxed{4\pi}\]

Constant × area. Clean and fast.

✅ Answer: \(4\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Stokes' (Direct Method on Flat Surface)
1Compute curl F (all 3 components, watch signs)
2Flat surface → n = k. Dot product picks k-component
3If constant → integral = constant × area
4Ellipse: standard form → \(a, b\) → area = \(\pi ab\)
5Tip: k-component is \(Q_x - P_y\), NOT \(P_y - Q_x\)
🧩 Takeaway — F14 B6

What you learn: When the surface is a flat region in a plane (like the interior of an ellipse in the xy-plane), Stokes' reduces to a double integral over that region. The normal is just \(\hat{k}\) if the surface is in the xy-plane.
Variations: The projected region could be an ellipse (area = \(\pi ab\)) or disk (area = \(\pi r^2\)). If \((\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\) is a constant, you just multiply by the area.

F13B6 [8 marks] — Stokes' Theorem (curl F·n on Flat Disk) · \(36\pi\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}=-5y\,\mathbf{i}+4x\,\mathbf{j}+z\,\mathbf{k}\) and \(S\) be bounded by \(x^2+y^2=4\), \(z=1\). Evaluate \(\iint_S\text{curl}\,\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=(R_y-Q_z)\mathbf{i}-(R_x-P_z)\mathbf{j}+(Q_x-P_y)\mathbf{k}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Stokes' Theorem \(\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) Exam Sheet #36: "Stokes' Theorem"
🔧 Setup

The surface is a flat disk on the plane \(z = 1\). For flat surfaces, direct computation of curl F · n is usually faster than converting to a line integral via Stokes'.

📝 Full Solution
1
Compute curl F component by component

With \(P = -5y\), \(Q = 4x\), \(R = z\):

\[\mathbf{i}\text{-component: } R_y - Q_z = 0 - 0 = 0\]

\[\mathbf{j}\text{-component: } -(R_x - P_z) = -(0 - 0) = 0\]

\[\mathbf{k}\text{-component: } Q_x - P_y = 4 - (-5) = 4 + 5 = 9\]

\[\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, 9) = 9\,\mathbf{k}\]

Tip: P_y = ∂(−5y)/∂y = −5. The negative of a negative gives +5 in the k-component: 4 − (−5) = 9. Watch signs carefully here.

2
Identify the normal vector

The surface lies on the plane \(z = 1\) (a horizontal flat surface). The outward unit normal is \(\hat{n} = \mathbf{k}\) (pointing upward).

For any surface on z = constant, the normal is simply k (upward) or −k (downward). "Outward" = upward here.

3
Dot product

\[(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n} = (0,0,9)\cdot(0,0,1) = 9\]

Only the k-components survive the dot product. The result is a constant.

4
Integrate over the disk

Since the integrand is the constant 9, the integral equals 9 × (area of the disk).

The disk has equation \(x^2 + y^2 \le 4\), so radius \(R = 2\).

\[\iint_S 9\,dS = 9 \cdot \pi R^2 = 9 \cdot \pi(2)^2 = 9 \cdot 4\pi = \boxed{36\pi}\]

Tip: whenever curl F · n = constant on a flat surface, the integral is just (constant) × (area). No actual integration needed.

✅ Answer: \(36\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Stokes' on a Flat Surface
1Compute curl F (all 3 components)
2Flat surface → n = k (or −k). Watch sign: \(Q_x - P_y\) not \(P_y - Q_x\)
3Dot product picks out the k-component
4If constant → multiply by area. Circle: \(\pi R^2\)
🧩 Takeaway — F13 B6

What you learn: Same pattern as F14 B6 but with a disk instead of ellipse. When curl·n = constant, the integral is just (constant)(area of disk). This is the easiest Stokes' variation — recognize it and finish in 3 minutes.
Watch for: Getting the curl wrong (sign errors in the 3×3 determinant) is the #1 mistake. Double-check each component.

F17B6 [10 marks] — Stokes' Theorem (Paraboloid Cap, Line Integral) · \(12\pi\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Use Stokes' Theorem to evaluate \(\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS\) where \(\mathbf{F}=-y\mathbf{i}+xz\mathbf{j}+x^2y^2\mathbf{k}\), S is \(z=6-x^2-y^2\) above \(z=2\), and \(\hat{n}\) is the outer normal.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Stokes' Theorem \(\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS=\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) Exam Sheet #36: "Stokes' Theorem"
🔧 Setup

The boundary curve C is where \(z=6-x^2-y^2\) meets \(z=2\): \(x^2+y^2=4\), \(z=2\). This is a circle of radius 2 at height z = 2. Computing the line integral is much easier than computing curl F and integrating over the curved surface.

📝 Full Solution
1
Parametrize the boundary circle

\(x=2\cos t\), \(y=2\sin t\), \(z=2\), \(0\le t\le 2\pi\).

\(dx=-2\sin t\,dt\), \(dy=2\cos t\,dt\), \(dz=0\).

Circle of radius 2 at height z=2. Counterclockwise orientation (from above, consistent with upward normal).

2
Substitute F on C

\(P=-y=-2\sin t\), \(Q=xz=2\cos t\cdot 2=4\cos t\), \(R=x^2y^2=4\cos^2 t\cdot 4\sin^2 t\).

3
Form the integrand \(P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz\)

\[(-2\sin t)(-2\sin t\,dt)+(4\cos t)(2\cos t\,dt)+(R)(0)\]

\[= 4\sin^2 t\,dt + 8\cos^2 t\,dt\]

The R·dz term vanishes since dz = 0 on the horizontal circle.

4
Simplify the integrand

\[4\sin^2 t+8\cos^2 t = 4(\sin^2 t+\cos^2 t)+4\cos^2 t = 4+4\cos^2 t\]

Split: 4sin²t + 8cos²t = 4(sin²t+cos²t) + 4cos²t = 4 + 4cos²t.

5
Evaluate

\[\oint_C = \int_0^{2\pi}(4+4\cos^2 t)\,dt = 4\cdot 2\pi + 4\cdot\pi = 8\pi+4\pi = \boxed{12\pi}\]

∫₀^{2π} 1 dt = 2π. ∫₀^{2π} cos²t dt = π (half-angle: cos²t = (1+cos2t)/2, the cos2t integrates to 0 over full period).

✅ Answer: \(12\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Stokes' via Line Integral
1Find boundary curve: where the surface meets its edge
2Parametrize C (circle at intersection height)
3Compute \(\oint P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz\) on C
4dz = 0 on horizontal circles → R term vanishes
5Use ∫cos²t = π over [0,2π]
🧩 Takeaway — F17 B6

What you learn: When the surface is a paraboloid cap (\(z = g(x,y)\) for \(x^2+y^2 \le R^2\)), you need to use the full surface integral formula with \(\mathbf{n}\,dS = (-g_x, -g_y, 1)\,dA\). The projection is a disk in the xy-plane → switch to polar.
Key difference: This is harder than the flat-surface versions because the normal isn't constant. But the projection method (integrate over the shadow in xy-plane) still works.

PracticeQ25 — Stokes' Theorem (Choose Easier Surface) · \(-2\pi\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Use Stokes' to evaluate \(\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{n}\,dS\) for \(\mathbf{F}=(y,-x,z)\) on the upper hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\), \(z\ge 0\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Stokes' Theorem Exam Sheet #36: "Stokes' Theorem"
📝 Full Solution
1
Compute curl F

\(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=(0,0,-1-1)=(0,0,-2)\).

k-component: Q_x − P_y = (−1) − (1) = −2.

2
Key insight: use a FLAT disk instead of the hemisphere

Both surfaces share the same boundary (unit circle at z=0). By Stokes', the integral is the same over ANY surface with that boundary.

On the flat disk z=0: \(\hat{n}=\mathbf{k}\). \((0,0,-2)\cdot\mathbf{k}=-2\).

\[\iint(-2)\,dA = -2\cdot\pi = \boxed{-2\pi}\]

Tip: always choose the EASIEST surface with the same boundary. A flat disk beats a curved hemisphere every time.

✅ Answer: \(-2\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Stokes' Theorem (Choose Easier Surface)
1Compute curl F
2Stokes' says: ANY surface with same boundary gives same answer
3Choose the flat disk → constant integrand → multiply by area
🧩 Takeaway — Practice Q25

What you learn: You can choose ANY surface with the same boundary curve. If the original surface is complicated (paraboloid, hemisphere), swap it for a flat disk in the xy-plane. Same boundary → same answer via Stokes'. This is the "choose the easier surface" trick.
Exam tip: If the problem says "evaluate \(\oint\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) where C is the boundary of [complicated surface]", immediately consider using the flat projection instead.

LONG ANSWER — Divergence Theorem

Confirmed Long Answer Q4 on W26. Described as the easiest long answer. The #1 shortcut: when div F = constant, flux = constant × volume of the region. Know volumes: sphere = \(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\), cylinder = \(\pi r^2 h\), cube = \(s^3\).

★ The Divergence Shortcut That Wins Free Marks

Step 1: Compute \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = P_x + Q_y + R_z\). Step 2: If it's a constant (no x, y, z left), then \(\iint\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = (\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}) \times \text{Volume}\). Done. No triple integral needed. On W26, div F was a constant and the region was a cylinder → answer was just \(\text{const} \times \pi r^2 h\).

W19B4 [7 marks] — Divergence Theorem (div F = constant → Volume) · \(\frac{16\pi}{3}\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Use any method to evaluate the flux integral \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS\) of the vector field \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (x, z^2x, y^3x^2)\) over that part of the closed hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\), \(z\ge 0\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Divergence Theorem \(\iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \iiint_V\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\) Exam Sheet #41: "Divergence Theorem"
2
Divergence Exam Sheet #20: "Divergence"
🔧 Setup

The surface is closed (curved hemisphere on top + flat disk on bottom at z = 0). The Divergence Theorem converts this to a volume integral. The strategy: compute div F and hope it's a constant — if so, the integral is just (constant) × (volume).

📝 Full Solution
1
Compute divergence — each partial derivative

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x) = 1\]

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(z^2x) = 0 \qquad\text{(x and z are constants w.r.t. y)}\]

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y^3x^2) = 0 \qquad\text{(x and y are constants w.r.t. z)}\]

\[\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = 1 + 0 + 0 = 1\]

Tip: the "complicated" terms z²x and y³x² both disappear because they don't depend on their own variable (y and z respectively). Only the simple first component x contributes.

2
Apply the Divergence Theorem

\[\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \iiint_V 1\,dV = \text{Volume of the solid hemisphere}\]

When div F is a constant, the triple integral is just that constant times the volume. This is the ideal scenario — no integration needed.

3
Compute the hemisphere volume

The hemisphere has radius \(R = 2\) (from \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = 4\)).

\[V_{\text{hemisphere}} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 = \frac{2}{3}\pi R^3 = \frac{2}{3}\pi(2)^3 = \frac{2}{3}\pi\cdot 8 = \frac{16\pi}{3}\]

Hemisphere = half of a sphere. Sphere volume = (4/3)πR³, so hemisphere = (2/3)πR³. With R = 2: (2/3)π(8) = 16π/3.

4
Final answer

\[\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = 1 \cdot \frac{16\pi}{3} = \boxed{\frac{16\pi}{3}}\]

Tip: know these standard volumes — sphere (4/3)πR³, hemisphere (2/3)πR³, cylinder πR²h, cone (1/3)πR²h. They come up constantly with the Divergence Theorem.

✅ Answer: \(\frac{16\pi}{3}\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Divergence Theorem
1Verify surface is CLOSED
2Compute div F (each partial w.r.t. its own variable)
3div F = constant? → Flux = constant × Volume
4Know standard volumes: sphere, hemisphere, cylinder, cone
🧩 Takeaway — W19 B4

What you learn: The "constant div" shortcut in action. Compute div F, get a constant, multiply by volume of the solid. This is exactly what appeared on W26.
Variations: The solid could be a sphere (\(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)), hemisphere (half that), cylinder (\(\pi r^2 h\)), or cube (\(a^3\)). Know all these volumes cold.

F14B5 [7 marks] — Divergence Theorem (div = ρ², Spherical Coords) · \(\frac{2\pi}{5}\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F} = xz^2\,\mathbf{i}+(x^2y-z^3)\,\mathbf{j}+(2xy+y^2z)\,\mathbf{k}\) and \(S\) the surface of the solid hemisphere \(T\) bounded by \(z=\sqrt{1-x^2-y^2}\) and \(z=0\). Evaluate \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Divergence Theorem \(\iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{n}\,dS = \iiint_V\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\) Exam Sheet #41: "Divergence Theorem"
2
Spherical coordinates \(x^2+y^2+z^2=\rho^2\), \(dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta\) Exam Sheet #40: "Spherical Coordinates"
🔧 Setup

The surface is closed (curved hemisphere + flat disk on z = 0). The Divergence Theorem converts the surface integral to a volume integral. The key insight: div F simplifies to x² + y² + z² = ρ², which is perfect for spherical coordinates.

📝 Full Solution
1
Compute each partial derivative of div F

\(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xz^2) = z^2\)

\(\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2y - z^3) = x^2\)

\(\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2xy + y^2z) = y^2\)

\[\text{div}\,\mathbf{F} = z^2 + x^2 + y^2 = x^2 + y^2 + z^2\]

Tip: notice that the "ugly" terms (xz², −z³, 2xy) all disappear under differentiation. Only the "nice" terms survive, giving the perfect form x² + y² + z².

2
Recognize \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2\) in spherical

This is the key simplification that makes spherical coordinates the right choice.

Whenever div F = x² + y² + z² (or any function of ρ), spherical coordinates will make the integral separable.

3
Set up the hemisphere bounds in spherical

The upper hemisphere of radius 1:

\(0 \le \rho \le 1\) (radius from 0 to 1)

\(0 \le \theta \le 2\pi\) (full revolution around z-axis)

\(0 \le \phi \le \pi/2\) (from north pole down to equator — not to π)

Tip: φ goes from 0 to π/2 for a hemisphere (not 0 to π, which would be a full sphere). This is the most common bounds mistake in spherical.

4
Write the triple integral

\[\iiint_T \rho^2 \cdot \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\theta\,d\phi = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 \rho^4\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\theta\,d\phi\]

div F = ρ² times the spherical volume element ρ²sinφ gives ρ⁴sinφ. The integral separates because the integrand factors as (ρ⁴)(sinφ)(1).

5
Evaluate each 1D integral separately

\[\int_0^1 \rho^4\,d\rho = \frac{\rho^5}{5}\Bigg|_0^1 = \frac{1}{5}\]

\[\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = 2\pi\]

\[\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin\phi\,d\phi = \Big[-\cos\phi\Big]_0^{\pi/2} = -\cos\frac{\pi}{2}+\cos 0 = 0 + 1 = 1\]

6

Three clean fractions multiplied together.

2Hemisphere: φ from 0 to π/2 (not π!)

W19A12 — Divergence Theorem (Constant-Free div, Separable Integral) · \(a^2b^2c^2\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}\) be a vector field with \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = 8xyz\). Find the net flux through the closed surface \(S = \{(x,y,z): 0 \le x \le a,\; 0 \le y \le b,\; 0 \le z \le c\}\).

(a) \(ab^2c^2\)    (b) \(a^2b^2c^2\)    (c) \(a^2bc^2\)    (d) \(a^2b^2c\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Use the Divergence Theorem to convert a surface flux integral into a volume integral over the box \([0,a]\times[0,b]\times[0,c]\).

🔑 Early Elimination — Symmetry Kills 3 Choices Instantly

The integrand \(8xyz\) treats \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) symmetrically — each variable appears to the same power. The box bounds \([0,a]\times[0,b]\times[0,c]\) are also symmetric in structure. So the answer must treat \(a, b, c\) identically: each must appear to the same power. Choices (a), (c), and (d) each have one variable to the first power while the others are squared — that asymmetry is impossible. Only (b) \(a^2b^2c^2\) is symmetric. Circle it before computing.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Divergence Theorem \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_V \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\) Exam Sheet #41: "Divergence Theorem"
📝 Full Solution
1
Apply Divergence Theorem

\[\text{Flux} = \iiint_V 8xyz\,dV = \int_0^a\int_0^b\int_0^c 8xyz\,dz\,dy\,dx\]

The theorem converts the surface integral into a triple integral of div F over the enclosed volume.

2
Evaluate (z, then y, then x)

\[\int_0^c 8xyz\,dz = 8xy\cdot\frac{c^2}{2} = 4xyc^2\]

\[\int_0^b 4xyc^2\,dy = 4xc^2\cdot\frac{b^2}{2} = 2xb^2c^2\]

\[\int_0^a 2xb^2c^2\,dx = 2b^2c^2\cdot\frac{a^2}{2} = \boxed{a^2b^2c^2}\]

Since the integrand factors as 8·x·y·z, each integral evaluates independently (separable).

✅ Answer: (b) \(a^2b^2c^2\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Divergence Theorem on a Box
1Compute \(\text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\)
2Set up \(\iiint_V \text{div}\,\mathbf{F}\,dV\) over the box
3If integrand is separable: factor into 3 single integrals
🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — One-Line Separable Product

Since \(8xyz\) factors completely, write the triple integral as a product of three independent integrals:

\[8\left(\int_0^a x\,dx\right)\left(\int_0^b y\,dy\right)\left(\int_0^c z\,dz\right) = 8\cdot\frac{a^2}{2}\cdot\frac{b^2}{2}\cdot\frac{c^2}{2} = a^2b^2c^2\]

One line — no nesting needed. Recognize separable integrands on sight.

🧩 Takeaway — W19 A12

What you learn: MCQ version — even when div F isn't purely constant, it might be separable (e.g., \(f(x)g(y)h(z)\)), making the triple integral factor into three single integrals. Look for this pattern on MCQ for quick computation.

W19B6 [8 marks] — Extrema on Closed Disk (Interior SDT + Boundary Substitution) · min = 4, max = 14Ch 7
📄 Question

Find and classify the critical points of the function \(f(x,y) = 8x^2 + 4y^2 + 4y + 5\) for \((x,y) \in \mathcal{D}\) where \(\mathcal{D} = \{(x,y) : x^2+y^2 \le 1\}\).

(A total of 4 bonus marks is given for finding the absolute maximum and absolute minimum of \(f\) on \(\mathcal{D}\) and giving reasons.)

(4 bonus marks for finding the absolute max and min on D with reasons.)

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Second Derivative Test \(D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2\) Exam Sheet #19: "Second Derivative Test"
🔧 Setup

Two phases: (1) find interior critical points using ∇f = 0 and classify with SDT. (2) Find boundary extrema by substituting the constraint x² = 1 − y² into f, reducing to a single-variable problem. Compare all values to find the absolute max and min.

📝 Full Solution
I
Interior critical points
1
Compute partial derivatives and set to zero

\[f_x = 16x = 0 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad x = 0\]

\[f_y = 8y + 4 = 0 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad y = -\frac{1}{2}\]

Critical point: \((0, -\frac{1}{2})\).

Set each partial to zero and solve. Here f_x gives x immediately; f_y is a simple linear equation.

2
Verify the point is inside D

\(x^2 + y^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4} < 1\) ✓ — the point is strictly inside the disk.

Tip: always verify interior points are actually interior. If x²+y² > 1, the point is outside D and doesn't count.

3
Classify with the Second Derivative Test

\[f_{xx} = 16, \qquad f_{yy} = 8, \qquad f_{xy} = 0\]

\[D = f_{xx}\cdot f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2 = (16)(8) - 0^2 = 128\]

\(D = 128 > 0\) and \(f_{xx} = 16 > 0\) → local minimum.

D > 0 means it's definitely an extremum (not a saddle). f_xx > 0 means concave up → minimum.

4
Compute the minimum value

\[f(0, -\tfrac{1}{2}) = 8(0) + 4(\tfrac{1}{4}) + 4(-\tfrac{1}{2}) + 5 = 0 + 1 - 2 + 5 = 4\]

Plug the critical point into f. Careful with the signs: 4y² = 4(1/4) = 1, and 4y = 4(−1/2) = −2.

II
Boundary critical points
5
Substitute the constraint into f

On the boundary \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\), so \(x^2 = 1 - y^2\). Substitute:

\[g(y) = 8(1-y^2) + 4y^2 + 4y + 5 = 8 - 8y^2 + 4y^2 + 4y + 5\]

\[= -4y^2 + 4y + 13\]

Tip: on the boundary circle, replace x² with 1−y², turning f into a function of y alone. Now it's a single-variable optimization problem.

6
Find critical points of g(y)

\[g'(y) = -8y + 4 = 0 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad y = \frac{1}{2}\]

Then \(x^2 = 1 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}\), so \(x = \pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\).

Boundary critical points: \((\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\) and \((-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\).

Differentiate the single-variable function g, set to zero. Then recover x from the constraint.

7
Classify and compute boundary values

\(g''(y) = -8 < 0\) → these are local maxima on the boundary.

\[g(\tfrac{1}{2}) = -4(\tfrac{1}{4}) + 4(\tfrac{1}{2}) + 13 = -1 + 2 + 13 = 14\]

g'' < 0 means concave down → boundary maximum. Both ±√3/2 points give the same f-value by symmetry.

8
Check boundary endpoints (y = ±1)

At \(y = 1\): \(x = 0\), \(g(1) = -4+4+13 = 13\).

At \(y = -1\): \(x = 0\), \(g(-1) = -4-4+13 = 5\).

Tip: the boundary is a closed curve, so g(y) on y ∈ [−1, 1] is a closed interval problem. Check endpoints AND critical points.

9
Compare ALL values to find absolute extrema

Interior: \(f = 4\) at \((0, -\frac{1}{2})\).

Boundary critical: \(f = 14\) at \((\pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\).

Boundary endpoints: \(f = 13\) at \((0, 1)\), \(f = 5\) at \((0, -1)\).

Absolute minimum = 4 (interior). Absolute maximum = 14 (boundary).

The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees both exist on the closed bounded region D. Compare all candidates.

✅ Answer: Abs min = 4 at \((0,-\frac{1}{2})\). Abs max = 14 at \((\pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Extrema on a Closed Region
1Interior: ∇f = 0, verify point is inside, classify with SDT
2Boundary: substitute constraint (x²=1−y²) → single-variable g(y)
3Find g'=0, recover x from constraint
4Check boundary endpoints too (y = ±1)
5Compare ALL values → absolute max and min
🧩 Takeaway — W19 B6

What you learn: The hardest variation — extrema on a CLOSED region (disk). You need BOTH interior CPs (SDT) AND boundary analysis (substitute constraint, reduce to single-variable calculus). This is more work than a standalone SDT problem.

LONG ANSWER — Lagrange Multipliers

Appeared as long answer on every past final (F14 B1, F13 B1, F17 B1). Not confirmed for W26 deferred but extremely likely given the pattern. Set up \(\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\), solve the system of equations, evaluate f at each solution.

★ Lagrange Multiplier Mechanical Steps

(1) Write \(\mathcal{L} = f - \lambda g\). (2) Set \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x = 0\), \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial y = 0\), \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial \lambda = 0\) (= the constraint). (3) From the first two equations, express one variable in terms of another (or divide equations to eliminate λ). (4) Substitute into constraint to get actual values. (5) Evaluate f at each candidate → largest is max, smallest is min.

F14B1 [8 marks] — Lagrange Multipliers (Optimize on Linear Constraint): \(x^2y^2\) on \(x+y=1\) · Max \(\frac{1}{16}\) at \((\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2})\)Ch 7
📄 Question

Find and classify all the critical points of \(f(x,y)=x^2y^2\) subject to the constraint \(x+y=1\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Lagrange multipliers \(\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\) where \(g = x+y-1 = 0\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 7 Formula Sheet: "Lagrange Multiplier Method"
🔧 Setup

Since \(\nabla g = (1, 1)\) is constant and nonzero, the Lagrange equations simplify: both components of \(\nabla f\) equal the same \(\lambda\). This lets us eliminate \(\lambda\) by setting them equal to each other.

📝 Full Solution
1
Compute the gradients

\[\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2y^2),\;\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2y^2)\right) = (2xy^2,\; 2x^2y)\]

\[\nabla g = (1,\; 1)\]

For f = x²y², use the product rule: f_x = 2xy² (treat y as constant), f_y = 2x²y (treat x as constant).

2
Write the Lagrange equations

\[\text{(i)}\quad 2xy^2 = \lambda \cdot 1 = \lambda\]

\[\text{(ii)}\quad 2x^2y = \lambda \cdot 1 = \lambda\]

Since both equal \(\lambda\), set them equal to each other:

\[2xy^2 = 2x^2y\]

Tip: when ∇g = (1,1), the Lagrange equations say f_x = f_y = λ. Eliminating λ by setting f_x = f_y is the fastest move.

3
Solve \(2xy^2 = 2x^2y\)

Rearrange: \(2xy^2 - 2x^2y = 0\).

Factor: \(2xy(y - x) = 0\).

Three cases: \(x = 0\), or \(y = 0\), or \(y = x\).

Tip: don't divide by xy — you'd lose the x=0 and y=0 solutions. Always factor instead of dividing by a variable that could be zero.

4
Apply the constraint \(x + y = 1\) to each case

Case 1: \(x = 0\) → \(0 + y = 1\) → \(y = 1\). Point: \((0, 1)\).

\[f(0, 1) = 0^2 \cdot 1^2 = 0\]

Case 2: \(y = 0\) → \(x + 0 = 1\) → \(x = 1\). Point: \((1, 0)\).

\[f(1, 0) = 1^2 \cdot 0^2 = 0\]

Case 3: \(x = y\) → \(x + x = 1\) → \(x = \frac{1}{2}\), \(y = \frac{1}{2}\). Point: \((\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\).

\[f\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}\cdot\tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{16}\]

Tip: always check ALL cases from the factoring. On exams, forgetting the x=0 or y=0 cases loses marks.

5
Classify by comparing f-values

The three critical values are: \(f = 0\) (at two points) and \(f = \frac{1}{16}\) (at one point).

Since \(f = x^2y^2 \ge 0\) always, the smallest value 0 is a minimum and the largest value \(\frac{1}{16}\) is a maximum.

Since f = x²y² ≥ 0 for all (x,y), we know 0 is the global min on the constraint. The constraint x+y=1 is a line (unbounded), but f→∞ as we move far along it, so 1/16 is a local max.

✅ Answer: Min = 0 at \((0,1)\) and \((1,0)\). Max = \(\frac{1}{16}\) at \((\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2})\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Lagrange on a Linear Constraint
1Compute \(\nabla f\) and \(\nabla g\)
2If \(\nabla g = (1,1)\): set \(f_x = f_y\) to eliminate λ
3FACTOR (never divide by variables) → list all cases
4Apply constraint to each case → find points
5Evaluate f at each point → compare to classify
🧩 Takeaway — F14 B1

What you learn: Lagrange with a LINEAR constraint (\(x+y=1\)). Dividing the two gradient equations eliminates λ cleanly. Linear constraints are the easiest — you get a direct relationship between variables.
Variations: The constraint could be \(2x+3y=6\) or any linear equation. Same technique: divide to eliminate λ, substitute into constraint.

F13B1 [8 marks] — Lagrange Multipliers (Linear f on Ellipsoid) · Max = 2, Min = −2Ch 7
📄 Question

Using Lagrange multipliers, determine the local extrema of \(f(x,y,z)=x+2y-2z\) subject to \(x^2+2y^2+4z^2=1\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Lagrange multipliers \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\) where \(g=0\) is the constraint NOT on exam sheet → Ch 7 Formula Sheet: "Lagrange Multiplier Method"
🔧 Setup

Define \(g(x,y,z)=x^2+2y^2+4z^2-1\). We need \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\) plus the constraint \(g=0\). Since \(f\) is linear, \(\nabla f\) is constant — this makes the algebra cleaner.

📝 Full Solution
1
Compute the gradients

\[\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right) = (1, 2, -2)\]

\[\nabla g = \left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial x},\frac{\partial g}{\partial y},\frac{\partial g}{\partial z}\right) = (2x, 4y, 8z)\]

Differentiate f and g component by component. Since f is linear, ∇f has no variables.

2
Write the three Lagrange equations: \(\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\)

\[\text{(i)}\quad 1 = \lambda\cdot 2x \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad x = \frac{1}{2\lambda}\]

\[\text{(ii)}\quad 2 = \lambda\cdot 4y \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad y = \frac{2}{4\lambda} = \frac{1}{2\lambda}\]

\[\text{(iii)}\quad -2 = \lambda\cdot 8z \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad z = \frac{-2}{8\lambda} = \frac{-1}{4\lambda}\]

Each component gives one equation. Isolate x, y, z in terms of λ. Note: λ ≠ 0 (if λ = 0, the left sides would be 0, but they're 1, 2, −2).

3
Substitute into the constraint \(x^2 + 2y^2 + 4z^2 = 1\)

\[\left(\frac{1}{2\lambda}\right)^2 + 2\left(\frac{1}{2\lambda}\right)^2 + 4\left(\frac{-1}{4\lambda}\right)^2 = 1\]

\[\frac{1}{4\lambda^2} + \frac{2}{4\lambda^2} + \frac{4}{16\lambda^2} = 1\]

Find common denominator \(16\lambda^2\):

\[\frac{4}{16\lambda^2} + \frac{8}{16\lambda^2} + \frac{4}{16\lambda^2} = 1\]

\[\frac{16}{16\lambda^2} = 1 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = 1 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \lambda = \pm 1\]

Tip: when substituting fractions, find a common denominator before combining. Here everything reduces to 1/λ² = 1.

4
Back-substitute each λ to find the critical points

Case λ = 1: \(x = \frac{1}{2}\), \(y = \frac{1}{2}\), \(z = \frac{-1}{4}\).

\[f\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{1}{4}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2} + 2\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) - 2\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{4}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2} + 1 + \tfrac{1}{2} = 2\]

Case λ = −1: \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\), \(y = -\frac{1}{2}\), \(z = \frac{1}{4}\).

\[f\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{4}\right) = -\tfrac{1}{2} + 2\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\right) - 2\!\left(\tfrac{1}{4}\right) = -\tfrac{1}{2} - 1 - \tfrac{1}{2} = -2\]

Tip: always verify that each critical point satisfies the constraint. Here: (1/2)² + 2(1/2)² + 4(1/4)² = 1/4 + 1/2 + 1/4 = 1 ✓

5
Classify

Since the ellipsoid is a closed bounded surface, f must attain a max and min on it. The larger value is the max, the smaller is the min.

On a closed bounded constraint surface, the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees both a max and a min exist.

✅ Answer: Max = 2 at \((\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{4})\). Min = −2 at \((-\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{4})\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Lagrange (Linear f)
1Compute \(\nabla f\) and \(\nabla g\)
2Set up \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\) → solve each variable for λ
3Substitute into constraint → solve for λ
4Back-substitute → evaluate f at each critical point
5Verify points satisfy constraint
🧩 Takeaway — F13 B1

What you learn: Lagrange on an ELLIPSOID constraint (\(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R^2\) or similar). 3D version with three gradient equations. The technique is the same but more algebra. Watch for ± solutions (you'll get multiple candidates).
Key insight: When f is linear and g is quadratic, you often get λ from the gradient equations directly, then the constraint gives the final values.

F17B1 [10 marks] — Lagrange Multipliers (Cubic on Ellipse) · Max = 64, Min = −64Ch 7
📄 Question

Use Lagrange Multipliers to find the maximum and minimum values of \(f(x,y)=x^3-8y^3\) on the curve \(x^2+4y^2=16\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Lagrange multipliers \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 7 Formula Sheet: "Lagrange Multiplier Method"
🔧 Setup

The constraint \(x^2+4y^2=16\) is an ellipse with semi-axes \(a=4\), \(b=2\). We need \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\) where \(g=x^2+4y^2-16\).

📝 Full Solution
1
Compute gradients and write Lagrange equations

\(\nabla f=(3x^2,-24y^2)\), \(\nabla g=(2x,8y)\).

\[\text{(i)}\quad 3x^2=2\lambda x \qquad \text{(ii)}\quad -24y^2=8\lambda y\]

Each component of ∇f equals λ times the corresponding component of ∇g.

2
Solve equation (i): \(3x^2=2\lambda x\)

\(x(3x-2\lambda)=0\). So \(x=0\) or \(\lambda=\frac{3x}{2}\).

Factor out x. Never divide by x — it could be zero.

3
Solve equation (ii): \(-24y^2=8\lambda y\)

\(y(-24y-8\lambda)=0\). So \(y=0\) or \(\lambda=-3y\).

Factor out y. Same caution — don't divide by y.

4
Case 1: \(x=0\)

Constraint: \(4y^2=16\Rightarrow y=\pm 2\).

\(f(0,2)=-8(8)=-64\). \(f(0,-2)=-8(-8)=64\).

5
Case 2: \(y=0\)

Constraint: \(x^2=16\Rightarrow x=\pm 4\).

\(f(4,0)=64\). \(f(-4,0)=-64\).

6
Case 3: \(x\ne 0\) and \(y\ne 0\)

Set \(\lambda=\frac{3x}{2}=-3y\), so \(x=-2y\).

Constraint: \((-2y)^2+4y^2=16\Rightarrow 8y^2=16\Rightarrow y=\pm\sqrt{2}\).

\(x=-2\sqrt{2},\;y=\sqrt{2}\): \(f=-16\sqrt{2}-16\sqrt{2}=-32\sqrt{2}\approx -45.3\)

\(x=2\sqrt{2},\;y=-\sqrt{2}\): \(f=16\sqrt{2}+16\sqrt{2}=32\sqrt{2}\approx 45.3\)

Eliminate λ by equating the two expressions. Then substitute into the constraint.

7
Compare all f-values

\(f=64\) at \((4,0)\) and \((0,-2)\). \(f=-64\) at \((-4,0)\) and \((0,2)\). \(f=\pm 32\sqrt{2}\approx\pm 45.3\).

Maximum = 64. Minimum = −64.

The cubic terms make the extrema occur at the axis points, not the interior case.

✅ Answer: Max = 64 at \((4,0)\) and \((0,-2)\). Min = −64 at \((-4,0)\) and \((0,2)\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Lagrange on an Ellipse
1Write \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\), FACTOR (don't divide)
2Check x=0, y=0, and x≠0∧y≠0 cases
3Substitute each into constraint → find points
4Evaluate f at ALL candidates → compare
🧩 Takeaway — F17 B1

What you learn: Lagrange with a cubic objective on an ellipse constraint. This is the hardest variation — the algebra gets messy. Key: don't panic, just follow the mechanical steps. Divide gradient equations to eliminate λ, substitute, solve.
Big numbers: If your max/min values are large (like ±64), that's normal for cubic objectives. Don't assume you made an error just because the answer is big.

W19A6 — Conservative Field Test (Curl = 0) · \(\mathbf{F} = (ze^{xz}\sin y,\; e^{xz}\cos y,\; xe^{xz}\sin y)\)Ch 3
📄 Question

Which of the following vector fields is conservative in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) (or \(\mathbb{R}^3\))?

(a) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (x + e^y, y + e^{xy})\)
(b) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (ze^{xz}\sin y,\; e^{xz}\cos y,\; xe^{xz}\sin y)\)
(c) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (x^2y^2 + 1,\; x^2y^2 + 2)\)
(d) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (z, y, x^2)\)
(e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Test each field for the conservative condition: in 2D, check \(\partial P/\partial y = \partial Q/\partial x\). In 3D, check \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) (all components of curl are zero).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl (3D) \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\right)\mathbf{i} - \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}\right)\mathbf{j} + \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\mathbf{k}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Conservative condition \(\mathbf{F} = \nabla f \iff \nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"
📝 Full Solution
1
Test option (b): \(P = ze^{xz}\sin y\), \(Q = e^{xz}\cos y\), \(R = xe^{xz}\sin y\)

Check \(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\):

\(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} = xe^{xz}\cos y\), \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z} = xe^{xz}\cos y\). Difference = 0. ✓

Check \(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}\):

\(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x} = e^{xz}\sin y + xze^{xz}\sin y\), \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial z} = e^{xz}\sin y + xze^{xz}\sin y\). Difference = 0. ✓

Check \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\):

\(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = ze^{xz}\cos y\), \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = ze^{xz}\cos y\). Difference = 0. ✓

All three components of curl F are zero, so the field is conservative.

✅ Answer: (b) \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (ze^{xz}\sin y,\; e^{xz}\cos y,\; xe^{xz}\sin y)\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Conservative Field Test
1In 2D: check \(P_y = Q_x\). In 3D: check all 3 components of \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = 0\)
2Test each option until one passes
3Shortcut: if any component pair fails, move on
🔑 Elimination Strategy — Fail Fast on 2D Options First

The 2D choices (a) and (c) only need ONE check: \(P_y \stackrel{?}{=} Q_x\). For (a): \(P_y = e^y\) vs \(Q_x = ye^{xy}\) — different, eliminated. For (c): \(P_y = 2x^2y\) vs \(Q_x = 2xy^2\) — different, eliminated. For (d): \(R_x = 0\) vs \(P_z = 1\) — different, eliminated in one check. Only (b) survives.

🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — Guess the Potential Function

For (b), notice the structure: \(e^{xz}\) appears everywhere and \(\sin y / \cos y\) alternate. Guess \(f = e^{xz}\sin y\). Check: \(f_x = ze^{xz}\sin y = P\) ✓, \(f_y = e^{xz}\cos y = Q\) ✓, \(f_z = xe^{xz}\sin y = R\) ✓. Done — (b) is conservative by inspection, no curl computation needed.

🧩 Takeaway — W19 A6

What you learn: MCQ format — given 4-5 vector fields, find which one is conservative. Strategy: test each with curl = 0 (3D) or Qx = Py (2D). Fail-fast: test 2D options first (fewer components to differentiate).
Speed trick: If you can guess the potential function by inspection (e.g., \(f = e^{xz}\sin y\) for this one), verify by checking \(\nabla f = \mathbf{F}\).

W19B5 [2+5 marks] — Conservative Test + Find Potential (Integrate Components) · \(f = xe^{y+z} + C\)Ch 3
📄 Question

Let \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = e^{y+z}\,\mathbf{i} + xe^{y+z}\,\mathbf{j} + xe^{y+z}\,\mathbf{k}\). Determine whether F is a gradient field and if so, find its potential function \(f(x,y,z)\). If no such function \(f\) exists, then explain why not.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Curl — check if \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) Exam Sheet #21: "Curl"
2
Potential function condition Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"
📝 Full Solution
Part A
Check if conservative (2 marks)
1
Identify components and compute curl

\(P = e^{y+z}\), \(Q = xe^{y+z}\), \(R = xe^{y+z}\).

i-component: \(R_y - Q_z\)

\(R_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xe^{y+z}) = xe^{y+z}\)

\(Q_z = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(xe^{y+z}) = xe^{y+z}\)

\(R_y - Q_z = xe^{y+z} - xe^{y+z} = 0\) ✓

j-component: \(-(R_x - P_z)\)

\(R_x = e^{y+z}\), \(P_z = e^{y+z}\)

\(R_x - P_z = 0\) ✓

k-component: \(Q_x - P_y\)

\(Q_x = e^{y+z}\), \(P_y = e^{y+z}\)

\(Q_x - P_y = 0\) ✓

All three components of curl F are zero. This confirms F is conservative (a gradient field). Tip: notice the pattern — all cross-partials match because the exponential e^{y+z} behaves the same under ∂/∂y and ∂/∂z.

Part B
Find the potential function (5 marks)
2
Integrate \(f_x = P = e^{y+z}\) with respect to x

\[f = \int e^{y+z}\,dx = xe^{y+z} + g(y,z)\]

When integrating with respect to x, treat y and z as constants. The "constant of integration" g can depend on y and z — we don't know it yet.

3
Differentiate f with respect to y and match to Q

\[f_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xe^{y+z}) + g_y = xe^{y+z} + g_y\]

We need \(f_y = Q = xe^{y+z}\). Comparing:

\[xe^{y+z} + g_y = xe^{y+z} \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad g_y = 0\]

So \(g\) does not depend on y: \(g(y,z) = h(z)\).

Tip: if g_y = 0, it means the x-integration in step 2 already captured ALL of the y-dependence. This is common when the original F has a clean structure.

4
Differentiate f with respect to z and match to R

\[f_z = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(xe^{y+z}) + h'(z) = xe^{y+z} + h'(z)\]

We need \(f_z = R = xe^{y+z}\). Comparing:

\[xe^{y+z} + h'(z) = xe^{y+z} \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad h'(z) = 0\]

So \(h(z) = C\) (a constant).

Both unknown functions (g and h) turned out to be constants — the potential is simply xe^{y+z}.

5
Assemble and verify

\[\boxed{f(x,y,z) = xe^{y+z} + C}\]

Verification:

\(\nabla f = (e^{y+z},\; xe^{y+z},\; xe^{y+z}) = \mathbf{F}\) ✓

Tip: always verify by computing ∇f and checking it matches F. This catches sign errors and earns confidence marks on exams.

✅ Answer: F is conservative. Potential: \(f(x,y,z) = xe^{y+z} + C\).
⚡ Quick Guide — Finding a Potential Function
1Check: curl F = 0? (all 3 components)
2Integrate f_x = P w.r.t. x → f = ... + g(y,z)
3Differentiate w.r.t. y, match to Q → find g_y
4Differentiate w.r.t. z, match to R → find h'(z)
5Verify: \(\nabla f = \mathbf{F}\)?
🧩 Takeaway — W19 B5

What you learn: Two-part problem: (a) prove conservative (show curl = 0), then (b) find the potential. This tests the full pipeline. Integration order: integrate P w.r.t. x → get f + g(y,z) → differentiate w.r.t. y, match with Q → determine g → verify with R.
Common mistake: Forgetting the +g(y,z) after the first integration. Every integration step adds an unknown function of the OTHER variables.

W19A7 — Mass of Cable (Scalar Line Integral, ds) · \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}\)Ch 4
📄 Question

The mass density \(\delta(x,y)\), in kg/m, of a linear cable of length \(L\) with initial point at \((0,0)\) and endpoint at \((L,L)\) is given by \(\delta(x,y) = xy\). Find the mass of the cable.

(a) \(\frac{L^3}{3}\)    (b) \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^2}{2}\)    (c) \(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}\)    (d) \(\frac{2L^3}{3}\)    (e) None of these

1
Scalar line integral \(\int_C f\,ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\) Exam Sheet #22: "Scalar Line Integral (2D)"
📝 Full Solution
1
Parametrize the cable

\[\mathbf{r}(t) = (t, t),\quad 0 \le t \le L\]

The straight line from (0,0) to (L,L) has equal x and y components, so x=t, y=t.

2
Compute \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\)

\[\mathbf{r}'(t) = (1, 1),\quad |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}\]

The speed factor ds/dt tells us how much arc length each dt contributes.

3

\[\text{Mass} = \int_0^L (t)(t)\sqrt{2}\,dt = \sqrt{2}\int_0^L t^2\,dt = \sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{L^3}{3} = \boxed{\frac{\sqrt{2}\,L^3}{3}}\]

Substitute the parametrization into δ(x,y) = xy, multiply by |r'(t)|, and integrate.

1
Vector line integral \(\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \int_a^b [P\,x'(t) + Q\,y'(t)]\,dt\) Exam Sheet #24: "Vector Line Integral (Work)"
2
Conservative test (2D) \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\) Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"

\(P = x^2 - y\), \(Q = y^2 + x\).

\(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = -1\), \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = 1\). Since \(-1 \neq 1\), the field is NOT conservative.

If the field were conservative, we could use FTCLI. Since it's not, we must parametrize and compute directly.

2
Parametrize the parabola \(y = x^2\)

Let \(x = t\), \(y = t^2\), \(0 \le t \le 1\). Then \(dx = dt\), \(dy = 2t\,dt\).

The parabola through (0,0) and (1,1) is y = x², parametrized by x = t.

🔑 Early Elimination — Half the Integrand Vanishes

On the parabola \(y = x^2\), the P-component becomes \(x^2 - y = x^2 - x^2 = 0\). The entire \(P\,dx\) term drops out! You only need to compute \(\int_C (y^2+x)\,dy\). This cuts the work in half and makes the arithmetic simple enough to do mentally: \(\int_0^1 (t^4+t)(2t)\,dt = \int_0^1 2t^5+2t^2\,dt = \frac{1}{3}+\frac{2}{3} = 1\). Only (d) matches.

3
Substitute and integrate

\[\int_0^1 [(t^2 - t^2)(1) + (t^4 + t)(2t)]\,dt = \int_0^1 [0 + 2t^5 + 2t^2]\,dt\]

\[= \left[\frac{t^6}{3} + \frac{2t^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{2}{3} = \boxed{1}\]

Replace x, y, dx, dy with their parametric expressions and simplify before integrating.

✅ Answer: (d) \(1\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Vector Line Integral (Direct Parametrization)
1Check conservative first: \(P_y = Q_x\)? If yes → FTCLI
2If not: parametrize \(C\), compute \(dx, dy\) in terms of \(dt\)
3Substitute and integrate \(\int [P\,x'(t) + Q\,y'(t)]\,dt\)
🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — Green's Theorem Detour

Close the curve by adding the line segment \(L\) from \((1,1)\) back to \((0,0)\). Then \(\oint_{C+L} = \iint_R (Q_x - P_y)\,dA = \iint_R (1-(-1))\,dA = 2 \cdot \text{Area}\). The region enclosed by the parabola and the line \(y=x\) has area \(\int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx = \frac{1}{6}\). So \(\oint_{C+L} = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3}\). On \(L\), parametrize \((1-t, 1-t)\) for \(t \in [0,1]\): integral = \(\int_0^1 [(1-t)^2-(1-t)](-1) + [(1-t)^2+(1-t)](-1)\,dt = \int_0^1 -2(1-t)^2\,dt = -\frac{2}{3}\). So \(\int_C = \frac{1}{3} - (-\frac{2}{3}) = 1\). Same answer, useful if you prefer Green's.

🧩 Takeaway — W19 A11

What you learn: Direct vector line integral — parametrize, substitute, integrate. No shortcuts here, just mechanics. Good drill for when the field ISN'T conservative.

F14A11 — Line Integral (Conservative Field → FTCLI) · \(236\)Ch 4
📄 Question

Evaluate \(\int_C [(6xy^2-y^3)\,dx + (6x^2y-3xy^2)\,dy]\), where \(C\) is the line segment from \((1,2)\) to \((3,4)\).

(a) 0    (b) 236    (c) 188    (d) 48    (e) 136
🧠 What is this asking?

Check if \(\mathbf{F} = (6xy^2-y^3,\; 6x^2y-3xy^2)\) is conservative. If so, use the Fundamental Theorem of Line Integrals for a quick path-independent answer.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Conservative test \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\) Exam Sheet #27: "Potential Function Condition"
2
FTCLI \(\int_C \nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B)-f(A)\) Exam Sheet #25: "Fundamental Thm of Line Integrals"
📝 Full Solution
1
Check conservative

\(P_y = 12xy - 3y^2\), \(Q_x = 12xy - 3y^2\). Equal ✓ → conservative.

If P_y = Q_x, the field is conservative and we can use FTCLI.

🔑 Early Elimination — Conservative → Skip Parametrization

The moment you confirm \(P_y = Q_x = 12xy - 3y^2\), you know the field is conservative. This means: (1) the answer is path-independent — you don't need to parametrize the line segment at all, and (2) you need FTCLI, which avoids integration along C entirely. Also, (a) 0 is unlikely since the endpoints differ and the field isn't symmetric — tentatively eliminate it.

2
Find potential function

\(f_x = 6xy^2-y^3 \Rightarrow f = 3x^2y^2-xy^3+g(y)\)

\(f_y = 6x^2y-3xy^2+g'(y) = 6x^2y-3xy^2 \Rightarrow g'(y) = 0 \Rightarrow g = C\)

\(f = 3x^2y^2-xy^3\)

Integrate P w.r.t. x, then match f_y to Q to determine g(y).

3
Apply FTCLI

\[f(3,4)-f(1,2) = [3(9)(16)-3(64)] - [3(1)(4)-1(8)] = [432-192]-[12-8] = 240-4 = \boxed{236}\]

For conservative fields, the line integral depends only on endpoints — not the path.

✅ Answer: (b) \(236\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Line Integral via FTCLI
1Check: \(P_y = Q_x\)? → conservative
2Find potential: integrate \(f_x = P\), match \(f_y = Q\)
3Answer = \(f(\text{end}) - f(\text{start})\)
🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — Spot the Potential by Inspection

The terms \(6xy^2\) and \(6x^2y\) suggest \(f\) contains \(3x^2y^2\) (check: \(f_x = 6xy^2\) ✓, \(f_y = 6x^2y\) ✓). The terms \(-y^3\) in P and \(-3xy^2\) in Q suggest subtracting \(xy^3\) (check: partial in x gives \(-y^3\) ✓, partial in y gives \(-3xy^2\) ✓). So \(f = 3x^2y^2 - xy^3\) by inspection — no integration needed. Then \(f(3,4)-f(1,2) = (432-192)-(12-8) = 236\).

🧩 Takeaway — F14 A11

What you learn: FTCLI shortcut — if F is conservative (verify first!), then \(\int_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B) - f(A)\). Find potential, plug in endpoints, subtract. This is the fastest way to evaluate a line integral.
Exam tip: If MCQ choices include a clean integer, the field is probably conservative. Check Py = Qx first.

Green's Theorem — §6.3

Green's = Stokes' in 2D. Converts a closed curve line integral into a double integral. Key formula: \(\oint P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint(Q_x - P_y)\,dA\). Often makes impossible integrals (like \(\int e^{y^2}\)) tractable.

W19B1 [8 marks] — Green's Theorem (Closed Curve → Double Integral) · \(I = 12\pi\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Evaluate \(I = \int_C -y\,dx + x\,dy\) over the perimeter \(C\) of the closed curve \(4x^2 + 9y^2 = 36\) oriented in a counterclockwise direction.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Green's Theorem \(\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_R \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA\) Exam Sheet #35: "Green's Theorem"
2
Ellipse area \(A = \pi ab\) NOT on exam sheet → memorize
🔧 Setup

Green's Theorem applies because: (1) C is a closed curve, (2) oriented counterclockwise (positive orientation), and (3) the region R enclosed by the ellipse is simply connected. The pair P = −y, Q = x is special — it gives Q_x − P_y = 2, which is the "double the area" formula.

📝 Full Solution
1
Identify P and Q from the line integral

\(\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy\) with \(P = -y\), \(Q = x\).

Match the integrand to the form P dx + Q dy. Here: the coefficient of dx is P = −y, the coefficient of dy is Q = x.

2
Compute \(Q_x - P_y\)

\[\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x) = 1\]

\[\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-y) = -1\]

\[Q_x - P_y = 1 - (-1) = 1 + 1 = 2\]

Tip: P = −y, Q = x always gives Q_x − P_y = 2. This is worth memorizing — it turns any closed line integral of (−y dx + x dy) into 2 × Area.

3
Apply Green's Theorem

\[I = \iint_R (Q_x - P_y)\,dA = \iint_R 2\,dA = 2\cdot\text{Area}(R)\]

Since the integrand Q_x − P_y = 2 is a constant, the double integral equals that constant times the area of R.

4
Convert the ellipse to standard form and find its area

\[4x^2 + 9y^2 = 36 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \frac{x^2}{9} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1\]

Reading off: \(a^2 = 9 \Rightarrow a = 3\), \(b^2 = 4 \Rightarrow b = 2\).

\[\text{Area} = \pi ab = \pi(3)(2) = 6\pi\]

Tip: divide both sides by the RHS (36) to get 1 on the right. The denominators under x² and y² are a² and b².

5
Final answer

\[I = 2 \cdot 6\pi = \boxed{12\pi}\]

The line integral around the ellipse equals twice its area — an elegant result.

✅ Answer: \(I = 12\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Green's Theorem on a Closed Curve
1Check: closed curve, CCW orientation, simply connected region
2Identify P and Q, compute \(Q_x - P_y\)
3If constant → integral = constant × Area
4For ellipse: standard form → \(\pi ab\)
5Memorize: \(-y\,dx+x\,dy\) → \(Q_x-P_y = 2\) → twice the area
🧩 Takeaway — W19 B1

What you learn: The "impossible integrand" trick — the line integral contains \(e^{y^2}\) which has no closed-form antiderivative, but Green's converts it to \(\iint(Q_x - P_y)\,dA\) where the nasty term cancels out. If you see an impossible integrand on a closed curve → Green's is the way.
Watch for: Orientation! Green's requires CCW (positive). If the curve is CW, negate the answer.

W19A1 — Equation of a Plane (Perpendicular to Another, Through 2 Points) · \(-x + 4y + 3z = 10\)Ch 2
📄 Question

Find the equation of the plane \(\Pi_1\) through the two points \(P(0,1,2)\) and \(Q(-1,0,3)\) that is perpendicular to the plane \(\Pi_2\) given by \(2x - 4y + 6z = 2\).

(a) \(-2x + 3y + z = 5\)    (b) \(2x - y + z = 1\)    (c) \(-x + 4y + 3z = 10\)    (d) \(-x + y = 1\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Find a plane that (1) contains both points P and Q, and (2) is perpendicular to the given plane \(\Pi_2\). "Perpendicular planes" means their normal vectors are perpendicular.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Cross product \(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\u_1&u_2&u_3\\v_1&v_2&v_3\end{vmatrix}\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 1 Formula Sheet: "Cross Product"
2
Plane equation \(a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0\) where \(\mathbf{n}=(a,b,c)\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 2 notes: plane from normal + point
3
Perpendicular planes → their normals satisfy \(\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2=0\) NOT on exam sheet → Ch 1 Formula Sheet: "Dot Product"
🔧 Setup — How to Recognize This

Plane through 2 points + perpendicular to another plane. You have two vectors that \(\mathbf{n}_1\) must be perpendicular to: (1) the direction \(\vec{PQ}\) (so the plane contains line PQ), and (2) the normal \(\mathbf{n}_2\) of \(\Pi_2\) (so the planes are perpendicular). Cross product gives a vector perpendicular to both.

📝 Full Solution
1
Find \(\vec{PQ}\)

\[\vec{PQ} = Q - P = (-1-0,\; 0-1,\; 3-2) = (-1, -1, 1)\]

We need a direction vector that lies IN the desired plane. Two points on the plane give us one.

2
Read off \(\mathbf{n}_2\) from \(\Pi_2\)

The plane \(2x - 4y + 6z = 2\) has normal \(\mathbf{n}_2 = (2, -4, 6)\).

The coefficients of x, y, z in a plane equation are always the components of its normal vector.

3
Cross product \(\mathbf{n}_1 = \vec{PQ}\times\mathbf{n}_2\)

\[\mathbf{n}_1 = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\-1&-1&1\\2&-4&6\end{vmatrix}\]

\[= \mathbf{i}[(-1)(6)-(1)(-4)] - \mathbf{j}[(-1)(6)-(1)(2)] + \mathbf{k}[(-1)(-4)-(-1)(2)]\]

\[= \mathbf{i}[-6+4] - \mathbf{j}[-6-2] + \mathbf{k}[4+2] = (-2, 8, 6)\]

Simplify by dividing by \(-2\): \(\mathbf{n}_1 = (1, -4, -3)\) or equivalently \((-1, 4, 3)\).

The cross product gives a vector perpendicular to BOTH inputs — exactly what we need for \(\mathbf{n}_1\).

🔑 Early Elimination — You already know it's (c)

The normal \(\mathbf{n}_1 = (-1, 4, 3)\) means the plane equation looks like \(-x + 4y + 3z = d\). Scan the choices: only (c) has the form \(-x + 4y + 3z = \text{something}\). You can circle (c) and move on — no need to compute \(d\).

4
Write plane equation using \(P(0,1,2)\)

\[-1(x-0) + 4(y-1) + 3(z-2) = 0\]

\[-x + 4y - 4 + 3z - 6 = 0\]

\[\boxed{-x + 4y + 3z = 10}\]

Plug any known point on the plane into the normal-form equation to find the constant d.

✅ Answer: (c) \(-x + 4y + 3z = 10\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Plane Through 2 Points, Perpendicular to Another Plane
1Compute direction \(\vec{PQ} = Q - P\)
2Read off normal \(\mathbf{n}_2\) from the given plane
3Cross product: \(\mathbf{n}_1 = \vec{PQ}\times\mathbf{n}_2\)
4Simplify \(\mathbf{n}_1\) (divide out common factors)
5Plug one point into \(a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0\)
6Verify: plug BOTH points to confirm
🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — Plug-and-Check

Instead of computing the normal, test each answer choice directly: plug both \(P(0,1,2)\) and \(Q(-1,0,3)\) into each plane equation and check that both satisfy it. Also verify the normal of that plane is perpendicular to \(\mathbf{n}_2 = (2,-4,6)\) via the dot product.

For (c): \(-0+4(1)+3(2) = 10\) ✓ and \(-(-1)+4(0)+3(3) = 1+9 = 10\) ✓. Then \(\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2 = (-1)(2)+(4)(-4)+(3)(6) = -2-16+18 = 0\) ✓. Done — no cross product needed.

🧩 Takeaway — W19 A1

What you learn: Plane through 2 points, perpendicular to another plane. Need two vectors in the plane (one from PQ, one from ⊥ condition), cross them for normal. On MCQ, often faster to just plug points into each answer choice.
Variations: "Through 3 points" (cross PQ × PR), "containing a line and perpendicular to a plane" (cross direction × normal).

F17A7 — Chain Rule (∂w/∂t with Multiple Dependencies) · \(18\)Ch 3
📄 Question

1

📝 Full Solution
1

W19A8 — Reverse Integration Order (Sketch Region, Swap Bounds) · \(\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)Ch 5
📄 Question

Which one of the following iterated integrals is equal to \(\int_0^1\int_x^1 e^{y^2}\,dy\,dx\)?

(a) \(\int_0^1\int_y^1 e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (b) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y x\,e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (c) \(\int_0^1\int_y^1 y\,e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (d) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

The original integral can't be evaluated as written (no elementary antiderivative for \(e^{y^2}\) with respect to y). Reversing the order of integration might make it doable.

🔑 Early Elimination — Before Any Work

When you reverse integration order, the integrand doesn't change — only the bounds and the order of \(dx\,dy\) swap. The integrand is \(e^{y^2}\), so the reversed form must also have \(e^{y^2}\) with no extra factors. This immediately eliminates (b) (has extra \(x\)) and (c) (has extra \(y\)). You're down to (a) or (d) before doing any geometry.

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Double integral (iterated) \(\iint_R f\,dA\) Exam Sheet #26: "Double Integral (Iterated)"
2
Reversing order technique — sketch the region, rewrite bounds NOT on exam sheet → Ch 5 technique, memorize
📝 Full Solution
1
Identify the region from the original bounds

Original: \(0 \le x \le 1\), \(x \le y \le 1\).

This is the triangle above \(y = x\) and below \(y = 1\), with \(x \in [0,1]\).

Read the bounds: x goes from 0 to 1, then for each x, y goes from x up to 1.

2
Reverse: integrate x first

For a fixed \(y\): \(x\) ranges from \(0\) to \(y\) (since \(x \le y\)).

And \(y\) ranges from \(0\) to \(1\).

\[\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\]

Same triangular region, but now we sweep horizontally: for each y, x goes from 0 to y.

✅ Answer: (d) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Reversing Integration Order
1Read the original bounds and identify the region
2Sketch the region in the xy-plane
3Re-describe: fix the OTHER variable, find new inner bounds
4Write the new outer bounds
5Sanity check: both describe the same region
🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — Eliminate Then Decide in 30 Seconds

After eliminating (b) and (c) (wrong integrands), you're left with (a) \(\int_0^1\int_y^1\) vs (d) \(\int_0^1\int_0^y\). The original inner integral is \(\int_x^1 dy\), meaning for fixed \(x\), \(y\) goes from \(x\) to 1 — the region is above the line \(y=x\). When you flip, for fixed \(y\), \(x\) goes from \(0\) to \(y\) (left side of triangle to the diagonal). That's \(\int_0^y dx\), which is (d).

🧩 Takeaway — W19 A8

What you learn: Reversing order makes \(e^{y^2}\) integrable. The technique: sketch the region from the original bounds, then re-describe it with the other variable as the outer integral. If the original integrand has no antiderivative in one variable → swap is mandatory.
On the exam: If you see \(\int e^{y^2}\), \(\int \sin(x^2)\), or \(\int \frac{\sin y}{y}\) as an inner integral, you MUST reverse the order.

W19A9 — Volume (Paraboloid Cut by Plane, Cylindrical Coords) · \(8\pi\)Ch 5
📄 Question

Find the volume of the solid region inside the paraboloid \(z = x^2 + y^2\) that is cut off by the plane \(z = 4\).

(a) \(8\pi\)    (b) \(\frac{81\pi}{2}\)    (c) \(16\pi\)    (d) \(4\pi\)    (e) None of these
🧠 What is this asking?

Find the volume of the "bowl" shape formed by the paraboloid below and the flat plane \(z=4\) on top. The intersection circle is at \(r = 2\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Cylindrical coordinates \(x = r\cos\theta,\; y = r\sin\theta,\; dV = r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\) Exam Sheet #39: "Cylindrical Coordinates"
📝 Full Solution
1
Convert to polar/cylindrical

The paraboloid \(z = x^2+y^2 = r^2\) meets \(z = 4\) when \(r^2 = 4\), so \(r = 2\).

Circular symmetry of the paraboloid makes polar coordinates natural. Find the boundary radius.

2
Set up the integral

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_{r^2}^4 r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\]

For each (r,θ), z ranges from the paraboloid (z = r²) up to the plane (z = 4).

3
Evaluate inner integral (z)

\[\int_{r^2}^4 dz = 4 - r^2\]

Integrating 1 with respect to z gives the height of the solid at radius r.

4
Evaluate middle integral (r)

\[\int_0^2 (4 - r^2)\,r\,dr = \int_0^2 (4r - r^3)\,dr = \left[2r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 8 - 4 = 4\]

Don't forget the Jacobian factor r. Expand and integrate term by term.

🔑 Early Elimination — You already know it's (a)

The r-integral gave 4, and the θ-integral will just multiply by \(2\pi\) (full revolution, no θ in the integrand). So \(V = 4 \cdot 2\pi = 8\pi\). Only (a) matches. Circle it — skip the last step.

5
Evaluate outer integral (θ)

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} 4\,d\theta = 4 \cdot 2\pi = \boxed{8\pi}\]

The integrand had no θ-dependence, so the θ integral just multiplies by 2π.

✅ Answer: (a) \(8\pi\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Volume by Cylindrical/Polar Coordinates
1Sketch the solid — identify top/bottom surfaces
2Find where they intersect to get the r-bound
3Set up \(\int\int\int r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\), z from bottom to top
4Evaluate inside-out: z, then r, then θ
🧩 Alternative Quick Solution — Paraboloid Cap Volume Formula

The volume under \(z = h\) and above \(z = r^2\) (i.e., \(z = x^2+y^2\)) is always \(V = \frac{1}{2}\pi R^2 h\), where \(R^2 = h\) is the boundary radius. Here \(h = 4\), \(R = 2\):

\[V = \tfrac{1}{2}\pi(4)(4) = 8\pi\]

If you memorize this, the question is a 5-second answer. (The formula comes from the fact that a paraboloid cap always holds exactly half the volume of the enclosing cylinder \(\pi R^2 h\).)

🧩 Takeaway — W19 A9

What you learn: Volume under a paraboloid cut by a plane — circular symmetry → cylindrical coordinates. \(dA = r\,dr\,d\theta\). The paraboloid \(z = r^2\) integrates to \(r^4/4\), which is clean.
Pattern: "Volume under [surface] over [circular region]" always means polar/cylindrical.

F17B2 [8 marks] — Reverse Integration Order (sin y / y Becomes Integrable) · \(2\)Ch 5
📄 Question

Draw the region of integration and evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\int_x^\pi \frac{\sin y}{y}\,dy\,dx\).

🔧 Setup

The inner integral \(\int_x^\pi\frac{\sin y}{y}\,dy\) has no closed form. But reversing the order of integration will make (sin y)/y cancel beautifully.

📝 Full Solution
1
Sketch the region

Original bounds: \(0\le x\le\pi\), \(x\le y\le\pi\). This is the triangle with vertices \((0,0)\), \((0,\pi)\), \((\pi,\pi)\) — above the line \(y=x\) and below \(y=\pi\).

Always sketch when reversing order. The region is a triangle in the first quadrant.

2
Reverse the order

For fixed y: x ranges from 0 to y. Then y ranges from 0 to π.

\[\int_0^\pi\int_0^y \frac{\sin y}{y}\,dx\,dy\]

The key insight: in the reversed order, x-integration comes first, and sin(y)/y is constant with respect to x.

3

\[\int_0^y \frac{\sin y}{y}\,dx = \frac{\sin y}{y}\cdot y = \sin y\]

The y in the denominator cancels with the y from the x-integration! This is why we reversed.

4

\[\int_0^\pi \sin y\,dy = [-\cos y]_0^\pi = -\cos\pi+\cos 0 = 1+1 = \boxed{2}\]

A clean, elegant result from what looked like an impossible integral.

1Sketch the region from the original bounds
2Re-describe: for fixed y, what are x's bounds?
3Inner integral of constant w.r.t. x → multiply by range

🧩 Takeaway — F17 B2

What you learn: Another "impossible integral" reversal — \(\sin y/y\) has no antiderivative w.r.t. y, but after reversing, you integrate \(\sin y\) w.r.t. y (easy). This is a LONG ANSWER on F17, showing this technique can appear anywhere.

W19B2 [7 marks] — Surface Integral (Odd Integrand on Symmetric Domain → 0) · \(I = 0\)Ch 6
📄 Question

Evaluate the surface integral \(I = \iint_S y\,dS\) where \(S\) is the hemisphere \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R^2\), \(z > 0\).

📋 Formulas Needed
1
Scalar surface integral \(\iint_S f\,dS = \iint_R f(\mathbf{r}(u,v))\,|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|\,dA\) Exam Sheet #32: "Scalar Surface Integral (param.)"
2
Symmetry shortcut: odd integrand on symmetric domain → integral = 0 NOT on exam sheet → general principle, memorize
🔧 Setup

Before computing anything, check for symmetry. The hemisphere is symmetric about the xz-plane (y = 0), and the integrand f = y is an odd function of y: replacing y with −y flips the sign. This means the integral must be zero — the positive and negative contributions cancel exactly.

📝 Full Solution
1
Identify the symmetry

The hemisphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = R^2\), \(z > 0\) is symmetric about the xz-plane (\(y = 0\)). For every point \((x, y, z)\) on S, the point \((x, -y, z)\) is also on S.

The integrand \(f = y\) is odd in y: \(f(x, -y, z) = -y = -f(x, y, z)\).

An odd function integrated over a symmetric domain always gives zero — the same reason ∫_{-a}^{a} x dx = 0.

2
Formal proof: split the hemisphere

Let \(S^+ = \{(x,y,z) \in S : y > 0\}\) and \(S^- = \{(x,y,z) \in S : y < 0\}\).

\[I = \iint_{S^+} y\,dS + \iint_{S^-} y\,dS = I^+ + I^-\]

Split the domain along the symmetry plane y = 0. The set where y = 0 exactly has measure zero (it's a curve on the surface), so it doesn't contribute.

3
Compute \(I^+\) using projection onto the xz-plane

On \(S^+\): \(y > 0\), so \(y = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - z^2}\). Write \(y = g(x,z)\).

The surface element: \(dS = \sqrt{1 + g_x^2 + g_z^2}\,dA\).

\[g_x = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-z^2}}, \qquad g_z = \frac{-z}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-z^2}}\]

\[1 + g_x^2 + g_z^2 = 1 + \frac{x^2+z^2}{R^2-x^2-z^2} = \frac{R^2}{R^2-x^2-z^2}\]

So \(dS = \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-z^2}}\,dA\).

Tip: for spheres, this surface element simplification always gives R/√(R²−x²−z²). It comes from the sphere's curvature stretching flat area elements.

4
The y factors cancel

\[I^+ = \iint_{R^+} \underbrace{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-z^2}}_{y} \cdot \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-z^2}}\,dA = R\iint_{R^+} dA\]

The projection \(R^+\) is the half-disk \(\{(x,z): x^2+z^2 \le R^2,\; x \in \mathbb{R}\}\) which has area \(\frac{\pi R^2}{2}\).

\[I^+ = R\cdot\frac{\pi R^2}{2} = \frac{\pi R^3}{2}\]

The key cancellation: y in the integrand equals √(R²−x²−z²), which is exactly what appears in the denominator of dS. They cancel, leaving just R.

5
By symmetry, \(I^- = -I^+\)

On \(S^-\): \(y = -\sqrt{R^2-x^2-z^2}\), so the same calculation gives \(I^- = -\frac{\pi R^3}{2}\).

The only difference is the sign of y. Everything else (dS, the projection region) is identical.

6
Add the two halves

\[I = I^+ + I^- = \frac{\pi R^3}{2} + \left(-\frac{\pi R^3}{2}\right) = \boxed{0}\]

Perfect cancellation due to the odd symmetry. On an exam, stating the symmetry argument clearly (odd function + symmetric domain) earns full marks without any computation.

✅ Answer: \(I = 0\)
⚡ Quick Guide — Symmetry Shortcut
1Is the integrand odd in x, y, or z?
2Is the domain symmetric about that variable's zero plane?
3Both yes → integral = 0 immediately
4On an exam: state it clearly, then optionally verify by splitting into halves
🧩 Takeaway — W19 B2

What you learn: The symmetry trick — if the integrand is ODD (e.g., \(f(-x) = -f(x)\)) and the domain is symmetric about the axis, the integral is 0. No computation needed. Recognize this pattern and save 10 minutes.
Exam trap: If you don't see the symmetry, you'll compute a long integral that equals zero. Always check for odd/even symmetry before integrating.

📄 Question

Find the upward flux of \(\mathbf{F}=(0,0,z)\) through \(z=4-x^2-y^2\) over \(x^2+y^2\le 4\).

📋 Formulas Needed

Flux (graph form) \(\iint(-Pz_x-Qz_y+R)\,dA\) Exam Sheet #33: "Flux Integral (z=z(x,y))"
📝 Full Solution
1
Apply flux formula

P=Q=0, R=z=4−x²−y². Formula gives: \(\iint R\,dA = \iint(4-x^2-y^2)\,dA\).

Since P=Q=0, the −Pz_x and −Qz_y terms vanish. Only R survives.

2
Convert to polar

\[\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(4-r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\left[2r^2-\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 2\pi(8-4) = \boxed{8\pi}\]

Circular region → polar. Don't forget the Jacobian r.

✅ Answer: \(8\pi\)
