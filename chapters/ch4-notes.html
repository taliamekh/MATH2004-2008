Chapter 4

Line Integrals

Integrating scalar and vector fields along curves — computing mass, work, circulation, and exploiting conservative fields for path independence.

§4.1 — Line Integrals with Respect to Arc Length

In single-variable calculus you integrate a function over an interval \([a,b]\). A line integral extends this idea: instead of integrating over a straight interval, you integrate a function along a curve \(\mathscr{C}\) in the plane or in space. The function \(f\) is evaluated at points on the curve, and you sum up \(f\) values weighted by tiny arc-length increments \(ds\).

Definition — Line Integral w.r.t. Arc Length

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be a regular curve parametrized by \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) for \(t\in[a,b]\), and let \(f\) be a continuous scalar field defined on \(\mathscr{C}\). Partition \(\mathscr{C}\) into subarcs of lengths \(\Delta s_i\), pick sample points \(P_i^*\) on each subarc, and form the Riemann sum \(\sum f(P_i^*)\Delta s_i\). If this limit exists as \(\max\{\Delta s_i\}\to 0\), we call it the line integral of \(f\) over \(\mathscr{C}\) with respect to arc length:

Line Integral (arc-length form)
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}} f(x,y,z)\,ds = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^{n}f(P_i^*)\,\Delta s_i\]
f — scalar field evaluated on the curveds — infinitesimal arc length𝒞 — the curve of integration

Key Properties

1. Setting \(f=1\) gives the arc length: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}ds = L\).

2. If \(\mathscr{C}=\mathscr{C}_1\cup\mathscr{C}_2\cup\cdots\cup\mathscr{C}_n\) (piecewise), then \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\int_{\mathscr{C}_i}f\,ds\).

3. If \(f\) is a mass density, then \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds=M\) (total mass of the wire).

4. The surface area of the "curtain" (sheet) under \(f\geq 0\) with base \(\mathscr{C}\) in the \(xy\)-plane equals \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f(x,y)\,ds\).

5. The value is independent of orientation — reversing the direction of \(\mathscr{C}\) gives the same answer.

Fig 4.1 — The line integral ∫𝒞 f ds equals the area of the "curtain" hanging from the curve z = f(x,y) down to the base curve 𝒞 in the xy-plane.

Computation Formulas

General Parametric (3D)
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}} f(x,y,z)\,ds = \int_a^b f\!\big(x(t),y(t),z(t)\big)\sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2+z'(t)^2}\;dt\]
r(t) = (x(t), y(t), z(t)) — parametrization|r′(t)| = √(x′²+y′²+z′²) — speed[a,b] — parameter interval

In compact notation: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds=\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\).

2D Special Case
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}} f(x,y)\,ds = \int_a^b f\!\big(x(t),y(t)\big)\sqrt{x'(t)^2+y'(t)^2}\;dt\]

If \(\mathscr{C}\) is parametrized by arc length (so \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=1\)), the formula simplifies to \(\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,dt\).

★ Strategy for Computing Line Integrals (Scalar)

Step 1: Parametrize the curve: find \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) and the interval \([a,b]\).

Step 2: Compute \(\mathbf{r}'(t)\) and then \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\).

Step 3: Substitute \(x=x(t),\,y=y(t),\,z=z(t)\) into \(f\) to get \(f(\mathbf{r}(t))\).

Step 4: Evaluate the single-variable integral \(\int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|\,dt\).

Example 4.1 — Line Integral of x Along a Parabola (Ex. 200)

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be the arc of the parabola \(y=x^2\) from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\). Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}x\,ds\) and show the value is independent of orientation.

Show Solution

Thought process: We need a parametrization of \(y=x^2\) from \((0,0)\) to \((1,1)\). The simplest choice: let \(t=x\).

Setup: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t,\,t^2)\), \(t\in[0,1]\). Then \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,2t)\), \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=\sqrt{1+4t^2}\).

1
Substitute into the integral: \(\int_0^1 t\sqrt{1+4t^2}\,dt\).
2
Let \(u=1+4t^2\), so \(du=8t\,dt\), i.e., \(t\,dt=du/8\). When \(t=0\), \(u=1\); when \(t=1\), \(u=5\).
3
\(=\frac{1}{8}\int_1^5\sqrt{u}\,du=\frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\Big|_1^5=\frac{1}{12}\left(5\sqrt{5}-1\right)\).

Answer: \(\displaystyle\frac{5\sqrt{5}-1}{12}\)

Orientation check: Reversing the curve (from \((1,1)\) to \((0,0)\)) uses \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t,(1-t)^2)\). A similar u-substitution yields the same value — confirming that line integrals w.r.t. arc length are orientation-independent.

Example 4.2 — 3D Helix Integral (Ex. 201)

Define \(\mathscr{C}\) by \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t\cos t,\,t\sin t,\,t)\) for \(t\in[-1,1]\) and \(f(x,y,z)=z\). Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}z\,ds\).

Show Solution

Setup: \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(-t\sin t+\cos t,\;t\cos t+\sin t,\;1)\).

1
Compute \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|^2=(-t\sin t+\cos t)^2+(t\cos t+\sin t)^2+1\). Expand: \(=t^2\sin^2t-2t\sin t\cos t+\cos^2t+t^2\cos^2t+2t\sin t\cos t+\sin^2t+1=t^2+1+1=t^2+2\).
2
Since \(z=t\) on \(\mathscr{C}\): \(\int_{-1}^{1}t\sqrt{2+t^2}\,dt\).
3
The integrand \(t\sqrt{2+t^2}\) is an odd function on the symmetric interval \([-1,1]\).

Answer: \(0\) (odd function on symmetric interval)

Example 4.3 — Piecewise Curve (Triangle, Ex. 203)

Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be the perimeter of the triangle in the \(xz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0)\), \((1,0)\), \((1,1)\), oriented counterclockwise. Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}(x^2+z^2)\,ds\).

Show Solution

Thought process: The curve consists of three line segments. Use different parameter symbols \(t_1,t_2,t_3\) for each to avoid confusion (the arc-length element \(ds\) depends on the segment).

\(\mathscr{C}_1\): \((0,0)\to(1,0)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t_1)=(t_1,0)\), \(t_1\in[0,1]\), \(|\mathbf{r}'|=1\).

\(\mathscr{C}_2\): \((1,0)\to(1,1)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t_2)=(1,t_2)\), \(t_2\in[0,1]\), \(|\mathbf{r}'|=1\).

\(\mathscr{C}_3\): \((1,1)\to(0,0)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t_3)=(1-t_3,1-t_3)\), \(t_3\in[0,1]\), \(|\mathbf{r}'|=\sqrt{2}\).

1
\(\int_{\mathscr{C}_1}(x^2+z^2)\,ds=\int_0^1 t_1^2\cdot 1\,dt_1=\frac{1}{3}\).
2
\(\int_{\mathscr{C}_2}(x^2+z^2)\,ds=\int_0^1(1+t_2^2)\cdot 1\,dt_2=1+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{4}{3}\).
3
\(\int_{\mathscr{C}_3}(x^2+z^2)\,ds=\int_0^1 2(1-t_3)^2\cdot\sqrt{2}\,dt_3=2\sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3}=\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}\).

Answer: \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{3}+\frac{4}{3}+\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}=\frac{5+2\sqrt{2}}{3}\)

★ Tip: Always use different parameter variables for each piece of a piecewise curve. The arc-length element \(ds\) is determined by the curve, not the parameter name.

Example 4.4 — Mass of a Wire (Ex. 204)

A thin wire has the shape \(\mathscr{C}:\; y=2x^2\), \(1\leq x\leq 2\), with mass density \(f(x,y)=y/x\) in g/cm. Find the total mass.

Show Solution

Setup: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t,2t^2)\), \(t\in[1,2]\). Then \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,4t)\), \(|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=\sqrt{1+16t^2}\).

\(f(\mathbf{r}(t))=y(t)/x(t)=2t^2/t=2t\).

1
\(M=\int_1^2 2t\sqrt{1+16t^2}\,dt\). Let \(u=1+16t^2\), \(du=32t\,dt\), so \(2t\,dt=du/16\).
2
Bounds: \(u(1)=17\), \(u(2)=65\). So \(M=\frac{1}{16}\int_{17}^{65}\sqrt{u}\,du=\frac{1}{16}\cdot\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\Big|_{17}^{65}=\frac{65\sqrt{65}-17\sqrt{17}}{24}\approx 18.9\) g.

Answer: \(\displaystyle M=\frac{65\sqrt{65}-17\sqrt{17}}{24}\approx 18.9\) g

Average Value and Center of Mass

Average Value on a Curve
\[\overline{f}=\frac{1}{L}\int_{\mathscr{C}}f\,ds\]
L — total arc length of 𝒞f̄ — average value of f on 𝒞
Center of Mass of a Wire
\[\bar{x}=\frac{1}{M}\int_{\mathscr{C}}x\,f\,ds,\quad\bar{y}=\frac{1}{M}\int_{\mathscr{C}}y\,f\,ds,\quad\bar{z}=\frac{1}{M}\int_{\mathscr{C}}z\,f\,ds\]
M = ∫𝒞 f ds — total massf(x,y,z) — mass density(x̄, ȳ, z̄) — center of mass

§4.2 — Line Integrals of Vector Fields

When integrating a vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) along a curve, we measure how much \(\mathbf{F}\) pushes along the curve. This gives the work done by the force field on a particle moving along \(\mathscr{C}\).

Definition — Line Integral of a Vector Field

Let \(\mathbf{F}:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n\) be a continuous vector field and \(\mathscr{C}\) a curve parametrized by \(\mathbf{r}(t)\), \(t\in[a,b]\). The line integral of F over 𝒞 is:

Vector Field Line Integral
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\int_a^b\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)\,dt\]
F — vector fieldr(t) — parametrization of 𝒞r′(t) — tangent vector (velocity)F·r′ — dot product (scalar!)

Key Differences from Arc-Length Integrals

PropertyScalar (∫f ds)Vector (∫F·dr)
OrientationIndependentDepends on direction: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=-\int_{-\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\)
Integrandf · |r′(t)| (magnitude)F · r′(t) (dot product)
Result signAlways ≥ 0 if f ≥ 0Can be negative
Fig 4.2 — The vector line integral ∫F·dr measures the tangential component of F along 𝒞. Only the part of F parallel to the curve does work.

Component Form

Writing \(\mathbf{F}=(P,Q,R)\) and \(d\mathbf{r}=(dx,dy,dz)\), the integral expands as:

Component Form
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\int_{\mathscr{C}}P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz=\int_a^b\!\big(Px'+Qy'+Rz'\big)\,dt\]
P, Q, R — components of Fdx = x′dt, dy = y′dt, dz = z′dt

Work and Circulation

Work Done by a Force
\[W=\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\]
W — work (scalar)F — force field

When \(\mathscr{C}\) is a closed curve (loop), the integral is called the circulation of \(\mathbf{F}\) around \(\mathscr{C}\), often written \(\oint_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) or \(\oint_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell}\).

★ Strategy for Computing ∫F·dr

Step 1: Parametrize \(\mathscr{C}\): find \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))\) and interval \([a,b]\).

Step 2: Compute \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(x',y',z')\).

Step 3: Substitute into \(\mathbf{F}\) to get \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\).

Step 4: Dot-product \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)\) — this gives a scalar function of \(t\).

Step 5: Integrate from \(a\) to \(b\).

Example 4.5 — Vector Line Integral + Orientation Reversal (Ex. 208)

Let \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z)=(x^2-1)\,\mathbf{i}+xy\,\mathbf{j}+z\,\mathbf{k}\) and \(\mathscr{C}\) be the line segment from \(O(0,0,0)\) to \((1,-1,2)\). Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) and \(\int_{-\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\).

Show Solution

Forward direction: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t,-t,2t)\), \(t\in[0,1]\), so \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,-1,2)\).

1
\(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))=(t^2-1,\,-t^2,\,2t)\). Dot with \(\mathbf{r}'(t)\): \((t^2-1)(1)+(-t^2)(-1)+(2t)(2)=2t^2+4t-1\).
2
\(\int_0^1(2t^2+4t-1)\,dt=\left[\frac{2t^3}{3}+2t^2-t\right]_0^1=\frac{2}{3}+2-1=\frac{5}{3}\).

Reversed: \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t,t-1,2-2t)\), \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(-1,1,-2)\). Compute \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}'\) and integrate over \([0,1]\) to get \(-5/3\).

Answer: \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\frac{5}{3}\), \(\int_{-\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=-\frac{5}{3}\) ✓ opposites

Example 4.6 — Three Methods for the Same Integral (Ex. 210)

Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}x\,dx+y\,dy\) where \(\mathscr{C}\) is the curve \(x=t,\,y=t^3\), \(1\leq t\leq 2\).

Show Solution

Method 1 — Direct substitution: \(\mathbf{F}=(x,y)\), \(d\mathbf{r}=(dx,dy)\), \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(t,t^3)\), \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,3t^2)\). So \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}'=(t)(1)+(t^3)(3t^2)=t+3t^5\). Integrate: \(\int_1^2(t+3t^5)\,dt=[\frac{t^2}{2}+\frac{t^6}{2}]_1^2=(2+32)-(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2})=33\).

Method 2 — Substitute differentials: \(dx=dt\), \(dy=3t^2\,dt\). Plug directly: \(\int_1^2 t\,dt+t^3(3t^2\,dt)=\int_1^2(t+3t^5)\,dt=33\).

Method 3 — Eliminate parameter: Since \(y=x^3\), \(dy=3x^2\,dx\). So \(\int_1^2(x+x^3\cdot3x^2)\,dx=\int_1^2(x+3x^5)\,dx=33\).

Answer: 33 (all three methods agree)

Example 4.7 — Piecewise 3D Path (Ex. 211)

Evaluate \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}yz\,dx-xz\,dy+xy\,dz\) where \(\mathscr{C}\) consists of three line segments: \(\mathscr{C}_1\) from \(P(0,0,0)\) to \(Q(2,0,0)\), \(\mathscr{C}_2\) from \(Q\) to \(R(1,3,-1)\), \(\mathscr{C}_3\) from \(R\) to \(S(1,3,0)\).

Show Solution

Use the segment parametrization \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t)\mathbf{A}+t\mathbf{B}\), \(t\in[0,1]\) for each piece.

1
\(\mathscr{C}_1\): \(\mathbf{r}=(2t,0,0)\), \(d\mathbf{r}=(2,0,0)\,dt\). Integrand: \(yz\cdot2=0\). So \(\int_{\mathscr{C}_1}=0\).
2
\(\mathscr{C}_2\): \(\mathbf{r}=(2-t,3t,-t)\), \(d\mathbf{r}=(-1,3,-1)\,dt\). Integrand: \((3t)(-t)(-1)-(2-t)(-t)(3)+(2-t)(3t)(-1)\). Simplify: \(3t^2+3t(2-t)-3t(2-t)=3t^2\). Integral: \(\int_0^1 3t^2\,dt=1\).
3
\(\mathscr{C}_3\): \(\mathbf{r}=(1,3,t-1)\), \(d\mathbf{r}=(0,0,1)\,dt\). Integrand: \(xy\cdot 1=3\). Integral: \(\int_0^1 3\,dt=3\).

Answer: \(0+1+3=4\)

Example 4.8 — Work by a Force Field (Ex. 212)

Find the work done by \(\mathbf{F}(x,y)=x\,\mathbf{i}+x^2y\,\mathbf{j}\) on a particle moving along \(\mathbf{r}(t)=t\,\mathbf{i}+t^2\,\mathbf{j}\), \(0\leq t\leq 1\).

Show Solution

\(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(1,2t)\). \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))=(t,\,t^2\cdot t^2)=(t,t^4)\).

\(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}'=t\cdot 1+t^4\cdot 2t=t+2t^5\).

\(W=\int_0^1(t+2t^5)\,dt=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{5}{6}\).

Answer: \(W=\frac{5}{6}\)

§4.3 — Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

This section ties together line integrals and conservative fields (recall from §3.7 that \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative if \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\) for some scalar field \(f\), equivalently \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\)).

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for Line Integrals

If \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\) (i.e., \(\mathbf{F}\) is a gradient field with potential function \(f\)) and \(\mathscr{C}\) is any piecewise smooth curve from \(\mathbf{r}(a)\) to \(\mathbf{r}(b)\), then:

FTCLI
\[\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\int_{\mathscr{C}}\nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r}=f(\mathbf{r}(b))-f(\mathbf{r}(a))\]
f — potential function (∇f = F)r(a) — start pointr(b) — end point

Just like the single-variable FTC: \(\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx=f(b)-f(a)\), but now the "derivative" is the gradient and the "interval" is a curve.

Consequences

★ Path Independence

If \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\), the integral depends only on the endpoints, not on the path taken between them. All paths from \(P\) to \(Q\) give the same answer: \(f(Q)-f(P)\).

Fig 4.3 — Path independence: when F = ∇f is conservative, all paths from P to Q yield the same integral f(Q) − f(P).
Closed-Loop Test
\[\mathbf{F}\text{ conservative and }\mathscr{C}\text{ closed}\;\Longrightarrow\;\oint_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=0\]

The converse also holds (in simply connected regions): if \(\oint\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=0\) for every closed curve, then \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative.

Testing for Conservativeness (Summary)

Equivalences in a Simply Connected Region

For a continuously differentiable \(\mathbf{F}\) defined on a simply connected region \(\mathscr{R}\) (no holes), the following are equivalent:

1. \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\) (curl-free / irrotational)

2. There exists a scalar field \(f\) such that \(\mathbf{F}=\nabla f\) (gradient field)

3. \(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) is path-independent (depends only on endpoints)

A simply connected region is one where every closed curve can be continuously shrunk to a point without leaving \(\mathscr{R}\). Disks, rectangles, and all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) are simply connected. A disk with its center removed is NOT simply connected.

Finding the Potential Function via Line Integrals

Once you verify \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative, the potential function can be found by evaluating:

Potential via Line Integral
\[f(x,y,z)=\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\]
𝒞 — any curve from a fixed P₀ to (x,y,z)The easiest choice: a line segment from the origin

The simplest path is the line segment from a convenient fixed point \(P_0\) to a general point \(P(x,y,z)\), parametrized as \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t)P_0+tP\), \(t\in[0,1]\). During integration, treat \(x,y,z\) as constants.

★ Two Methods to Find Potential Functions

Method 1 (Component integration, from §3.7): Integrate \(f_x=P\) w.r.t. \(x\), then match \(f_y=Q\) and \(f_z=R\) to determine the "constants" of integration. Best for polynomial \(\mathbf{F}\).

Method 2 (Line integral method, eq. 4.12): Choose \(P_0\), parametrize the segment from \(P_0\) to \((x,y,z)\), integrate. The answer gives \(f\) directly. Best for complicated non-polynomial fields.

Example 4.9 — Newton's Gravity & Coulomb's Law (Ex. 215)

Show that the gravitational field \(\mathbf{F}=-\frac{GmM}{|\mathbf{r}|^3}\mathbf{r}\) and the electrostatic field \(\mathbf{F}=-\frac{kqQ}{|\mathbf{r}|^3}\mathbf{r}\) are both conservative. Show that \(\mathbf{F}(x,y)=(-y,x)\) is NOT conservative.

Show Solution

Gravity: Let \(f(x,y,z)=\frac{GmM}{|\mathbf{r}|}=\frac{GmM}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}}\). Compute \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=-GmM\frac{x}{(x^2+y^2+z^2)^{3/2}}=-GmM\frac{x}{|\mathbf{r}|^3}\). By symmetry, \(\nabla f=-\frac{GmM}{|\mathbf{r}|^3}(x,y,z)=-\frac{GmM}{|\mathbf{r}|^3}\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{F}\). ✓

Coulomb: Same form with constants \(kqQ\) replacing \(GmM\). ✓

\(\mathbf{F}=(-y,x)\) is NOT conservative: Method — compute the curl. Extend to 3D: \(\mathbf{F}=(-y,x,0)\). Then \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\det\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\\partial_x&\partial_y&\partial_z\\-y&x&0\end{pmatrix}=(0,0,2)\neq\mathbf{0}\). Not conservative.

Verification via closed loop: Let \(\mathscr{C}\) be the unit circle \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(\cos t,\sin t)\), \(t\in[0,2\pi]\). Then \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}'=(-\sin t,\cos t)\cdot(-\sin t,\cos t)=\sin^2t+\cos^2t=1\). So \(\oint_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\int_0^{2\pi}1\,dt=2\pi\neq 0\). ✗

Example 4.10 — Finding a Potential via Line Integral (Ex. 219)

Find the potential function for the conservative field \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z)=(2xy,\,z+x^2,\,y)\).

Show Solution

Verify conservative: \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=(R_y-Q_z,\,P_z-R_x,\,Q_x-P_y)=(1-1,\,0-0,\,2x-2x)=\mathbf{0}\). ✓

Line integral method: Let \(P_0=(0,0,0)\) and \(\mathscr{C}\) be the segment to \(P(x,y,z)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(tx,ty,tz)\), \(t\in[0,1]\), \(\mathbf{r}'(t)=(x,y,z)\).

1
\(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))=(2t^2xy,\,tz+t^2x^2,\,ty)\).
2
\(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}'=2t^2x^2y+(tz+t^2x^2)y+tyz=3t^2x^2y+2tyz\).
3
\(f=\int_0^1(3t^2x^2y+2tyz)\,dt=x^2y\cdot t^3\Big|_0^1+yz\cdot t^2\Big|_0^1=x^2y+yz\).

Answer: \(f(x,y,z)=x^2y+yz+C\)

Check: \(\nabla f=(2xy,\,x^2+z,\,y)=\mathbf{F}\). ✓

Example 4.11 — Evaluating via FTCLI (Ex. 220)

Let \(f(1,-1,2)=2\) and \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z)=(2y+z,\,2x,\,x)\). Find \(f(2,3,-1)\).

Show Solution

Verify conservative: \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=(0-0,\,1-1,\,2-2)=\mathbf{0}\). ✓

Use the line segment from \(P_0=(1,0,-1)\) to \(P=(x,y,z)\): \(\mathbf{r}(t)=(1-t)(1,0,-1)+t(x,y,z)\), \(t\in[0,1]\).

After expanding and integrating (treating \(x,y,z\) as constants):

\(f(x,y,z)=2xy+xz+1\). Since \(f(1,-1,2)=2(-1)+2+1=-2+2+1=1\neq 2\), we need \(C=1\): \(f=2xy+xz+1+C\) with \(C=1\), so \(f=2xy+xz+2\).

\(f(2,3,-1)=2(2)(3)+(2)(-1)+2=12-2+2=12\).

Answer: \(f(2,3,-1)=12\)

Work-Energy Theorem

Kinetic Energy and Line Integrals

If a mass \(m\) moves along \(\mathscr{C}\) under force \(\mathbf{F}\), Newton's second law gives:

\(\int_{\mathscr{C}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}(b)|^2-\frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}(a)|^2\) — the work equals the change in kinetic energy.

If \(\mathbf{F}\) is also conservative with potential \(f\), then \(K(P)-f(P)=C\) — conservation of energy.
