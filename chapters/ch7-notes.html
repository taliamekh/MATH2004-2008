Chapter 7

Maxima, Minima & Lagrange Multipliers

Finding and classifying critical points of multivariable functions. The Second Derivative Test for surfaces. Constrained optimization via Lagrange multipliers — the elegant technique for optimizing one function subject to another equation.

§7.2 — Maxima and Minima

In single-variable calculus you found max/min by setting f'(x) = 0, then used the second derivative to classify. We now extend this idea to functions of two (or more) variables: z = f(x, y). The core question is the same — where are the peaks and valleys of a surface? — but we now have two partial derivatives to set to zero simultaneously, and the classification test is more involved.

Critical Points

Recall from §3.5 that the gradient ∇f = (fx, fy) points in the direction of steepest increase. At a maximum or minimum, there is no direction of increase or decrease — the surface is momentarily "flat." This means the gradient must be the zero vector.

Critical Point

A point P(a, b) in the domain of f(x, y) is a critical point (or stationary point) if both partial derivatives vanish at P:

\[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a, b) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(a, b) = 0\]
fx = partial w.r.t. xfy = partial w.r.t. y(a,b) = coordinates of the critical point

Equivalently: ∇f(a, b) = 0. Every local maximum and every local minimum of a differentiable function occurs at a critical point. However, not every critical point is a max or min — it could be a saddle point.

The reasoning is clean: if P is a local max, then along any line through P the function has a single-variable max. In particular, moving along the x-direction, f(x, b) has a max at x = a, forcing fx(a, b) = 0. The same argument along the y-direction gives fy(a, b) = 0.

★ Step-by-Step Strategy for Finding Critical Points

Step 1: Compute both partial derivatives fx and fy.

Step 2: Set fx = 0 and fy = 0 simultaneously — you need BOTH to be zero at the same point.

Step 3: Factor. Look for common factors. For example, fx = y(2x + y − 1) = 0 gives two branches: y = 0 OR 2x + y − 1 = 0.

Step 4: Case analysis. For each branch from Step 3, substitute into the OTHER equation and solve. Track every solution pair (x, y). This is where most students lose points — they check one case and forget the others.

Step 5: List ALL critical points found from ALL cases. Verify each satisfies BOTH fx = 0 and fy = 0.

Step 6: Classify each one using the Second Derivative Test (compute D at each point).

The Second Derivative Test

Once you have the critical points, you need to classify each one: is it a local max, local min, or saddle point? In single-variable calculus, you check the sign of f''(x). For two variables, we need to consider not just fxx and fyy, but also the mixed partial fxy, because the surface can curve differently in different directions.

The key quantity is the discriminant D, built from the second-order partial derivatives evaluated at the critical point:

Discriminant (Hessian Determinant)
\[D = f_{xx} \cdot f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 \quad \text{at } P(a,b)\]
fxx = ∂²f/∂x²fyy = ∂²f/∂y²fxy = ∂²f/∂x∂y (mixed partial)

This is the determinant of the Hessian matrix \(H = \begin{pmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy} \\ f_{xy} & f_{yy}\end{pmatrix}\). The discriminant tells you whether the surface curves the same way in all directions (D > 0) or curves up in one direction and down in another (D < 0).

Second Derivative Test (for two variables)

Let P(a, b) be a critical point of f (so fx = fy = 0 at P). Compute D = fxx · fyy − (fxy)² at P. Then:

\[\begin{cases} D > 0 \text{ and } f_{xx} > 0 &\Rightarrow \text{local minimum at } P \\ D > 0 \text{ and } f_{xx} < 0 &\Rightarrow \text{local maximum at } P \\ D < 0 &\Rightarrow \text{saddle point at } P \\ D = 0 &\Rightarrow \text{inconclusive (need other methods)}\end{cases}\]
D = fxx fyy − f_xy² (discriminant)fxx determines concavity when D > 0
★ Why fxx and not fyy?

When D > 0, the signs of fxx and fyy must agree (since D = fxx · fyy − f_xy² > 0 forces fxx and fyy to have the same sign). So checking fxx alone suffices — you could equally check fyy and get the same answer.

Saddle Point

Think of a mountain pass — the road between two peaks. Standing at the pass and looking left-right along the road, you're at a valley (the road goes up on both sides). But looking forward-backward toward the peaks, you're at a hilltop (the mountains drop away on both sides). So are you at a min or a max? Neither. You're at a saddle point.

Mathematically: cut the surface through P along the x-axis → you see a valley (curves up). Cut along the y-axis → you see a hilltop (curves down). The surface has no consistent curvature at P — it goes up one way and down the other. The discriminant D < 0 detects exactly this "disagreement."

xx > 0 Local Maximum P goes down ↙ ↘ goes down D > 0 & fxx < 0 Saddle Point P x-slice: valley ↑ y-slice: hilltop ↓ D < 0
Fig 7.1 — At a minimum, every slice through P curves up. At a maximum, every slice curves down. At a saddle point, the x-slice is a valley (solid) but the y-slice is a hilltop (dashed) — P is neither max nor min.

Worked Examples — Critical Points & Second Derivative Test

Example 7.1 — Relative Extrema of a Quadratic Surface

Problem: Find the relative maxima and minima of \(z = 2x + 4y - x^2 - y^2 - 3\).

Show full solution

Thought process: This is a quadratic function in x and y — the highest powers are x² and y², both with negative coefficients. Think of it like an upside-down bowl. That means there's one hilltop (max) and no valley (min). We need to find where the hilltop is by solving ∇f = 0, then confirm it's a max using the Second Derivative Test.

What we need to find: The (x, y) location of any critical points, then classify each as local max, min, or saddle.

Step 1 — Compute partial derivatives and set to zero:

\[f_x = 2 - 2x = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = 1\]
\[f_y = 4 - 2y = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad y = 2\]

Each equation has exactly one solution, so the only critical point is P(1, 2).

Step 2 — Compute all three second partial derivatives:

\[f_{xx} = -2, \quad f_{yy} = -2, \quad f_{xy} = 0\]

These are all constants (no x or y), so they're the same everywhere — we only have one CP to check anyway.

Step 3 — Compute the discriminant D:

\[D = f_{xx} \cdot f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 = (-2)(-2) - 0^2 = 4 > 0\]

Step 4 — Classify: D = 4 > 0 ✓ and fxx = −2 < 0, so by the SDT this is a local maximum.

Step 5 — Find the maximum value:

\[f(1, 2) = 2(1) + 4(2) - 1^2 - 2^2 - 3 = 2 + 8 - 1 - 4 - 3 = \boxed{2}\]

Is this a global max? Yes — since f → −∞ as \(\|(x,y)\| \to \infty\) (the −x² − y² terms dominate), this local max is also the global maximum. There is no global minimum.

★ Tips

When both x² and y² have negative coefficients, expect a single hilltop (max). When both are positive, expect a single valley (min). When they have opposite signs (like x² − y²), expect a saddle.

Concept bridge: Uses formula sheet #45 (Critical Point Condition) and #46 (Second Derivative Test).

Example 7.2 — Multiple Critical Points with Saddles

Problem: Classify the critical points of \(f(x, y) = x^2 y + x y^2 - xy\) on ℝ².

Show full solution

Thought process: This function has degree-3 terms (x²y, xy²), so it can have multiple critical points — unlike quadratics which typically have just one. The key technique is factoring each partial derivative equation, then doing systematic case analysis on each factor.

What we need to find: ALL critical points (there may be several), then classify each one individually using the SDT.

Step 1 — Compute partials and factor:

\[f_x = 2xy + y^2 - y = y(2x + y - 1) = 0\]
\[f_y = x^2 + 2xy - x = x(x + 2y - 1) = 0\]

Each equation factors into two parts. For fx = 0: either y = 0 or 2x + y − 1 = 0. For fy = 0: either x = 0 or x + 2y − 1 = 0.

Step 2 — Case analysis (check every combination):

Case 1: y = 0 and x = 0. Both equations satisfied → CP at (0, 0). ✓

Case 2: y = 0 and x + 2(0) − 1 = 0. Gives x = 1 → CP at (1, 0). ✓

Case 3: 2x + y − 1 = 0 and x = 0. Gives y = 1 → CP at (0, 1). ✓

Case 4: 2x + y − 1 = 0 and x + 2y − 1 = 0. Solve the 2×2 system: subtract → x − y = 0 → x = y. Substitute: 3x = 1 → x = y = 1/3. CP at (1/3, 1/3). ✓

Total: 4 critical points found.

Step 3 — Second partial derivatives:

\[f_{xx} = 2y, \quad f_{yy} = 2x, \quad f_{xy} = 2x + 2y - 1\]

Step 4 — Classify each CP (compute D at each point):

P(a,b)fxxfyyfxyD = fxxfyy − fxy²Classification
(0, 0)00−10·0 − (−1)² = −1Saddle (D < 0)
(1, 0)0210·2 − 1² = −1Saddle (D < 0)
(0, 1)2012·0 − 1² = −1Saddle (D < 0)

Step 5 — Find the minimum value:

\[f\!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3}\right) = \tfrac{1}{9} \cdot \tfrac{1}{3} + \tfrac{1}{3} \cdot \tfrac{1}{9} - \tfrac{1}{9} = \tfrac{1}{27} + \tfrac{1}{27} - \tfrac{3}{27} = \boxed{-\tfrac{1}{27}}\]

Is this a global min? No — for example, f(−1, −1) = −1 − 1 + 1 = −1 < −1/27. On ℝ² with no boundary, there's no guaranteed global min.

★ Tips

When factoring gives multiple branches, set up a systematic grid: list all combinations of "factor 1 = 0" and "factor 2 = 0" from each equation. Students lose points by checking only the obvious cases and missing the "both non-zero factors" case.

Extrema on Closed Regions

When f is continuous on a closed, bounded region 𝒟 (including its boundary), the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees a global max and min. Finding them requires checking both interior and boundary.

Local vs. Global Extrema

A local maximum at P means f(P) ≥ f(Q) for all Q near P. A global (absolute) maximum means f(P) ≥ f(Q) for all Q in the domain. The global max is the largest of all local maxima. Similarly for minima. On a closed bounded domain, both global max and global min are guaranteed to exist.

Fig 7.2 — Local vs. global extrema on a closed domain [a, b]. The global min is the lowest of all local minima and boundary values. The global max is the highest. On a closed bounded region, both are guaranteed to exist (Extreme Value Theorem). Double-ring markers ◎ denote the global extrema.
Closed-Region Extrema Algorithm

1. Find and classify all critical points inside 𝒟 (not on the boundary) using the SDT.

2. Find critical points of f on the boundary — parametrize each boundary curve and reduce to single-variable optimization.

3. Evaluate f at all critical points (interior + boundary) and at all corners/endpoints.

4. The largest value = absolute maximum; smallest = absolute minimum.

★ Always Check Corners/Endpoints

Derivatives don't exist at corners of a polygonal boundary. These points must be checked by direct evaluation. On a rectangle: 4 corners. On a triangle: 3. On a disk: the boundary parametrization handles everything (no corners).

Fig 7.3 — Absolute extrema on a closed rectangle: check interior CPs (from fx = fy = 0), boundary CPs (single-variable calculus on each edge), and all 4 corners (direct evaluation).
Example 7.3 — Absolute Extrema on a Closed Rectangle

Problem: Find all local and absolute extrema of \(f(x,y) = x^2 + xy + y^2\) on \(\mathscr{D} = \{-2 \le x \le 2,\; -1 \le y \le 1\}\).

Show full solution

Thought process: Closed rectangle → Extreme Value Theorem guarantees absolute max/min exist. We must check three places: interior CPs (SDT), boundary CPs (single-variable calculus on each edge), and corners (direct evaluation). The global answer is whichever of these gives the largest/smallest f-value.

What we need to find: The absolute max and min of f over the entire rectangle including boundary.

Step 1 — Interior critical points:

\[f_x = 2x + y = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad f_y = x + 2y = 0\]

From the first: y = −2x. Substitute into the second: x + 2(−2x) = −3x = 0, so x = 0 and y = 0.

Classify (0, 0): D = fxx·fyy − fxy² = (2)(2) − (1)² = 3 > 0 and fxx = 2 > 0 → local minimum. f(0, 0) = 0.

Step 2 — Boundary (each edge becomes a 1-variable problem):

𝒞₁ (y = −1): h(x) = x² − x + 1. h'(x) = 2x − 1 = 0 → x = 1/2. h(1/2) = 3/4. Ends: h(−2) = 7, h(2) = 3.

𝒞₂ (x = 2): h(y) = y² + 2y + 4. h'(y) = 2y + 2 = 0 → y = −1 (corner). End: h(1) = 7.

𝒞₃ (y = 1): h(x) = x² + x + 1. h'(x) = 2x + 1 = 0 → x = −1/2. h(−1/2) = 3/4. Ends: h(−2) = 3, h(2) = 7.

𝒞₄ (x = −2): h(y) = y² − 2y + 4. h'(y) = 2y − 2 = 0 → y = 1 (corner). End: h(−1) = 7.

Step 3 — Compare ALL values:

Corners often give the global max/min on rectangles. Each edge reduces to a 1-variable problem: fix one coordinate, differentiate, find CPs, check edge endpoints.

§7.3 — Lagrange Multipliers

What if we need to optimize f(x, y) but the variables must satisfy a constraint g(x, y) = 0? The method of Lagrange multipliers handles this without substitution.

The Geometric Idea — Why Lagrange Works

Imagine you're hiking on a mountain (the surface z = f(x, y)) but you're constrained to walk along a specific trail (the curve g(x, y) = 0). Where on this trail is the highest point? It's NOT necessarily where the mountain's own peak is — it's the highest point reachable from the trail.

Here's the key insight: as you walk along the constraint curve, the function f changes. At the constrained maximum, f has stopped increasing along the trail. This means the gradient ∇f (which points in the direction of steepest increase of f) must be perpendicular to the trail at that point — if ∇f had any component along the trail, you could still increase f by walking further.

But wait — ∇g is ALSO perpendicular to the constraint curve g = 0 (recall from §3.5 that the gradient is always perpendicular to level curves). Since both ∇f and ∇g point perpendicular to the same curve at the same point, they must be parallel:

\[\nabla f(P) = \lambda \, \nabla g(P)\]

where λ is a scalar called the Lagrange multiplier. We don't care about the value of λ itself — it's just a ratio telling us how much bigger (or smaller) ∇f is compared to ∇g.

★ The "Kissing Curves" Picture

Think of the level curves of f (f = 1, f = 2, f = 3, ...) as concentric rings. The constraint g = 0 is another curve cutting through them. As you "inflate" the level curves (increasing c), at some point the level curve f = c will just touch ("kiss") the constraint curve — tangent to it, not crossing it. At that tangent point, the two curves share the same normal direction, meaning ∇f ∥ ∇g. That's your constrained optimum!

Fig 7.4 — At a constrained extremum P, ∇f (dashed) is parallel to ∇g (solid). Both point perpendicular to the constraint curve g = 0. The dashed ellipses are level curves of f — the optimum is where a level curve "kisses" the constraint.
Lagrange Multiplier Method — Step by Step

Given: Optimize f(x, y) subject to the constraint g(x, y) = 0.

Step 1: Write the Lagrangian: \(\ell(x, y, \lambda) = f(x, y) - \lambda \, g(x, y)\)

Step 2: Take partial derivatives and set each to zero:

\[\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial x} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; f_x = \lambda \, g_x \qquad \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial y} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; f_y = \lambda \, g_y \qquad \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \lambda} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; g(x,y) = 0\]

Step 3: Solve this system of 3 equations in 3 unknowns (x, y, λ). Use case analysis: try λ = 0 vs λ ≠ 0, or eliminate λ by dividing equations.

Step 4: Evaluate f at every solution (x, y). The largest value = constrained max; smallest = constrained min.

∂ℓ/∂λ = 0 recovers the constraintλ is a helper variable — its value usually doesn't matter

For 3 variables: ℓ(x,y,z,λ) = f − λg. Set ∂ℓ/∂x = ∂ℓ/∂y = ∂ℓ/∂z = ∂ℓ/∂λ = 0 (4 equations, 4 unknowns).

★ Lagrange Does NOT Classify

Lagrange finds candidates. To classify, evaluate f at all candidates and compare. The largest value is the constrained max and the smallest is the constrained min. On a closed constraint (like a circle), both must exist.

★ Case-by-Case Analysis

Lagrange systems involve products. When a factor can be zero, consider all possibilities: if y = 0, what does the constraint give? If y ≠ 0, divide and continue. Missing a case = missing critical points.

Worked Examples — Lagrange Multipliers

Example 7.5 — Largest Rectangle in a Semicircle

Problem: Find the area of the largest rectangle inscribed in a semicircle of radius a.

Show full solution

Thought process: This is a constrained optimization problem — we want to maximize area (the objective) but the rectangle's corner must stay on the semicircle (the constraint). The constraint is nonlinear (x² + y² = a²), making Lagrange multipliers the natural tool.

What we need to find: The maximum value of the rectangle's area A = 2xy, where (x, y) is constrained to lie on x² + y² = a².

Step 1 — Set up the Lagrangian:

Objective: f(x, y) = 2xy. Constraint: g(x, y) = x² + y² − a² = 0.

\[\ell(x, y, \lambda) = 2xy - \lambda(x^2 + y^2 - a^2)\]

Step 2 — Partial derivatives = 0:

\[\ell_x = 2y - 2\lambda x = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; y = \lambda x \quad \text{[1]}\]
\[\ell_y = 2x - 2\lambda y = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \lambda y \quad \text{[2]}\]
\[\ell_\lambda = -(x^2 + y^2 - a^2) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; x^2 + y^2 = a^2 \quad \text{[3]}\]

Step 3 — Solve the system:

From [1]: y = λx. Substitute into [2]: x = λ(λx) = λ²x. Since x ≠ 0 (otherwise area = 0): λ² = 1, so λ = ±1.

For maximum area we need x > 0 and y > 0, so from [1]: y = x (taking λ = 1).

Substitute y = x into [3]: 2x² = a² → x = a/√2, and y = a/√2.

Step 4 — Compute the answer:

\[A = 2xy = 2 \cdot \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}} = 2 \cdot \frac{a^2}{2} = \boxed{a^2}\]

The largest inscribed rectangle is a square whose side equals the circle's radius!

★ Tips

When Lagrange gives λ² = 1 (i.e. λ = ±1), the two solutions often correspond to max and min of the objective on the constraint curve. Pick the one matching your optimization goal (here: positive area).

Concept bridge: Uses #48 (Lagrange Method). The constraint x² + y² = a² is a circle — a classic Lagrange setup.

Example 7.6 — Distance from Origin to a Rotated Ellipse

Problem: Find the shortest and longest distance from the origin to \(x^2 + xy + y^2 = 16\).

Show full solution

Thought process: We want to optimize distance = √(x² + y²), but since √ is monotone, we can equivalently optimize f = x² + y² (distance squared — avoids the square root). The constraint is the curve x² + xy + y² = 16. This curve is a rotated ellipse (the xy cross-term rotates it by −π/4; recall from §2.8 that B² − 4AC = 1 − 4 = −3 < 0 → ellipse).

What we need to find: Max and min of f = x² + y² subject to g = x² + xy + y² − 16 = 0.

Step 1 — Lagrangian:

\[\ell = x^2 + y^2 - \lambda(x^2 + xy + y^2 - 16)\]

Step 2 — Set partials to zero:

\[\ell_x = 2x - \lambda(2x + y) = 0 \quad \text{[1]} \qquad \ell_y = 2y - \lambda(x + 2y) = 0 \quad \text{[2]}\]

Step 3 — Case analysis:

Case λ = 0: [1] gives x = 0, [2] gives y = 0. But (0,0) doesn't satisfy g = 0. ✗

Case λ ≠ 0: From [1]: λ = 2x/(2x + y). Substitute into [2] and simplify (multiply out, cancel terms): y² − x² = 0, so y = ±x.

Sub-case y = x: Constraint: x² + x² + x² = 3x² = 16 → x = ±4/√3. Distance² = x² + x² = 32/3.

Sub-case y = −x: Constraint: x² − x² + x² = x² = 16 → x = ±4. Distance² = x² + x² = 32.

Step 4 — Compare:

Pointsx² + y²Distance
(±4/√3, ±4/√3)32/3 ≈ 10.67√(32/3) ≈ 3.27 — SHORTEST
(4, −4), (−4, 4)32√32 ≈ 5.66 — LONGEST
\[\boxed{\text{Shortest} = \tfrac{4\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{3}}} \qquad \boxed{\text{Longest} = 4\sqrt{2}}\]
★ Tips

Always optimize distance² instead of distance — it removes the square root and doesn't change where the max/min occur. Also: when you get y = ±x from a symmetric problem, check both cases against the constraint.

Concept bridge: Uses #48 (Lagrange). Recognizing the rotated ellipse uses the conic discriminant from §2.8.

Example 7.7 — Lagrange in Three Variables

Problem: Maximize \(f = xy^2z^3\) subject to \(x+y+z = 12\) with x, y, z > 0.

Show full solution

Thought process: Three variables, one linear constraint → Lagrange with 4 equations in 4 unknowns (x, y, z, λ). The trick is to eliminate λ by setting the three "fvar = λ · gvar" equations equal to each other, since gx = gy = gz = 1 (the constraint is linear).

What we need to find: Values of x, y, z > 0 with x + y + z = 12 that make xy²z³ as large as possible.

Step 1 — Lagrangian: ℓ = xy²z³ − λ(x + y + z − 12).

Step 2 — Set partials to zero:

\[\ell_x = y^2z^3 = \lambda \qquad \ell_y = 2xyz^3 = \lambda \qquad \ell_z = 3xy^2z^2 = \lambda\]

Step 3 — Eliminate λ (all three equal λ, so set them equal to each other):

y²z³ = 2xyz³: divide both sides by yz³ (all positive) → y = 2x.

y²z³ = 3xy²z²: divide both sides by xy²z² → z = 3x.

Step 4 — Substitute into constraint:

\[x + 2x + 3x = 6x = 12 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 2,\; y = 4,\; z = 6\]

Step 5 — Compute answer:

\[f(2, 4, 6) = 2 \cdot 16 \cdot 216 = \boxed{6912}\]
★ Tips

Exponent pattern: The exponents in xy²z³ are (1, 2, 3) and the split of 12 is proportional: x gets 1/6 of 12 = 2, y gets 2/6 = 4, z gets 3/6 = 6. This always works for products with a linear sum constraint.

Concept bridge: Uses #48 (Lagrange). Compare with Ex 7.9 which solves the same problem by substitution.

Example 7.8 — Interior + Lagrange Boundary (Disk)

Problem: Find the absolute max/min of \(f = 3x^2+2y^2-2x-1\) on the disk \(x^2+y^2 \le 9\).

Show full solution

Thought process: The disk x² + y² ≤ 9 is a closed bounded region. The interior is the open disk x² + y² < 9, where we use ordinary critical points + SDT. The boundary is the circle x² + y² = 9, where we use Lagrange multipliers (since the boundary is a curve defined by an equation, not flat edges).

What we need to find: Absolute max and min, checking both interior and boundary.

Step 1 — Interior critical points:

\[f_x = 6x - 2 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \tfrac{1}{3} \qquad f_y = 4y = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 0\]

Is (1/3, 0) inside the disk? (1/3)² + 0² = 1/9 < 9 ✓. Value: f(1/3, 0) = 3/9 − 2/3 − 1 = 1/3 − 2/3 − 1 = −4/3.

Step 2 — Boundary via Lagrange (g = x² + y² − 9 = 0):

\[\ell_x = 6x - 2 - 2\lambda x = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; x(3 - \lambda) = 1 \quad \text{[1]}\]
\[\ell_y = 4y - 2\lambda y = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; y(2 - \lambda) = 0 \quad \text{[2]}\]

Case 1 (y = 0): From [3]: x² = 9 → x = ±3. Values: f(3, 0) = 27 − 6 − 1 = 20. f(−3, 0) = 27 + 6 − 1 = 32.

Example 7.9 — Substitution Method (Compare with Lagrange)

Problem: Maximize xy²z³ with x + y + z = 12 and x, y, z > 0 using substitution instead of Lagrange.

Show full solution

Thought process: Same problem as Example 7.7, but solved by eliminating a variable instead of using Lagrange. From the constraint x = 12 − y − z, substitute into the objective to get a 2-variable unconstrained problem, then use ordinary critical points.

What we need to find: Same as Ex 7.7 — max of xy²z³ with x + y + z = 12.

Step 1 — Substitute x = 12 − y − z:

\[g(y, z) = (12 - y - z)\,y^2 z^3\]

Step 2 — Find critical points of g:

\[g_y = yz^3(24 - 3y - 2z) = 0 \quad \xrightarrow{y,z > 0} \quad 24 - 3y - 2z = 0\]
\[g_z = y^2z^2(36 - 3y - 4z) = 0 \quad \xrightarrow{y,z > 0} \quad 36 - 3y - 4z = 0\]

Step 3 — Solve the 2×2 system:

Subtract first from second: (36 − 3y − 4z) − (24 − 3y − 2z) = 12 − 2z = 0 → z = 6.

Back-substitute: 24 − 3y − 12 = 0 → y = 4. Then x = 12 − 4 − 6 = 2.

Step 4 — Verify it's a maximum (via SDT on g):

The discriminant D at (y, z) = (4, 6) is 2985984 > 0 and gyy = −2592 < 0 → local maximum of g. ✓

\[\text{Max} = f(2, 4, 6) = 2 \cdot 16 \cdot 216 = \boxed{6912}\]

Same answer as Lagrange (Example 7.7)!

★ Substitution vs. Lagrange

Substitution works best when the constraint is linear (easy to isolate one variable). Lagrange is cleaner for nonlinear constraints (circles, spheres, ellipses). Both give the same answer — choose whichever creates less algebraic mess for the problem at hand.

★ Substitution vs. Lagrange — When to Use Which

Substitution is simplest when the constraint is linear (easy to isolate one variable). Lagrange is symmetric and cleaner for nonlinear constraints (circles, ellipses, spheres). Both give the same answer — choose based on which creates less algebraic mess.
