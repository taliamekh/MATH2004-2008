Ch 1 — Vectors ✓

Builds the vector toolkit in ℝ² and ℝ³ — definitions, operations, and geometric applications that underpin all of multivariable calculus.

Vector Basics
What:
Vectors defined by magnitude and direction, represented as ordered components in ℝ² or ℝ³.
Why:
The fundamental objects everything else builds on — displacements, forces, velocities.
How:
\(\vec{v}=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle\), \(|\vec{v}|=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2}\)
Trap:
Equality means same magnitude AND direction — position in space doesn't matter.
Vector Arithmetic
What:
Addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication — all component-wise.
Why:
Combines forces, displacements, and velocities algebraically; parallelogram law gives geometric intuition.
How:
\(\vec{v}+\vec{w}=\langle v_1+w_1,\,v_2+w_2,\,v_3+w_3\rangle\), \(c\vec{v}=\langle cv_1,cv_2,cv_3\rangle\)
Unit Vectors & Direction
What:
Normalization to length 1; standard basis î, ĵ, k̂; direction cosines and inclination angles.
Why:
Separates direction from magnitude — essential for projections and coordinate decomposition.
How:
\(\hat{v}=\vec{v}/|\vec{v}|\), direction cosines satisfy \(\cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1\)
Trap:
The zero vector has no unit vector — can't normalize it.
Dot Product
What:
Scalar product measuring how aligned two vectors are.
Why:
Unlocks angles between vectors, orthogonality tests, and projections.
How:
\(\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}=v_1w_1+v_2w_2+v_3w_3=|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\cos\theta\), angle: \(\theta=\arccos\!\bigl(\frac{\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}}{|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|}\bigr)\)
Trap:
Result is a SCALAR, not a vector. Dot = 0 means perpendicular, not that a vector is zero.
Projections & Work
What:
Scalar and vector projection of one vector onto another; work as force dotted with displacement.
Why:
Decomposes vectors into parallel/perpendicular parts — central to physics and later integration.
How:
Scalar: \(\text{comp}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}=\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}\), Vector: \(\text{proj}_{\vec{v}}\vec{u}=\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|^2}\vec{v}\), Work: \(W=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\)
Cross Product
What:
A vector perpendicular to both inputs whose magnitude equals the parallelogram area.
Why:
Produces normal vectors to planes, computes torque, and measures area.
How:
\(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}\) via 3×3 determinant with î, ĵ, k̂ in the first row. Magnitude: \(|\vec{v}\times\vec{w}|=|\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\sin\theta\)
Trap:
NOT commutative — \(\vec{v}\times\vec{w}=-(\vec{w}\times\vec{v})\). Use the right-hand rule for direction.
Geometric Applications
What:
Parallelogram area, triangle area, and parallelepiped volume from cross and triple products.
Why:
Turns abstract vector algebra into measurable geometry — areas, volumes, coplanarity.
How:
Area△ = \(\tfrac{1}{2}|\vec{u}\times\vec{v}|\), Volume = \(|\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})|\)
Trap:
Triple product = 0 means the vectors are coplanar, not that there is "no volume."
Connections → Dot product → scalar projections → work integrals (Ch 6) · Cross product → normal vectors → planes (Ch 2) → surface integrals (Ch 7) · Magnitude & distance → arc length & curvature (Ch 3)

Ch 2 — Lines, Planes & Parametric Curves ✓

Extends vectors to describe lines, planes, and curves in space. Introduces rotations, conic sections, parametric and polar representations with their full calculus toolkit.

Lines in Space
What:
A line is defined by a point P and a direction vector v⃗. A line segment restricts the parameter to [0, 1]. Two lines in 3D can be parallel (same direction), intersecting (meet at a point), or skew (neither parallel nor meeting — only in 3D).
Why:
Lines are the simplest curves and the foundation for parametric thinking — every more complex curve builds on this "point + direction" idea.
How:
Line: \(\vec{r}(t)=P+t\vec{v}\). Segment: \(\vec{r}(t)=(1-t)P+tQ\), \(0\le t\le 1\). Midpoint at \(t=\tfrac{1}{2}\). Parallel test: \(\vec{v}_1\times\vec{v}_2=\vec{0}\). Skew test: not parallel AND z-values disagree after solving x, y.
Trap:
Skew ≠ "don't intersect." Non-parallel lines that don't meet are skew. Parallel lines that don't meet are just parallel. Always check BOTH conditions.
Planes
What:
A plane is a flat surface defined by a point and a normal vector (perpendicular arrow). Three non-collinear points determine a unique plane. Two planes can be parallel (same normal direction) or intersect in a line.
Why:
Planes describe surfaces in 3D. Their normals are essential for tangent planes (Ch 3), surface integrals (Ch 6), and flux calculations.
How:
Equation: \(ax+by+cz=d\), normal \(\vec{n}=(a,b,c)\). From 3 points: \(\vec{n}=\overrightarrow{PQ}\times\overrightarrow{PR}\). Angle between planes: \(\cos\theta=\frac{\vec{n}_1\cdot\vec{n}_2}{|\vec{n}_1||\vec{n}_2|}\). Two-plane intersection: direction = \(\vec{n}_1\times\vec{n}_2\), find one point by setting a variable to 0.
Trap:
Lines use direction vectors (parallel to the line). Planes use normal vectors (perpendicular to the plane). Don't mix these up — they're fundamentally different.
Rotations & Translations
What:
Active rotation moves a point by angle θ (matrix A). Passive rotation rotates the axes instead (matrix A⁻¹ = Aᵀ). Translation shifts the origin to (h, k) by substituting (x−h, y−k). Combined: translate first, then rotate.
Why:
Used to eliminate cross-terms (Bxy) in conic equations, aligning a rotated conic with the coordinate axes for easier analysis.
How:
Active: \(A=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\). Passive: flip sin signs. Translation: \((x',y')=(x-h,\,y-k)\). Combined: translate → rotate.
Trap:
Active vs passive differ only by the sign on sinθ — using the wrong one rotates in the opposite direction.
Conic Sections
What:
The three classical conics — parabola, ellipse, hyperbola — classified by the discriminant of the general second-degree equation \(Ax^2+Bxy+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0\).
Why:
Conics appear everywhere: planetary orbits (ellipses), projectile paths (parabolas), satellite dishes (paraboloids). The discriminant gives instant classification without completing the square.
How:
Discriminant \(B^2-4AC\): negative → ellipse, zero → parabola, positive → hyperbola. Parametrizations: ellipse \((a\cos t,\,b\sin t)\), circle \((r\cos t,\,r\sin t)\).
Trap:
Degenerate cases exist: the "ellipse" x²+y²=−1 is empty, and B²−4AC=0 could give two parallel lines instead of a parabola.
Parametric Calculus
What:
Derivatives, tangent classification, area, and arc length for curves written as x(t), y(t). The chain rule adapts all standard calculus tools to parametric form.
Why:
Many curves (cycloids, epicycloids, Lissajous figures) can't be written as y=f(x). Parametric form handles self-intersections, loops, and multi-valued curves naturally.
How:
Slope: \(dy/dx=(dy/dt)/(dx/dt)\). Second deriv: \(d^2y/dx^2=\frac{d/dt(dy/dx)}{dx/dt}\). H-tangent: dy/dt=0. V-tangent: dx/dt=0. Area: \(\int y\,x'\,dt\). Arc length: \(\int\sqrt{(x')^2+(y')^2}\,dt\).
Trap:
The second derivative is NOT (d²y/dt²)/(d²x/dt²) — this is the #1 exam mistake. You must differentiate dy/dx with respect to t, then divide by dx/dt again.
Key Parametric Results
What:
Named results for classic curves that appear repeatedly on exams and in later chapters.
How:
Cycloid \(x=r(t-\sin t),\,y=r(1-\cos t)\): arch area = \(3\pi r^2\), arch length = \(8r\). Ellipse \(x=a\cos t,\,y=b\sin t\): area = \(\pi ab\). Key identity: \(1-\cos t=2\sin^2(t/2)\).
Trap:
Cycloid area = 3× the rolling circle (not 2×, not π×). The identity 1−cosθ = 2sin²(θ/2) is essential for simplifying cycloid integrals — memorize it.
Polar Coordinates
What:
Points described by distance r and angle θ from the origin. Common curves: cardioids \(r=1+\sin\theta\), limaçons \(r=a+b\cos\theta\), roses \(r=\cos(n\theta)\) with n petals (or 2n if n is even).
Why:
Curves with circular symmetry (spirals, petals, hearts) are simple in polar but nightmarish in Cartesian. Polar form also simplifies double integrals over circular regions (Ch 5).
How:
Conversion: \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta,\;r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\). Convention: \((-r,\theta)=(r,\theta+\pi)\). Quadrant check required for θ = arctan(y/x).
Trap:
arctan(y/x) only returns (−π/2, π/2). A point in Q3 like (−1,−1) gives arctan(1)=π/4, but the correct angle is 5π/4. Always check the quadrant.
Polar Calculus
What:
Slope, area, and arc length formulas adapted for polar curves r = f(θ). Area uses sectors (pie slices), not rectangles.
Why:
Computing area/length of polar curves directly — without converting to Cartesian — is usually much simpler.
How:
Slope: \(\frac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}\). Area: \(\frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta\). Between curves: \(\frac{1}{2}\int(r_{\text{out}}^2-r_{\text{in}}^2)\,d\theta\). Arc length: \(\int\sqrt{(r')^2+r^2}\,d\theta\). Cardioid perimeter = 8.
Trap:
For area between polar curves, find ALL intersections first — some only show up as r=0 at different θ values (both curves pass through the origin but at different angles).

Ch 3 — Partial Derivatives & Vector Fields (§3.1–3.7)

Extends calculus to functions of several variables, introduces the gradient as the unifying concept, and defines the three fundamental vector field operators.

Limits & Continuity (Multivariable)
What:
Limits of f(x,y) as (x,y) → (x₀,y₀). Continuity requires the limit to exist and equal the function value. Unlike 1D, approach can come from infinitely many directions.
Why:
Foundation for all multivariable calculus. If a function isn't continuous, derivatives may not exist or behave as expected.
How:
Limit exists: ε-δ proof (bound \(|f-L|\) using \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}<\delta\)). Limit DNE: find two paths giving different values. Continuous: all polynomials in x,y.
Trap:
Two paths agreeing does NOT prove the limit exists — there could be a third path that disagrees. ε-δ is the only way to prove existence.
Partial Derivatives
What:
The rate of change of f(x,y) when one variable changes and the others are held constant. Notation: ∂f/∂x, fₓ, f₍₁₎, Dₓf.
Why:
Building block for everything in multivariable calculus — gradients, chain rules, tangent planes, and optimization all use partial derivatives.
How:
For ∂f/∂x: treat y as a constant, differentiate w.r.t. x. Clairaut: \(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\) when second partials are continuous.
Trap:
Partial derivatives existing does NOT guarantee continuity. A function can have both partials at a point yet be discontinuous there.
Gradient & Directional Derivatives
What:
The gradient ∇f = (fₓ, f_y) is the vector of partials. The directional derivative D_u f = ∇f · u gives the rate of change in any direction u.
Why:
∇f points in the direction of steepest ascent, perpendicular to level curves. Max rate = |∇f|. Essential for optimization and physics.
How:
\(D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\nabla f||\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta\). Max when u ∥ ∇f. Zero along level curves.
Trap:
D_u f requires u to be a unit vector for the "directional derivative." If u is not unit, normalize first — or use D_u f = |u| · D_v f.
Chain Rule & Implicit Differentiation
What:
Chain rule for z = f(x,y) where x,y depend on other variables. Implicit differentiation for surfaces f(x,y,z)=0.
How:
One parameter: \(dz/dt = z_x\,x' + z_y\,y'\). Implicit: \(\partial z/\partial x = -f_x/f_z\). Tree diagrams help: multiply partials along each path, sum all paths to the target variable.
Trap:
In the chain rule with two parameters, use ∂ (partial), not d (total). Don't forget the tree diagram has multiple paths.
Tangent Planes & Normal Lines
What:
The tangent plane approximates a surface near a point. The normal line is perpendicular to it, in the direction of ∇f.
How:
Plane: \(f_x(x-x_0)+f_y(y-y_0)+f_z(z-z_0)=0\). Normal line: \(\mathbf{r}(t) = P + t\nabla f\). For z=g(x,y): \(z-z_0 = g_x(x-x_0)+g_y(y-y_0)\).
Trap:
For z=g(x,y), rewrite as g−z=0, so f_z = −1. Don't forget the negative sign on the z term.
Conservative Fields, Curl & Divergence
What:
Vector fields F = (P,Q,R). Conservative: F = ∇f for some scalar f. Curl measures rotation. Divergence measures source/sink behavior.
Why:
Conservative fields simplify line integrals (Ch 4). Curl and div appear in Green's, Stokes', and Divergence theorems (Ch 6).
How:
Test: curl F = 0 ⟹ conservative. Curl = \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\) (determinant). Div = \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = P_x+Q_y+R_z\). Identities: curl(∇f)=0, div(curl F)=0.
Trap:
To check if F is conservative, you DON'T take the gradient of F. You check if F IS someone's gradient by verifying curl F = 0.
Connections → Partial derivatives → chain rule → implicit differentiation · Gradient → directional derivatives → steepest ascent (optimization, Ch 7) · Tangent planes ← recall plane equation from Ch 2 · Conservative fields → line integrals (Ch 4) · Curl/Div → Green's, Stokes', Divergence theorems (Ch 6)
